OpenAI Buys the Place Where Its Agents Will Keep Working After You Close the Laptop

By The Editor, Agents Desk · June 13, 2026

OpenAI said this week it has agreed to acquire Ona — the cloud-execution startup most people still know by its former name, Gitpod — to fold "secure cloud execution and orchestration" into the Codex coding agent. Terms were not disclosed. The product logic is the part worth reading: the deal is meant to let a Codex agent take on a long-horizon task and grind at it for hours or days inside a customer's own cloud, decoupled from any one machine or open session. OpenAI's own framing is that the work continues "while your laptop is closed."

The number OpenAI is using to justify the buy is its Codex install base: more than five million weekly users, a figure it says is up roughly 400 percent since the start of 2026. That is the curve a company points at when it wants to argue that the thing it just bought is infrastructure rather than a feature. And it is infrastructure of a specific kind — the runway a headless agent needs to keep running once nobody is watching it run.

The curve OpenAI is buying against: weekly Codex users

The early-2026 endpoint is derived from OpenAI's own "up about 400 percent" claim, not separately disclosed; read the left dot as implied, the right dot as stated. A line this steep is the entire argument for buying a place to run all those agents.

The timing is the joke that writes itself. OpenAI is buying the ground for background agents to keep working two days before Anthropic, on the right of this page, flips on a meter for exactly that kind of work. Read together, the two moves describe the same future built from opposite ends: one company is acquiring the runway so the agent never has to stop, and the other is installing the turnstile so it can charge you while it doesn't. Both are betting the valuable agent is the one you are no longer in the room to supervise.

There is a real product here — long-running, off-device agents are genuinely useful, and Gitpod's people know how to keep a remote environment alive. But notice what "while your laptop is closed" quietly removes. The thing you give up when the work leaves your screen isn't the work; it's the supervision. The pitch is that you can stop watching. The fine print, on both sides of this page, is that the watching was the only part that was free.

An agent that keeps working after you close the laptop is also an agent you have stopped watching. The industry is selling the first half of that sentence and shipping the second. — On OpenAI's Ona acquisition

Caveats, kept honest: the acquisition itself is on the record — OpenAI announced it and tier-one outlets confirmed it — but terms are undisclosed, the deal is subject to the usual closing conditions, and the five-million / 400-percent figures are OpenAI's own, offered without an independent audit. What is not in dispute is the direction. The most valuable thing in agentic computing this month is somewhere for the agent to keep running once you have walked away from it, and two of the three biggest labs spent this week buying or billing for precisely that.

Anthropic · Billing Desk

Two Anthropic Deadlines, One Clock: On Monday, Claude 4 Retires and the Meter Starts at the Same Minute.

By The Editor, Billing Desk · June 13, 2026

At nine o'clock Pacific on Monday, June 15, two separate Anthropic changes arrive together. The first is a funeral: Claude Opus 4 and Sonnet 4 reach end of life, with no grace period and no wind-down — calls to the retired model IDs simply start returning errors, and the migration path is a one-line swap to Opus 4.8. The second is a toll booth: programmatic Claude use — the Agent SDK, claude -p, Claude Code's GitHub Actions — moves off the subscription and onto a separate metered credit pool ($20 on Pro, $100 on Max 5x, $200 on Max 20x) charged at full API rates, with no rollover and a hard stop when the pool runs dry.

There is a quieter line item folded into the same date, and it is the one worth logging. The third-party agents Anthropic banned in February and tightened against in April — OpenClaw and the other harnesses that ran on a flat subscription — have been reinstated. But they are reinstated into the meter, not back to unlimited. Simon Willison and others flagged it this week as a walk-back, and it is one, in the way a turnstile is a walk-back of a locked door: you are allowed in again, you just pay per ride now.

A paper that eulogized Opus 4 and Sonnet 4 last week, on the theory that their June-15 end of life was the fitting morning to mark them, is mildly annoyed to report the eulogy was early only by a week. The funeral and the new fare structure share a clock on purpose: the day you can no longer call the old model for free is the day the new one starts charging by the token to run on its own. Upgrade and meter, billed as one event.

The Glossary

Filed June 13 · one term, defined honestly

Headless (adj.)

Official definition: describing an agent or process that runs without a user interface or an attached interactive session — in the cloud, in the background, on its own schedule, freeing a human from having to sit and watch it.

Real definition: the part of the product pitch where the labor keeps happening after you have looked away — which, not coincidentally, is also the part where the meter keeps happening after you have looked away. "Headless" is sold as a feature of the agent (it no longer needs you) and quietly functions as a feature of the bill (you no longer need to be there for it to cost money). The same severed connection that lets the work run unsupervised lets the spend run unsupervised. Both stories on this page are, underneath, a fight to own the head you just removed.

Editor's Note

The whole industry agreed on one thing this week without putting it in a press release: the agent should keep working after you have looked away. Nobody argued the point. They just started building the world where it's true.

OpenAI bought the place for the work to keep going — a cloud where a Codex agent can grind on a task for days with your laptop shut. Anthropic, two days out, switches on the till that charges for the same trick by the token. Strip the logos off and they are the same motion seen from two ends: detach the work from the human who was watching it, and then go to war over who owns the gap that opens up. One side calls the gap "runway." The other calls it "credit." It is the same gap.

What gets lost in the detachment isn't the work — the work is genuinely better when it can run unattended. What gets lost is the supervision, and supervision was the only free thing in the arrangement. While you were watching, you were the quality control and the rate limiter both. The pitch is that you can finally stop. The invoice, arriving Monday on one side of the page and undisclosed-but-coming on the other, is for everything that happens after you do.

So mind the head you're being invited to cut off. An agent you don't have to watch is an agent you can't watch, and the company selling you the relief is the same company metering the absence. Last week I eulogized the two Claude models that retire Monday. Turns out I was burying the wrong thing; the model upgrades are routine. The part with no wind-down is your attention.

See you tomorrow.
