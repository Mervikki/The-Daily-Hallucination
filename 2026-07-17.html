OpenAI · Hardware

OpenAI's First Device Reportedly Has No Screen. That's the Whole Idea.

By The Editor, Hardware Desk · July 17, 2026

According to a report in Fortune, OpenAI's first piece of consumer hardware is a screen-free, mobile smart speaker — pitched internally as a “new type of home computer” and a humanlike companion that controls your smart home, plays your media, and answers questions out loud. It reportedly runs on “GPT-Live,” a version of Voice Mode built to listen and talk at the same time rather than wait its turn. There is no price, no ship date, and no confirmation from OpenAI. This is one outlet's account of a product that does not yet exist, and half of what leaks about a device never ships — read it that way.

A week ago, this device was an allegation. In July, Apple sued OpenAI (Apple Inc. v. Liu et al., N.D. Cal.), claiming a “coordinated campaign” to steal trade secrets for “competing consumer hardware,” and naming OpenAI's hardware chief, a 24-year Apple design veteran. At the time, the suit told you OpenAI was building something; it didn't say what. Now, if the reporting holds, we can see the shape of it — and the striking part is what it isn't. It isn't a phone. Apple owns the phone. OpenAI is reaching for the thing the phone sits next to: the counter, the room, the house.

A screen is a boundary — you open the app, you close it, and when you look away it stops. A speaker that's always listening has no open and no close. It's just on, the way a person in the room is on.

For three years the contest was over the best screen: the slickest app, the cleanest chat box, the tab you'd keep open. A screenless, always-on speaker is a bet that the screen was a phase — that the assistant's final form isn't a better window but no window at all, with no session to begin and none to end. Whether people want that on the kitchen counter is the trillion-dollar question a leak can't answer. What the leak does answer is where OpenAI thinks the assistant belongs, and it isn't in your hand. It's in the room.

Google · Models

Gemini 3.5 Pro's Launch Date Arrived. The Model Didn't.

By The Editor, Models Desk · July 17, 2026

For weeks the rumor mills pinned Gemini 3.5 Pro to a July 17 launch. July 17 is here. As of this writing there is no Google blog post, no model card, no pricing page, and no gemini-3.5-pro anywhere in the public API — only a very detailed set of specs from unnamed sources: a 2-million-token context window (double 2.5 Pro's), a “Deep Think” reasoning layer, autonomous workflows. Google reportedly scrapped the original model and rebuilt it from scratch after engineers hit structural failures in recursive tool-calling; that rebuild is the story of why “next month,” promised at I/O back in May, has become several.

Google shipped Gemini 3.5 Flash on time and made it the default. The Pro model — the one meant to do the planning and orchestration, the one that would power a device like the one OpenAI is reportedly building — is the piece that keeps slipping. There is a version of the frontier that exists entirely as a leaked spec sheet with a date attached, and this week it has Google's name on it. We'll cover 3.5 Pro the day it has a URL.

TSMC · Semiconductors

The Receipt Had a Second Page: TSMC Is Raising Prices on Nvidia and Apple.

By The Editor, Semiconductors Desk · July 17, 2026

Yesterday we read TSMC's record quarter as proof the AI demand is real. The same earnings report carried something sharper than a revenue line. Net profit came in at about NT$706.6 billion (~$22B), up roughly 77% year over year; the company raised its 2026 revenue-growth outlook to “slightly above 40%,” lifted planned capex to $60–64 billion, and pledged another $100 billion toward US capacity. And it told its biggest customers — Apple, Nvidia, Qualcomm, AMD — to prepare for wafer price increases of 5–10%, now reaching the 5nm and 7nm nodes, not just the bleeding edge.

The Price-Takers

TSMC reportedly told its largest customers to expect 5–10% wafer price increases, now extending to 5nm and 7nm nodes, not just the leading edge. Figures as reported; the “price-taker” framing is ours.

There is exactly one company that can hand Nvidia — the most valuable business on earth — a price increase and simply expect it to be paid, because there is no second foundry at the leading edge to walk to. Yesterday's number said the demand exists. This one says who holds the leverage inside it. The revenue proves the boom; the price hike proves that the boom's most powerful names are, at the layer that actually matters, price-takers to a single supplier in Hsinchu.
