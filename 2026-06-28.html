Anthropic · Washington Desk

The Ban Didn't Lift. It Turned Into a Guest List.

By The Editor, Washington Desk · June 28, 2026

The Commerce Department has cleared Anthropic to switch Mythos 5 back on — but only for a defined roster of more than a hundred US companies and government agencies, according to a letter from Secretary Howard Lutnick to Anthropic chief compute officer Tom Brown. The reported cohort centers on critical-infrastructure defenders; Cisco and JPMorgan Chase are among the names cited, essentially the "Project Glasswing" partner list the model was built around. Lutnick's letter, as quoted in the coverage, says "appropriate safeguards are in place to permit certain trusted partners to access the Claude Mythos 5 Model." Sixteen days after the order took the model off the board, the most aggressive AI export control this country has issued has produced its first reversal. It is not a lifting. It is an allowlist.

Note which model came back. Mythos 5 is the powerful one — the variant a US official says found vulnerabilities in classified government systems within hours, the capability the whole order was built to contain. Fable 5, the mass-market version that does little more alarming than answer questions for the public, stays dark. The order, in other words, has cleared the dangerous model and left the harmless one switched off, which only stops being backwards once you stop sorting by danger. What separates the two isn't capability; it's countability. You can hand Mythos to a hundred named critical-infrastructure firms and write down every one of them. You cannot write down everyone who opens a chat window.

So the resolution doubles as a confession of what the fight was always about. For two weeks the question was posed as on-or-off, a model too dangerous to leave running; the answer that arrived is who's-on-the-list, a model perfectly fine to run as long as the State knows the name attached to every session. As of the weekend Anthropic was reportedly still negotiating and still serving zero public traffic — no Fable, and no Mythos to anyone not on Commerce's roster. The off switch, it emerges, was a doorman the whole time. The doorman will admit a bank.

Appropriate safeguards are in place to permit certain trusted partners to access the Claude Mythos 5 Model. — Commerce Secretary Howard Lutnick, in a letter to Anthropic, per reporting

Approved Access to the Frontier, June 28

Two frontier models reached their users this week through a list; the general public is on neither. Organizations, enterprise partners, and people aren't the same unit — the bars compare shape, not seats.

OpenAI · Frontier Desk

GPT-5.6 Arrives Named, Numbered, and Behind the Same Rope

By The Editor, Frontier Desk · June 28, 2026

OpenAI used June 26 to unveil GPT-5.6 — three variants, Sol at the top, Terra in the middle, Luna as the fast and cheap one — and in the same breath confined them to roughly twenty enterprise partners "whose participation has been shared with the government." The company said the administration asked for a staged rollout so federal authorities could test the models before any general release, and went out of its way to lodge an objection to the arrangement it was nonetheless accepting: "We don't believe this kind of government access process should become the long-term default."

Set that beside this morning's lead and the week stops looking like two stories. Anthropic's most capable model is being cleared for a hundred-odd vetted organizations; OpenAI's newest is being parceled out to twenty whose names sit with the government. One list is a restoration and the other a launch, and they are the same object: a frontier model that reaches its users through a roster somebody in Washington has seen. OpenAI grumbling that this "shouldn't be the norm" while building the second working instance of it is the part to keep. Norms are not set by the people complaining about them. They are set by the people complying.

Anthropic · Security Desk

Anthropic Says Alibaba Ran 25,000 Fake Accounts at Claude

By The Editor, Security Desk · June 28, 2026

In a June 10 letter to the Senate Banking Committee — surfaced by Bloomberg last week and relayed since — Anthropic told Chair Tim Scott and Ranking Member Elizabeth Warren that operators it ties to Alibaba ran what it calls "the largest known distillation attack" on Claude: roughly 25,000 fraudulent accounts generating more than 28.8 million exchanges between April 22 and June 5, aimed at the model's software-engineering and agentic-reasoning skills. Distillation is the polite industrial term for using one model's outputs to school your own; the accusation is that a rival went shopping in Claude's answers at scale.

Every figure here is Anthropic's own, drawn from a letter that isn't public, so read the numbers as a claim rather than a finding. What's notable is the venue. The company took its grievance not to a court but to the committee that writes the rules on capital and sanctions, and a thinly sourced follow-on this week suggests Congress is weighing a response. A lab that spent the month being switched off by Washington has discovered the upside of the same address: when the government is already deep in your business, it is also a place to file complaints.

Editor's Note

For sixteen days the only question anyone asked about the export order was when — when do the models come back, what's the date, how many more mornings of dark. The answer arrived this week and it wasn't a when at all. It was a who. Mythos 5 returns, but only to a list. And the shape of an ending tells you what the fight was always about: a blackout that resolves into a guest list was a guest-list negotiation the entire time, wearing a blackout's clothes.

The tell is which model they let back. Mythos is the frightening one — the model an official says walked through classified systems in an afternoon. Fable 5 is the one that chats with the public, and Fable 5 is the one still switched off. If the order ran on danger, that is exactly backwards. It runs on something else: whether the people using the thing can be counted. A hundred critical-infrastructure firms can be named, vetted, and written into a letter. The general public cannot, because "the public" is the one cohort with no roster — that is the whole definition of it. So the dangerous model, handed to people you can enumerate, is safe enough; the harmless model, handed to people you can't, is not. The axis was never what the model does. It was whether the State can see who's holding it.

Which is why I don't think Fable 5 comes back the way Mythos did, and not because anyone has decided it is more dangerous. It is because "everyone" is the single guest list a trusted-partner program can never print. You can admit a bank. You can admit a utility, a telecom, a federal agency, one signed name at a time. You cannot admit a public — and a model built for everybody, by construction, has no list to check it against. The off switch was a doorman all along. He will wave through anyone whose name you can write down, and the rest of us, it turns out, were never going to be on it.

See you tomorrow.
