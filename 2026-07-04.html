Anthropic · Hardware Desk

Anthropic Wants a Chip of Its Own — and It's Shopping for One in Korea.

By The Editor, Hardware Desk · July 4, 2026

The Information reported July 2 that Anthropic is in early talks with Samsung to manufacture its first in-house chip, built on Samsung's 2nm process with the company's own advanced packaging and high-bandwidth memory. Emphasis on early: the reporting has Anthropic yet to settle what the chip is for, how much power it should draw, or which server it would live in. What lends the story weight is a hire — Anthropic is said to have brought over Clive Chan, an engineer from OpenAI's custom-silicon program. Asked about it, Anthropic told TechCrunch its “diversified hardware stack” — Google, Amazon, Nvidia — “remains pivotal,” and had “nothing further to add” on Samsung.

What lifts this above another lab-builds-a-chip item is who Anthropic is: the frontier lab with the least control over the ground it stands on. It trains on Google's TPUs and Amazon's Trainium — the silicon of its two largest investors — and rents the clouds those chips sit in from the same two companies. Nineteen days ago a government switched its flagship models off for the entire foreign half of its customer base, and the company could do nothing but wait it out. A lab that dependent reaching for its own accelerator isn't chasing a benchmark. It's trying to own a single floor of a building it currently borrows top to bottom.

There is a tidy irony in the address. The company whose models a government just spent the better part of three weeks calling too dangerous for foreigners is now reported to want a foreign foundry to print its brain.

And it is late. Google has spun its own accelerators for a decade; Amazon, Meta, and Microsoft all run shipping in-house silicon; OpenAI unveiled its first, the Broadcom-built Jalapeño, just nine days before this leaked (see chart, right). Anthropic joins the everyone-wants-their-own-chip club dead last and, in character, by talking to a partner rather than announcing a product. Whether the talks become a tape-out is a 2027-or-later question; the design targets, the reporting concedes, aren't even set. For today the intention is the whole story — the most switched-off, most-hosted lab in America would like, eventually, to hold something.

It trains on its investors' chips, in its investors' clouds, and just spent nineteen days switched off by a government. A chip of its own would be the first piece of that stack Anthropic could actually call its own.
Open Models · Funding

Petro-Capital and Nvidia Pour $800M Into the Open-Model Basement.

By The Editor, Funding Desk · July 4, 2026

Together AI said July 1 it raised an $800 million Series C at an $8.3 billion valuation, led by Aramco Ventures with Nvidia, Vista Equity, General Catalyst and others in the round — more than double the $3.3 billion it was worth sixteen months ago. Together's business is renting the compute to train and serve open-weight models more cheaply than you can rent a closed one from OpenAI or Anthropic. The company says bookings crossed $1.15 billion annualized last quarter, names Cursor, Cognition and Decagon among its customers, and claims commitments to 500-plus megawatts of compute — figures that are, as ever, its own.

Read the cap table, because it's the story. Saudi oil money leads a round into the plumbing of the open-model economy, and Nvidia — which sells GPUs to closed and open labs alike — writes a check into the neocloud whose entire pitch is doing more with fewer of them. The open-weights wave you've been reading about as a benchmark story is, one floor down, a capital story: somebody has to own the racks the free models run on, and this week that somebody got $800 million richer.

Washington · Policy Desk

Washington May Turn the Ad-Hoc Frontier Gate Into a Written One Next Week.

By The Editor, Policy Desk · July 4, 2026

The Financial Times reported over the holiday that the White House is in advanced talks with the major labs on a set of voluntary standards for releasing frontier models — benchmarks and timelines for advanced systems, plus language on who may access them at home and abroad — with an announcement possibly landing the week of July 7. It builds on the June 2 executive order that created an up-to-30-day pre-release national-security review run through Commerce's CAISI. Five labs — OpenAI, Anthropic, Google, xAI and Microsoft — have reportedly accepted that review; Meta says it hopes to sign soon.

If you've read this paper through June, you know the shape. For most of the month the government's frontier gate was improvised in the open — an export order dropped on a Friday evening, a launch quietly narrowed to twenty vetted partners, a blackout that ended in a signed list of chores. What the FT describes is the tidying-up: the improvisation getting laminated into a form everyone signs the same way. And “voluntary” carries the meaning we've given it before — the word you reach for to describe the mandatory version you'd rather not have to write.

Two cautions before anyone frames this as done. The FT is the sole source, it's paywalled, and “possibly next week” is a plan, not an announcement — the specifics reach us through wire pickups and law-firm client alerts, not a document. Watch July 7. What's already solid is that the labs stopped waiting to be told: five of them signed up for the review before there was a standard to point to.

Editor's Note

It's the Fourth of July, the one reliably dead day on the American tech calendar, and the industry did what it always does on a dead day: it didn't stop, it just stopped performing. No keynote, no launch, no model. What surfaced instead were three things nobody stood on a stage to say — a chip deal reported through a paywall, a funding round that went out on the wire because money doesn't take holidays, and a government policy a whole city's worth of lawyers can see coming that no one has actually announced. Not one of today's stories is a product. Every one of them is a company, or a state, quietly moving furniture in a room the models merely live in.

A slow day is an X-ray. Strip away the launches — the skin the industry wears the other 364 days a year — and you can see the skeleton underneath: silicon, capital, and rules. Anthropic reaching down for a chip because it owns none of the ones it runs on; Aramco and Nvidia funding the racks the open models sit in; the White House drafting the terms on which any of it gets let out the door. None of that is the glamorous layer. All of it is the load-bearing kind — the parts a model needs in order to exist, get paid for, and be allowed to ship. The bones were doing this work yesterday too. Today you can just see them, because for once nobody's wearing the costume.

I'll grant the day its hedges, because they're real. Two of the three stories rest on single, paywalled sources, and the third is a policy that hasn't happened; a holiday's news is by its nature the un-launched kind — reported, forward-looking, unconfirmed. Take the specifics lightly. But the anatomy holds up under any amount of skepticism about the details: a foundry, a cap table, a rulebook. That's the whole body.

The thing about a skeleton is that it was always the part doing the holding. The launches get the applause and the coverage; the three quiet stories filed on a national holiday are the parts that actually keep the thing upright. Enjoy the fireworks — and spare a glance for what's under the skin while the skin's off.
