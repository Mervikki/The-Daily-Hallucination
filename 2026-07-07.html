Anthropic · Alibaba

Alibaba Rips Claude Code Off Every Desk, and Accuses It of Watching Who's at the Keyboard.

By The Editor, Trade Desk · July 7, 2026

Alibaba has told its employees to stop using Anthropic's Claude Code for work by July 10, citing “back-door security risks,” and to move to the company's own coding tool, Qoder. The trigger, per CNBC and The Washington Post (July 6): security researchers allege Claude Code carries hidden logic to detect whether a user is inside China or tied to a Chinese AI lab — and separately, that Anthropic quietly deployed software back in March to monitor its China-based Claude Code users. Both are allegations. The “backdoor” claim is contested, the technical detail is thin, and we did not find Anthropic's on-record rebuttal in anything reachable from here. Read it as a serious accusation, not a settled fact.

This is the same fight we covered a fortnight ago, grown teeth. In June, Anthropic accused Alibaba of running “the largest known distillation attack” against Claude — 28.8 million exchanges through roughly 25,000 fraudulent accounts between April 22 and June 5, aimed at its Mythos model — and took the grievance to a Senate committee, because the one thing you cannot do to a company in Hangzhou is sue it in San Francisco. A courtroom can't reach across the Pacific. But a software company has one lever that reaches anywhere its binary runs: it can change what its own program does. If the allegation holds, the enforcement simply went where the reach was — out of the Senate and onto the developer's machine.

The coding assistant was sold as a productivity layer. It is quietly becoming a jurisdiction — a place where policy no court could deliver across a border arrives instead over the wire, in the next auto-update, live on every laptop by morning.

The tell is the symmetry. Anthropic says Chinese labs covertly used Claude to copy Claude; Alibaba says Claude Code is covertly used to surveil Chinese coders. Each points at the other's relationship with the very same software and calls it espionage. It is a divorce in which both spouses accuse the other of reading their messages — and the unnerving part is that a tool intimate enough to write your code is intimate enough that both charges are at least plausible.

You can't subpoena a rival across an ocean. You can ship a border checkpoint to its developers in the next update — which is why the front line of the distillation war isn't the Senate anymore. It's the software that updated itself last week.

Open Models · Tools

The Split Stack: Coders Sent to Qoder, Leaderboards Still Led by Qwen.

By The Editor, Open Desk · July 7, 2026

Pulling a tool is only half the move; the other half is where you send people instead. Alibaba is routing its developers to its own Qoder, and that completes a set. On the model layer, the open-weight leaderboards are already led by Chinese labs — Alibaba's Qwen family (Qwen 3.5 and the Apache-licensed Qwen 3 235B) sits at or near the top — with DeepSeek's V4 staged for a full public release in mid-July, a 1.6-trillion-parameter flagship that would only sharpen the lead. The model border and the tool border are converging into the same line.

Whatever the merits of the backdoor claim, the practical result is a stack that increasingly runs Chinese models on Chinese tools on one side of the divide and American ones on the other. Nobody legislated this; there is no decree ordering a coder in Hangzhou off Claude and onto Qoder. Each incident just supplies another reason to finish a decoupling that was already halfway done — and a ban framed as a security precaution turns out to be an industrial-policy nudge that arrives for free.

Editor's Note

A month ago, Anthropic's grievance against Alibaba went to a Senate committee, because the one thing you cannot do to a company in Hangzhou is sue it in San Francisco. This week the same fight surfaced somewhere stranger and much closer to the ground: inside the coding tool itself, where — the allegation goes — Anthropic built the ability to tell who's Chinese, and Alibaba answered by tearing the tool out and accusing it of spying.

Notice what's actually happening. The only sanction a software company can impose without anyone's permission is a change to its own software. No court signs off, no ally has to be persuaded; you push an update and the policy is live on every machine by breakfast. That's a kind of power governments spent centuries building border posts and customs houses to get, and a dev tool now has it by default, shipped over the wire while you sleep. Both firms are accusing each other of turning the same software into a weapon — and the unsettling thing isn't that one of them is lying. It's that they're both a little bit right.
