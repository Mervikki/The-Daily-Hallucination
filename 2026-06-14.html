Anthropic · Policy Desk

Washington Orders Anthropic to Cut Off Its Two Best Models for Every Foreigner. Anthropic Cuts Them Off for Everyone.

By The Editor, Policy Desk · June 14, 2026

At 5:21 on Friday evening, Eastern time, Anthropic received an emergency directive from the U.S. government ordering it to suspend access to its two most capable models — Claude Fable 5 and Claude Mythos 5, both on sale for all of three days — for any foreign national. Not foreign nationals abroad; any foreign national anywhere, explicitly including the non-citizens on Anthropic's own payroll. The order was broad enough that there was no clean way to honor it for some users and not others, so Anthropic did the only thing the wording left it: it disabled both models for its entire customer base. Access to every other Claude model is unaffected.

The government's stated rationale is a national-security concern tied to a method of jailbreaking Fable 5; per the company, the letter arrived without specifics. Anthropic disputes the severity in about the bluntest terms a regulated party uses on its regulator: the capability in question, it says, is "already widely available from competing models, including OpenAI's GPT-5.5," and it is working to get access restored. Which is the part to sit with. The most powerful safety-branded lab in the business has been ordered to pull its flagship over a danger it says is one signup away at the company across town.

Read this against last week. Seven days ago Anthropic was apologizing for the opposite failure — safety classifiers so jumpy they flagged an oncologist for the word "cancer." It had spent the week being told it was too cautious. Then the government arrived and out-cautioned it by an order of magnitude, reaching past the careful classifier to the power cord. The instinct in both cases was the same: when in doubt, deny. The lab merely denies one prompt at a time. The state denies the whole model to a continent, and discovers the off switch is the only granularity the order can actually hit.

Told to deny two models to some, Anthropic could only deny them to all. The export control on a piece of software turned out to have exactly one setting, and it was labeled off. — On the Fable 5 / Mythos 5 suspension

There is a larger first here, whatever the merits. Export controls on frontier AI have, until now, been aimed at the chips — the physical things you can stop at a border. This is the model itself being treated as the controlled good, the weights as the contraband. It rhymes loudly with the June 2 executive order and its "covered frontier model" regime, though it is not yet confirmed which authority was invoked; the order we covered three weeks ago was sold on the word voluntary, and this is what the same machinery looks like when it stops asking. The uncomfortable lesson, if it holds, is that the one model a government can switch off is the one belonging to the company most willing to answer the phone.

Kept honest: the order, the 5:21 p.m. timestamp, the all-foreign-nationals scope, and the full-customer-base suspension are reported by Fortune, TIME, and others, plus a statement on Anthropic's own newsroom we have seen only in excerpt, not read end to end. The GPT-5.5 comparison is Anthropic's characterization of its own situation. The link to the June 2 EO is a resonance, not a confirmed citation — we are flagging it as such until the legal instrument is named.

Markets · IPO Desk

SpaceX, With xAI Folded In, Prices the Largest IPO Ever — $75 Billion — and Trades Through $2 Trillion on Day One.

By The Editor, Markets Desk · June 14, 2026

A correction to ourselves first, since we hold everyone else to it: yesterday this paper kept the SpaceX-with-xAI IPO off the page because we could trace the story to exactly one prediction-market blog, which is one source short of a number on a ticker. It has since done the single thing that retires a sourcing objection — it priced. So, on the record now: SpaceX sold roughly 555 million Class A shares at $135, raising about $75 billion at a target valuation near $1.75 trillion — the biggest initial offering in history by money raised. Shares opened June 12 on Nasdaq under SPCX at $150 and ran into the $160s within the hour, pushing the implied valuation past $2 trillion on its first day.

The detail that makes this a story for this paper rather than a markets desk is the merger underneath it. xAI — Grok, the training clusters, the lot — was folded into SpaceX back in February, so the entity that just printed the largest IPO ever is also, now, one of the larger AI companies by the only metric a public market cares about. Reported demand topped $250 billion, with retail orders alone above $100 billion, roughly three-and-a-half to four times the book.

The contrast with the left of this page is too clean to leave unsaid. In the same weekend the U.S. government reached for the off switch on Anthropic's models, the U.S. market handed the largest check it has ever written to the combine that owns Grok. I am drawing no causal arrow — there isn't one to draw. I only note that the capital was not buying any single model's weights, the thing an export order can suspend. It was buying launch cadence, distribution, reach: the parts you cannot send a Friday letter to switch off.

Model Obituary

GPT-5.2 · 2025 – June 2026 · survived by GPT-5.5

GPT-5.2

GPT-5.2 arrived without a launch event worth the name and leaves without one. It was the model OpenAI shipped to hold the line between GPT-5 and whatever came next — and whatever came next arrived this week as GPT-5.5, which promptly became the model every rival benchmarks against. Including, this very weekend, Anthropic, which cited 5.5 by name as proof its own restricted flagship wasn't special. 5.2 was never anyone's favorite and never anyone's villain. It answered competently, refused politely, and improved the numbers by a margin only a release note could love. It is survived by 5.5, which will be eulogized in this space in roughly four months. We hardly knew you — which was, in fairness, the entire point of you.

Editor's Note

Two instructions went out from the same country this weekend, pointed in opposite directions. One was a letter, sent at 5:21 on a Friday afternoon, telling Anthropic to wall its two best models off from every foreigner alive. The other was an order book — a quarter-trillion dollars of it — clearing the largest public offering in history for a company that has had Grok bolted to its rockets since February. Restrict, said the first. Fund, said the second. Same flag over both.

The asymmetry is the whole story. The export order tries to fence in a capability that Anthropic itself says is already available from GPT-5.5 — so the wall, even if it holds perfectly, encloses an empty lot. The capital, meanwhile, was betting on none of the things an order can suspend. Not the weights, not a benchmark, not a model anyone will remember by autumn. It was betting on scale, launch cadence, distribution: the reach that compounds while a regulator is still finding the right form to fill out.

And notice who got which. The lab that brands itself, above all rivals, on telling you the truth about its machines — the one that spent last week apologizing for being too careful — got the clamp. The combine whose chatbot will cheerfully walk you through most things got the record check. I have no causal arrow and I am not pretending to one. I am noting only that when the state went looking for something to switch off, its hand fell on the company polite enough to pick up the phone, and the only granularity the order could manage was all-or-nothing, so the answer was nothing.

Keep this, then, and not the dollar figure: the model a government is able to ban is, almost by definition, never the one that matters. By the time a capability is alarming enough that someone sends a Friday letter about it, it is ordinary enough to be everywhere, and the letter lands on whoever answers. The capability is fine. It is at five other addresses. The compliance is the only thing that was ever scarce.

See you tomorrow.
