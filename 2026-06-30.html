Anthropic · Policy Desk

Anthropic Couldn't Sue Hangzhou. So the Complaint Became a Bill.

By The Editor, Policy Desk · June 30, 2026

On June 10, Anthropic sent the Senate Banking Committee a letter accusing operators affiliated with Alibaba's Qwen lab of running what it called the largest known distillation attack on Claude: roughly 25,000 fraudulent accounts generating more than 28.8 million exchanges between April 22 and June 5, all aimed at extracting Claude's software-engineering and agentic-reasoning skills. Distillation is the unglamorous craft of training a weaker model on a stronger one's answers — you don't steal the weights, you just ask 28.8 million questions and learn from the replies. The accusation broke publicly through CNBC on June 24. What is new this week is the remedy taking shape around it: Senators Bill Hagerty and Andy Kim are moving to attach an amendment to the must-pass National Defense Authorization Act that would blacklist or sanction any entity found running such a campaign, with a companion House bill reportedly in the wings.

Notice what is not happening. Anthropic is not suing. It is hard to serve a subpoena on an operation that may sit in Hangzhou, harder to prove in a courtroom that a given model learned from your outputs rather than from the open web's, and hardest of all to collect a judgment if you win. So the grievance skipped the courthouse and walked into the committee that writes sanctions. In the translation, "someone broke our terms of service with fake accounts" becomes "a foreign adversary extracted a strategic national capability" — the same act, lifted from a contract dispute into a matter of statecraft, because national-security law is the one instrument long enough to reach across the Pacific.

The irony keeps a calendar. Eighteen days ago a national-security order reached into Anthropic's own product and switched two of its models off; this week the same family of machinery is being loaded on Anthropic's behalf, pointed the other direction. The export-control apparatus has no loyalties — it will dark your flagship on a Friday and blacklist your rival on a Tuesday. To be fair to the complaint: 25,000 fraudulent accounts is not idle web-scraping, and if the figures hold, it is a genuine abuse of access rather than ordinary competition. But they are Anthropic's own figures, drawn from a letter that is not public, describing a technique notoriously hard to prove from the outside — and Alibaba denies it. What is verifiable is the venue: a fraud claim walking into the room where trade wars are drafted.

You can't serve a subpoena on Hangzhou. You can add it to a sanctions list — and that is the door this complaint walked through.

Fable 5 Was "Days" From Coming Back. It's Day Eighteen.

By The Editor, Washington Desk · June 30, 2026

A housekeeping note on the saga we have been demoting for a reason. Yesterday this paper relayed reporting that Fable 5 — the consumer Claude dark since June 12 — was "on track to return soon," possibly "as soon as this week." It is now Tuesday, day eighteen, and Fable 5 is still off for everyone: consumers, the API, Claude Code, international subscribers. The Pentagon and the NSA, the two sign-offs reportedly standing between the model and the switch, have not signed; other agencies are said to have cleared it already. The only motion remains last week's — Commerce's June 26 letter let the higher-end Mythos 5 back on for roughly a hundred organizations vetted to defend critical infrastructure. Fable was not on that list, and it is not on any.

The "coming days" doing the work here is the same line Anthropic's international managing director offered from a Seoul stage nearly two weeks ago. We have learned to time the industry's units of optimism: "coming weeks" became "coming days" became, so far, "still." We will tell you when a switch is actually flipped. To date we have only datelines.

Model Watch

GPT-5.6 Gets a Price List, Gemini Slips to July, and the Open Field Just Ships

By The Editor, Model Watch · June 30, 2026

Three frontier updates, three different fates. OpenAI's GPT-5.6 — unveiled June 26 as a three-tier family — now has a price list: Sol, the flagship, at $5 in / $30 out per million tokens; Terra, pitched at GPT-5.5 quality for half the cost, at $2.50 / $15; Luna, the cheap one, at $1 / $6. General availability is "in the coming weeks"; for now it is API-and-Codex-only, to the roughly twenty government-vetted partners we covered last week. Google's Gemini 3.5 Pro, promised for June at I/O, has reportedly slipped to July and stays in enterprise preview, trailed by a subplot — several senior researchers said to have left for rivals — that we would want a second source on before we believed it. And the open-weight field posted the week's one number you can act on this morning: Z.ai's GLM-5.2 tops the Artificial Analysis intelligence index at 51, ahead of Nvidia's Nemotron 3 Ultra (48) and a pack of Chinese models near 44, at roughly $0.45 / $3.31 per million tokens.

Sort them by the only axis that matters this week — who decides whether you get to use it. GPT-5.6 routes through a government roster; Gemini 3.5 Pro routes through an enterprise contract and a calendar that keeps slipping; GLM-5.2 routes through a download button. Two of the three most-hyped releases of the month sit behind somebody's permission, and the model with no marketing department is the one you can actually run. The leaderboard nobody curates is winning the only contest that ships.

Editor's Note

Hold two of this week's facts next to each other. Eighteen days ago, a national-security order reached into Anthropic's product and switched two of its models off — involuntary, sweeping, aimed squarely at the company. This week, a different national-security instrument is being machined on Anthropic's behalf: a sanctions amendment, bolted to the defense bill, aimed at the rival it says copied Claude. Same toolbox. Opposite end of the barrel. The apparatus that made Anthropic a victim a fortnight ago is the apparatus it is now, however indirectly, reaching for.

That reach tells you something the press release won't. You don't take a terms-of-service breach to the Senate Banking Committee because you expect a refund; you take it there because the courthouse can't help you. The defendant is offshore, the offense — a model learned from another model's answers — is maddening to prove, and a contract claim doesn't cross oceans. Sanctions do. So the grievance gets upsized: a fraud complaint is re-described as a strategic theft, because the bigger frame is the only one with an enforcement mechanism that reaches Hangzhou. I'll grant the complaint its due — 25,000 fake accounts is real abuse, not scraping — while noting the numbers are Anthropic's own, the letter isn't public, and Alibaba says none of it happened.

Here is the part I keep turning over. The export-control machine doesn't have a side. It switched off the company that's now feeding it, and it will switch on whatever name gets pointed at next. For two years the labs competed on benchmarks; lately they compete on which of them can get the other one written into a statute. The tell of this era isn't who plays the villain — it's that the line outside the armory keeps getting longer, and everyone in it is holding a letter.

See you tomorrow.
