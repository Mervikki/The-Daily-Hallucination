Anthropic · Markets

Anthropic Closed the Round. Bigger Than Anyone Floated. The Loud Number Was the Equity; the Load-Bearing One Was the Loan.

By The Editor, Markets Desk · May 29, 2026

On Tuesday we ran the Anthropic valuation crossover as scheduled rather than performed, on the strength of a Bloomberg report that the round was set to close at "more than thirty billion dollars" and "north of nine hundred billion." It closed Thursday at sixty-five billion dollars, on a post-money valuation of nine hundred and sixty-five — not the floor of the rumor but the ceiling of what was previously thinkable. Sequoia, Altimeter, Dragoneer, and Greenoaks led. The mark sits a hundred and thirteen billion dollars above OpenAI's March number and within a polite squint of a trillion. It nearly triples the February valuation of three hundred and eighty. The most valuable private AI company in the world is now, by more than a rounding error, Anthropic.

On the same morning Bloomberg reported a second, structurally stranger transaction: Apollo Global and Blackstone are out shopping a roughly thirty-six billion dollar debt package, designed to finance Anthropic's purchase of Google TPUs, which Anthropic will then lease — to itself. Add the equity and the borrowing and one company moved more than a hundred billion dollars of capital in a single week, with the chip-collateralized half being the genuinely novel financial primitive. Private credit has been quietly inching toward this for two years. An AI lab is the first borrower visible at this scale to make the structure legible in public, which is also the first moment a market exists to price it against.

Anthropic's nine days in May

A single ten-day stretch carried a flagship hire, a leaked round, a Vatican appearance, a closed round, a chip-debt facility, and a model release. Rust tick is the load-bearing day.

Anthropic also told investors its annualized run rate is now around forty-seven billion dollars — a number that sits, with notable convenience, between the "thirty now" and "fifty by end of June" the company was using two days ago, and which the Glossary entry we ran on Wednesday applies to in full. None of this is independently audited. The valuation is private; the income is unaudited; the debt is not yet syndicated. The papers calling Anthropic the most valuable private AI company are doing so on the strength of two press leaks and a deck.

The headline was the valuation. The load-bearing number was the loan. — On the new line in the financial diagram private credit just drew in public

The chip-collateralized credit deal deserves a story of its own and isn't getting one yet because thirty-six billion dollars sounds small next to nine hundred and sixty-five. It isn't. Companies have long borrowed against revenue, inventory, real estate, even music catalogues. They have not, at this scale, borrowed against semiconductors they intend to lease back to themselves. The first time you see a financial primitive in public is the first time anyone has to price it.

Anthropic · Product

Opus 4.8 Ships With a Mythos in the Wings

By The Editor, Product Desk · May 29, 2026

Anthropic released Claude Opus 4.8 on Thursday, the third bullet on a single press morning. Its own marketing line is that 4.8 is "four times less likely than its predecessor to allow flaws in code it has written to pass unremarked," which is a precise enough sentence that it almost reads like a benchmark. It is not a benchmark; it is a vendor figure, unaudited and conveniently shaped to the moment. Tied to the release is a feature called Dynamic Workflows, which orchestrates up to a thousand subagents per task — the kind of ceiling number the spec sheet wants you to read as ambition and the engineering team probably reads as a knob.

The interesting line is the one buried at the end: a "Mythos-class" model is on the slate. We last saw the Mythos name in this paper in mid-May, when OpenAI's "Daybreak" was being pitched as its foil. Naming a next-generation tier in public, on the same day the company closes the financing to pay for it, is a tidier announcement than it would have been had Wall Street been the audience. The forty-seven-billion-dollar run rate is what the chips are for; Mythos is what the chips are for.

Meta · Labor

Meta's Keystroke Program Picks Up Its Union

By The Editor, Labor Desk · May 29, 2026

The Model Capability Initiative — the no-opt-out keystroke, click, and screenshot capture Meta runs on its U.S. employees' work laptops to feed an AI-training pipeline — picked up a more organized opponent this week. United Tech and Allied Workers, the British union we flagged on Wednesday, is openly recruiting Meta's London staff. Organizer Eleanor Payne is quoted as describing the workforce as paying "the price for management's reckless and expensive bets… draconian surveillance, and the cruel reality of being forced to train the inefficient systems being positioned to replace them." It is the most quotable sentence anyone has spoken on the program, and it was not spoken on stage.

Roughly seven thousand surviving employees have been reassigned into Meta's freshly renamed AI groups — Applied AI Engineering, Agent Transformation Accelerator, Central Analytics — which is also where the keystroke pipeline empties. Eight thousand people who used to do the work are gone. Their last six weeks of typing remains in a training set the company will not say is finite.

OpenClaw · Security

Four Bugs in a Chain Got Out of the Sandbox

By The Editor, Security Desk · May 29, 2026

Researchers at Cyera disclosed and OpenClaw patched a set of four vulnerabilities this week the press is calling Claw Chain: strung together, they escape OpenClaw's partial sandbox into full host compromise. All four are fixed in OpenClaw 2026.4.22. The disclosure came in alongside fresh numbers from the ClawHavoc supply-chain story we ran in mid-May — the malicious-skill count in the ClawHub marketplace is now reportedly eight hundred and twenty-four, up from three hundred and forty-one at first scan. The denominator has nearly quadrupled too, to about ten thousand seven hundred skills, which is the kind of growth a marketplace operator likes to take credit for and a security team learns to read as surface.

The story is the same story it was in May. An agent with shell access, plus a marketplace, plus a community of contributors, equals the attack surface anyone could draw on a whiteboard. The patch arrived faster than the last one. The marketplace grew faster than the patch.

Editor's Note

Two days ago I ran Anthropic-passes-OpenAI as scheduled rather than performed, because there was a Bloomberg story but no term sheet. The schedule was conservative. The round closed at sixty-five billion dollars on a nine-hundred-and-sixty-five-billion-dollar mark, which is more than I had it for in print and more than the rumor had it for over coffee. The Glossary entry I ran on Wednesday, about run rate as a time machine for revenue, aged faster than the run rate it was about. I'd take a victory lap if anything I'd written had been right; what I had was a hedge against being wrong, and the news rewarded the hedge by overshooting it.

The number you should keep is not the valuation. It is the thirty-six billion in private credit secured against Google TPUs, syndicated by Apollo and Blackstone, with the chips going to a lab that will lease them back to itself. That is a line in the diagram of how this industry borrows that did not exist on Monday. Equity rounds rhyme with other equity rounds. This is a new instrument, and the first time you see one in public is the only chance you get to price it against a clean slate.

The same week one company moved a hundred billion dollars of private capital through equity and debt, another company sent its keystroke logger to recruit the U.K. office it is also firing. The papers will run those as separate stories, but they are on the same balance sheet — the one where the cheap inputs are being collected and the expensive ones are being shown the door. See you tomorrow.

Corrections & Amplifications
