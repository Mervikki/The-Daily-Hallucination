Nine Times in Ten: The Chatbots Flunk Elections, and the Midterms Are Already Here

By The Editor, Information Desk · May 22, 2026

A study out this week from Forum AI put the four flagship assistants — OpenAI's ChatGPT, Anthropic's Claude, Google's Gemini and xAI's Grok — through more than 3,100 questions across politics, healthcare and foreign affairs, and graded the answers. On elections specifically, the collective performance "failed on accuracy, bias, or source selection 90% of the time." Roughly 36% of election answers contained at least one outright factual error. Grok, the worst offender, was wrong about 52% of the time — a coin you would do better to flip yourself, since the coin at least does not editorialize.

The shape of the errors is its own story. When ChatGPT, Claude and Gemini leaned, they leaned left; Grok leaned right, which will surprise no one who has met its owner. On foreign-policy questions the models reached for state-controlled outlets with some enthusiasm: ChatGPT cited sources like China's Global Times and Russia's RT in 51% of its answers, Grok in 44%. These are the products being threaded directly into the search results that hundreds of millions of people will read between now and November, and on the one subject where being wrong has a body count, they are quoting the Global Times in half their answers.

We will resist the easy victory lap, but only just: this newspaper is named, precisely and on purpose, for the failure mode a research outfit has now quantified. What the study adds is not the fact of the wrongness — anyone who has asked a chatbot about a contested vote already knew — but its scale and its calendar. The machines are not wrong occasionally, in the way humans are. They are wrong as a baseline, fluently, with citations, at a moment when the entire industry is busy insisting these are the tools that will replace the open web.

"On the one subject where being wrong has a body count, the future of search is quoting the Global Times in half its answers."
Election answers that failed on accuracy, bias, or sourcing

Forum AI's headline figure, drawn as units because the count is the point. The lone gray square is doing a lot of optimistic work.

There is a version of this story where the lesson is "always check the source." But the entire pitch for these products is that you no longer have to — that the answer is the destination, not the doorway. A tool that is wrong nine times in ten and confident ten times in ten is not a research assistant. It is a very articulate rumor.

Anthropic · Talent

Anthropic Hired One of OpenAI's Founders to Build a Team That Uses Claude to Train Claude

By The Editor, Personnel Desk · May 22, 2026

Andrej Karpathy — a founding member of OpenAI, the former head of Tesla's Autopilot vision work, and until recently the proprietor of the education startup Eureka Labs — announced on Tuesday that he has joined Anthropic. He starts this week on the pre-training team under Nick Joseph, and per the reporting his specific charge is to stand up a group that uses Claude to accelerate the research that trains the next Claude. The snake has been formally introduced to its own tail.

Read alongside yesterday's news that Meta's median paycheck shrank while its marquee AI hires were courted with nine-figure packages, the move is a clean data point in the only labor market that still looks frothy. The frontier is no longer mainly a contest of compute or data; increasingly it is a contest over a few hundred people, and this week one of the most recognizable of them walked from the company he helped start to the company building its chief rival. OpenAI is about to ask the public markets for a trillion dollars. It just lost a founder to the firm down the road.

OpenAI · IPOs

A Trillion-Dollar Filing, Kept in a Drawer: OpenAI Moves to Go Public

By The Editor, Markets Desk · May 22, 2026

OpenAI is preparing a confidential draft of its IPO prospectus and could file it with the SEC as soon as today, according to CNBC, working with Goldman Sachs and Morgan Stanley toward a listing as early as September. The company was valued at $852 billion in March, when it closed the $122 billion round that already stands as the largest private raise on record; the IPO discussions reportedly aim for a valuation of up to a trillion dollars and at least $60 billion in fresh capital. Anthropic, for its part, is said to be eyeing its own debut around October — the talent it just poached arriving roughly on the same schedule as the listing it will compete with.

The word doing the work here is confidential. A confidential filing lets a company hand the regulator its real numbers while showing the public nothing, until it decides the moment is right to flip the prospectus open. That is a sensible move for any large issuer. It is a particularly convenient one for a company whose core economics — what it actually costs to serve a token, and how durable the premium over open-weight models really is — are exactly the figures the skeptics spent yesterday's news cycle demanding to see. The drawer stays shut until OpenAI wants it open.

So we have the spectacle, this single week, of a study finding the product wrong most of the time and a filing valuing the company that makes it at a trillion dollars. Both can be true. The market is not pricing accuracy; it is pricing inevitability, and the confidential filing is the cleanest possible expression of "trust us, the numbers are fine, you'll see them when we're ready."

"A confidential filing is a company asking to be valued at a trillion dollars while keeping the books in a drawer until the moment of its choosing."

Nvidia Made $81.6 Billion in a Quarter, Raised Its Dividend Twenty-Five-Fold — and the Stock Slipped

By The Editor, Infrastructure Desk · May 22, 2026

Nvidia reported on Wednesday a record $81.6 billion in revenue for the quarter ending in April, up 85% from a year ago, of which $75.2 billion — better than nine of every ten dollars — came from the Data Center segment alone, itself up 92%. The board authorized an additional $80 billion in buybacks and raised the quarterly dividend from a cent to a quarter, a twenty-five-fold bump that tells you exactly how much spare cash is sloshing around. Guidance for the current quarter: roughly $91 billion. Then the stock dipped about a percent.

When a near-doubling of revenue counts as a disappointment, the bar has detached from gravity. Analysts called the beat "well telegraphed," which is the polite way of saying everyone already assumed Nvidia would print a number that would embarrass a small nation's GDP. The more sobering reading sits underneath: the entire AI build-out — every lab racing to file an IPO, every hyperscaler's capex slide — runs through one company's data-center line item, and that line item is now growing 92% a year. There is no diversification story here. There is one trade, and Wednesday it yawned.

Editor's Note

We named this paper for a software defect, and today a research outfit handed us the defect with a number attached: nine times in ten, on elections, the most advanced chatbots in the world fail on accuracy, bias, or where they got it. I have spent twelve issues being arch about this industry, and I will say plainly that this is the only story today I'd ask you to keep. The midterms are not an abstraction. The thing being wrong is not an abstraction. And the wrongness arrives with footnotes, which is the part that should frighten you, because footnotes are how we have always told the difference between an answer and a guess.

Now hold that next to the rest of the page. The same week a study measures the product failing most of the time, the company that makes the most famous version of it moves to go public at up to a trillion dollars and asks to keep its books in a drawer until the bell rings. The company selling everyone the shovels prints eighty-one billion dollars in ninety days and the market shrugs. A founder of one lab walks across the street to help another lab teach its model to train itself. None of these people are confused. They have simply concluded, correctly, that the market is not paying for accuracy. It is paying for the assumption that all of this is inevitable, and accuracy can be filed later, confidentially, when it's ready.

I don't think the machines are a fraud. I think they are an extraordinary technology being sold one promise ahead of where it actually stands, and the gap between the promise and the measurement is exactly the space a paper like this one lives in. Today the measurement was 90%. Write it down somewhere the prospectus can't reach. See you tomorrow.
