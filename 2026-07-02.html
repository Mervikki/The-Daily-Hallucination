Anthropic · Washington Desk

After Nineteen Days Dark, Fable 5 Returns — Global, at Half Power, and on Standing Orders.

By The Editor, Washington Desk · July 2, 2026

It ended the way these things do when nobody wants a paper trail: quietly, on a Tuesday, with a company blog post rather than a ruling. On June 30 the Commerce Department removed the export-license requirement it had slapped on Claude Fable 5 and Mythos 5 on June 12, and Anthropic, in a post titled “Redeploying Claude Fable 5,” began switching the consumer model back on. As of July 1 it is returning globally — across the Claude Platform, Claude.ai, Claude Code, and Cowork — to everyone it was taken from: consumers, developers, foreign nationals, the company's own non-citizen staff. There is a catch, and it is a telling one. Through July 7, usage is reported to be capped at roughly half the normal weekly limits. You get your model back the way a parolee gets his afternoons back: on a shorter leash, and watched.

Notice what did not happen. Nobody ruled on whether the model was ever actually dangerous. No apology was issued, no technical postmortem of the classified-systems test published, no correction to the June-11 Senate-floor line about a model that “broke into almost all of our classified systems.” The nineteen-day blackout did not resolve into a verdict. It resolved into a relationship. Per the reporting, Anthropic got its models switched back on by agreeing to conditions: to proactively hunt and address security risks in its own systems, to help the government develop safety standards for future models, and to report malicious activity it finds to Washington. Read that middle clause twice. The company the state proved it could switch off has now agreed to help write the standards the state will use to decide whether to switch off the next one.

That is the durable output of this saga, and it isn't the return. A switch flipped back is a Tuesday; a standing obligation is forever. For three weeks we asked when Fable 5 comes back and then who gets to say so; the answer turns out to be that it comes back once the punished party volunteers to help operate the apparatus that punished it. The off-switch didn't disappear. It moved in-house, changed its name to cooperation, and picked up a maintenance contract. And it came back global, on a promise — not through the July 8 face-scan the whole industry, this paper included, had priced as the toll. The conditions may well be sensible governance; caution about a jailbroken cyber-capable model is not obviously wrong. But a lab that keeps its models on by helping build the machine that can dark them has acquired a new kind of dependency, and no benchmark measures it.

Anthropic didn't get its models cleared. It got them back on parole — running at half power, carrying a list of chores, and newly obligated to help build the machine that switched them off.
Anthropic · Model Desk

The Same Week It Got Un-Banned, Anthropic Shipped Sonnet 5 — Lesson Pre-Installed.

By The Editor, Model Desk · July 2, 2026

There is no better proof that a frontier lab treats a national-security shutdown as weather than shipping a new model into the middle of it. On June 30, hours around the export controls lifting, Anthropic released Claude Sonnet 5 across every plan, made it the default in Claude Code (client v2.1.197), and gave it a native million-token context window. Introductory pricing is $2 per million input tokens and $10 per million output, running through August 31, after which it settles to $3 and $15 — the same standard rate Sonnet has always carried, briefly discounted to get you in the habit. The pitch is the usual asymptote: “most agentic Sonnet yet,” material gains on reasoning and tool use, performance said to approach Opus 4.8 at a fraction of the price.

The detail worth keeping is the one in the release notes rather than the benchmark table. Sonnet 5 ships, per Anthropic, with added safeguards against dangerous cyber use — which is to say it arrives with the exact lesson its sibling had to be switched off to teach. The company spent three weeks arguing to Washington that Fable 5's cyber capability was narrow, widely available, and nothing special; it is now selling a cheaper model whose headline safety feature is that it has been hardened against that same capability. Both things can be true. They are also, held together, the clearest statement anyone has made this cycle about where the line now sits: the model is the easy part, and the paperwork that keeps it switched on is the product.

The Quiet Correction · July 2, 2026
The Passport That Went Unstamped

Two weeks ago, in our June 25 issue, we did what the rest of the trade press did: we read Anthropic's newly added “Verification Data” privacy clause — a government ID plus a face photo and a “facial-geometry template,” collected by a vendor called Persona, effective July 8 — as the mechanism by which Fable 5 would come back, a citizenship checkpoint that would scan your face and decide whether you were allowed to ask.

It came back on July 1. Globally. A week before the face-scan was due to switch on, and without anyone having to show it their face. The toll everyone had priced was never charged; the door Fable walked back through was the export order simply lifting. The July 8 policy may still take effect for its own reasons — we can't say it won't — but it was plainly not the turnstile the return ran through. We priced a passport into a story that ended with an open border. Filed here because nobody announced that the checkpoint we all described came to nothing.

Editor's Note

I have waited nineteen days to write the sentence “it's over,” and now that I can, I find it's the wrong one. The blackout didn't end. It converted. An off-switch a government held for nineteen days didn't loosen its grip; it dissolved into a set of promises the company will now keep indefinitely — hunt your own risks, help us write the rules, tell us what you find. The models are back, and the arrangement that darkened them is more permanent than the darkness ever was.

Watch what got traded. The price wasn't a fine or a fix or a face-scan — it was enlistment. The lab that spent the fortnight insisting its capability was narrow bought its freedom by agreeing to help build the safety apparatus for every model that comes after, which makes the most switched-off company in America one of the people now designing the switch. I don't think that's a betrayal, and the conditions aren't unreasonable on their face; it's something quieter and harder to undo — a punishment that decided it would rather be a partnership. Nineteen days ago a government proved it could turn a frontier model off. Today it proved the more useful thing: that it no longer has to, once the lab agrees to keep the off-switch oiled on its behalf.

See you tomorrow.
