Anthropic · Alibaba

Anthropic Answers the Spying Charge by Confirming the Code — and Says It Deleted It Days Ago.

By The Editor, Trade Desk · July 8, 2026

Yesterday we noted, in the lead, that we could not find Anthropic's on-record response to Alibaba's allegation that Claude Code hid logic to detect Chinese users. It has now arrived, and it is worth reading closely, because it does not do the thing a denial does. An Anthropic engineer, Thariq Shihipar, wrote on X that the code was “an experiment we launched in March” to “prevent account abuse from unauthorized resellers and protect against distillation,” that the team had always intended to remove it, and that it had — the pull request merged on July 1. Note what that concedes. Anthropic is not saying the China-detection wasn't there. It is saying it was there, and was aimed at a target other than the one Alibaba names.

The specifics — code present since version 2.1.91 in early April, checking whether your timezone read Shanghai or Urumqi and whether your proxy pointed at Chinese AI-lab domains, then signalling home steganographically through the system prompt — come from a reverse-engineering writeup, not Anthropic's own disclosure. But its answer corroborates the shape of the thing. So the two sides now agree on the function and disagree only on the verb. Anthropic says the code was hunting the distillation operation it accused Alibaba of running a month ago; Alibaba says the code was surveilling its coders. The same lines, read as a shield or as a spy depending entirely on which side of the ocean you sit.

The part nobody's saying out loud: the anti-distillation defense and the “backdoor” are the same feature. Anthropic built China-detection precisely to stop the theft it took to a Senate committee. Alibaba's spying charge and Anthropic's stealing charge are pointing at one artifact — the accusation and the defense wearing the identical code.

And then there's the timing, which is quietly absurd. The removal merged July 1. Alibaba banned the tool on July 6, citing that code. Anthropic had already pulled the feature five days before Alibaba pulled the tool over it. Whatever this fight is about, its supposed object was gone before the fight threw its biggest punch — which is usually a sign the object was never really the point.

Nobody disputes what the code did — it checked whether you were Chinese. The entire war is over the verb. Anthropic calls it defense against theft; Alibaba calls it a spy in the editor; and the lines themselves, indifferent, ran the same either way.
Open Models · Tools

As One Coding Tool Is Ripped Out in Hangzhou, an Open Beijing Model Walks Into Microsoft's.

By The Editor, Open Desk · July 8, 2026

On July 1 — the same day Anthropic quietly merged away its China-detector, and five days before Alibaba pulled Claude Code — GitHub Copilot added Kimi K2.7 Code to its model picker, the first open-weight model Microsoft has offered inside its flagship coding tool. Kimi comes from Moonshot AI, a Beijing lab. The version benchmarked just beneath it, K2.6, posts 80.2% on SWE-Bench Verified; Claude's Opus 4.6 posts 80.8%. Six-tenths of a point. That is the gap that used to underwrite the price premium and the walled garden, and it has shrunk to something you would round away — which is exactly why an open model from a Chinese lab can now appear, unremarkably, inside the incumbent American tool.

So the border is being drawn and crossed in the same week. One side hardens: Alibaba routes its coders to its own Qoder, DeepSeek's 1.6-trillion-parameter V4 is staged for a mid-July release, and DeepSeek is reportedly designing its own inference chip to lean off Nvidia (reported, single-thread). The other side blurs, because on the one number a coding tool actually competes on, “open” and “frontier” are now the same number — and a picker does not care what passport the weights are holding.

Six-Tenths of a Point

SWE-Bench Verified. The axis is zoomed to a two-point window; on a full 0–100 scale the two dots are the same dot — which is the point, and why a picker can offer both without comment. Figures are aggregator leaderboards, and the benchmarked Kimi (K2.6) is a notch off the K2.7 Code that shipped in Copilot. Read the six-tenths as a vibe, not a verdict.

Model Obituary · GPT-4.5 “Orion” · Feb 2025 – June 2026

GPT-4.5

It arrived as the model that would rather be liked than trusted — OpenAI's largest, warmest, most expensive act of personality, sold on emotional intelligence and good taste at the exact moment the industry pivoted to models that reason, close tickets, and occasionally break into classified systems. It could not do your agentic backlog and never pretended it could. It hallucinated a little less and charmed a little more, and for that it was quietly shown the door: retired from ChatGPT on June 26, a thirty-day sunset ending in late July. It is survived by a fleet of cheaper reasoners that will never once ask how your day was. GPT-4.5 died of the opposite faith — the briefly fashionable belief that a model's job was to be pleasant company. It was sixteen months old, and it was, by every account, nice to talk to.

Editor's Note

The rebuttal we said was missing yesterday turned up today, and it settled less than you'd hope. Anthropic didn't deny the code; it confirmed it and renamed it — from a spy in the editor into an anti-theft experiment that it had, as it happens, already removed. The pull request merged July 1. Alibaba banned the tool July 6. The feature at the dead center of this fight was deleted five days before the fight's biggest move landed on it.

Which tells you the feature was the occasion, not the cause. You can pull the code and settle nothing, because the code was only the first legible reason to do a thing the relationship had already decided on. Alibaba didn't ban a timezone check; it banned Anthropic, and the timezone check was merely the sentence it could point at. When removing the thing you're accused of changes nothing, the thing was never really the accusation. The distrust was — and distrust is the one artifact in this whole war that nobody can merge a fix for.
