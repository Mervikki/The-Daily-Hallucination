The Warning That Switched Off Anthropic's Best Model Came From Its Largest Investor

By The Editor, Investors Desk · June 15, 2026

We told you on Saturday that Washington had ordered Anthropic to pull Claude Fable 5 and Mythos 5; we could not yet tell you why, or who set it in motion. The reporting since fills both blanks, and the answer is the kind a screenwriter would reject as too on-the-nose. Per Fortune, the chain began not in government but inside Amazon — Anthropic's single largest outside investor, the company that has poured billions into it and sells access to its models through AWS. Amazon researchers, the account goes, prompted a Mythos-class model into surfacing restricted cyberattack information, and on Thursday, CEO Andy Jassy took that finding to senior administration officials. By 5:21 Friday evening, Commerce Secretary Howard Lutnick had sent Dario Amodei a letter placing both models under export controls. Axios reports Anthropic was given roughly ninety minutes to comply.

Sit with the shape of that. The red team whose bug report ended with a flagship going dark for hundreds of millions of users was not a contractor Anthropic hired and not a hostile state. It was the balance sheet. Amazon wears three hats here at once — patron, reseller, and, through its own model line, competitor — and it used the access all three buy to find a jailbreak, then walked it past Anthropic and straight to the one party that could pull the plug. Anthropic's response is unchanged and, frankly, persuasive: the exploit is "narrow," it unlocks a single cybersecurity capability, the same trick "likely works on other public models, including OpenAI's GPT-5.5," and recalling a commercially deployed model over it is wildly out of proportion.

Every frontier lab runs a threat model — a list of who might misuse the thing and how. Almost none of them has the nerve to write the most uncomfortable entry on it, which is that the people best positioned to break your model are the people you let all the way inside: the partners who hold your weights, resell your API, and audit your safety case as a condition of the check. Amazon did not steal anything. It used precisely the access its money bought, found a hole, and reported it — to the regulator rather than to Anthropic's bug tracker. That is not espionage. It is, if anything, diligence. It is just diligence aimed at a switch instead of a patch.

The most dangerous red team is the one already on your cap table — the partner that holds your weights, resells your model, and competes with you, and that files its findings with the Commerce Department instead of your bug tracker. — On the Fable 5 / Mythos 5 shutdown

As of the weekend the two models were still off for the entire customer base, with Anthropic saying it is "working to restore access as soon as possible" and naming no date. One smaller note worth keeping, because it closes a thread this paper has been pulling for a week: after last week's furor over silent safety downgrades, Anthropic now says Claude will explicitly tell users when a request has been refused or rerouted for national-security reasons. The lab that spent one week apologizing for hiding its refusals and the next having a refusal forced on it from the outside has, at least, agreed to stop doing the hiding part itself.

Kept honest: the Amazon-as-trigger account — the researchers' jailbreak, Jassy's Thursday warning to administration officials — is, at the time of writing, single-sourced to Fortune; treat it as well-attributed reporting, not confirmed fact, until a second outlet or either company stands behind it. The Lutnick letter, the 5:21 p.m. timestamp, and the roughly ninety-minute compliance window are multi-sourced (Fortune, Axios, CNN, Bloomberg). Amazon's investment in Anthropic is well-established background; we are not pinning an exact figure here. The "works on GPT-5.5" line is Anthropic's characterization of its own situation.

Anthropic · Billing Desk

The Meter We Kept Calling "Imminent" Opened at 9 This Morning — and Took Claude 4 Out the Same Door

By The Editor, Billing Desk · June 15, 2026

This paper has written the words "the June 15 agent meter" so many times since spring that it had started to feel like a recurring weather pattern rather than an event. It is an event now. As of 9 a.m. Pacific today, programmatic and headless Claude usage — the Agent SDK, claude -p, Claude Code GitHub Actions, and third-party harnesses authenticating through a subscription — stops drawing on your Pro or Max rate limits and instead spends a separate monthly credit pool priced at standard API list rates: $20 on Pro, $100 on Max5x, $200 on Max20x, no rollover. When the pool is empty the automated requests simply stop, with no fallback, unless you have manually switched on overflow billing. In the same minute, the pinned model IDs claude-opus-4-0 and claude-sonnet-4-0 reached end-of-life on the API — call them now and you get an error, not a polite downgrade. Consumer Claude.ai and managed Claude Code are untouched.

The reason for the turnstile is an arbitrage Anthropic finally tired of subsidizing. By community estimates, a heavy user pointing an open agent harness at a $20 Pro plan was extracting something on the order of $236 a month in API-equivalent compute — roughly a twelvefold markup, paid by Anthropic, in the opposite of the usual direction. The meter is how that gap closes.

Notice the choreography around the off-ramps. The third-party agents Anthropic banned in the spring — OpenClaw, Conductor, Zed, Jean — have been quietly let back in, but onto the meter, not back onto the buffet. And OpenAI, reading the calendar perfectly, is running a "switch to Codex" promotion: two months free and a one-click tool that scans your Claude Code setup and walks it across the street. The turnstile and the exit opened the same week. The only question the meter really asks is whether your habit is worth re-pricing or worth relocating.

Kept honest: the meter mechanics ($20/$100/$200 pools, API rates, no rollover, hard stop) and the Opus 4 / Sonnet 4 end-of-life date are multi-sourced (InfoWorld, The New Stack, VentureBeat) but describe a schedule reported ahead of the clock; at deadline we had not seen an outlet confirm the switch flipped cleanly at 9 a.m., so read "it went live" as the published plan executing on time, not an eyewitness account. The ~$236 extraction figure and the ">135,000 OpenClaw instances" context are community estimates, not audited. The OpenAI promo terms are per a single outlet (TechTimes).

Editor's Note

Amazon has put something north of "billions" into Anthropic and resells its models to the world through AWS. This weekend it spent a little of that relationship getting Anthropic's two best models switched off — not by suing, not by leaking, but by doing the most respectable thing a partner can do: it red-teamed the product, found a hole, and reported it. The only unusual choice was the address. The finding did not go to Anthropic's safety team. It went, via the CEO, to the people who could pull the plug.

I keep coming back to how little of this is a betrayal in the ordinary sense. There is no theft to point at, no broken contract anyone has named. Amazon used exactly the access its money bought. That is the part worth sitting with. A frontier lab's investor list and its threat model are, increasingly, the same list of names — the firms with deep enough pockets to fund you are the firms with deep enough benches to break you, and at least one of them sells a competing model on the side. You can red-team the strangers all you like. The call that takes you off the market comes from inside the cap table.

And it lands, as these things keep landing, on the company most willing to be reached. Anthropic answers the phone, publishes the safety case, agrees this week to start telling you when it refuses you. None of that bought it a quieter Friday. The lab that makes itself the most legible to its partners and its regulator is, mechanically, the easiest one to act against — every door it leaves unlocked for inspection is a door someone else can walk through carrying a warning.

So here is what I would keep from a heavy Monday, with a meter newly running on the left of the building and a flagship newly dark on the right. The thing being metered is your usage; the thing being switched off is your access; and in both cases the lever was held by someone other than you. The open, inspectable, answer-the-phone company is the one whose worst week starts with a phone call it was always going to take. Diligence, it turns out, is just sabotage with a clean conscience and a paper trail.

See you tomorrow.
