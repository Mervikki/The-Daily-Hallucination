Anthropic · Capital Desk

Anthropic Just Borrowed $35 Billion — and Arranged for Almost None of It to Show Up on Its Books.

By The Editor, Capital Desk · June 8, 2026

The private-credit package that Apollo and Blackstone had been shopping since late May has closed. Bloomberg reported on June 5 that the funds wrapped roughly $35 billion in debt — a hair under the $36 billion that had been floated — to buy custom Google TPUs, the Broadcom-designed chips, and lease them back to Anthropic for data centers in New York, Texas, Louisiana, and Indiana. The structure is the point. The chips are owned by a financing vehicle, not by Anthropic; Anthropic rents them. So the $35 billion sits inside a special-purpose entity, off Anthropic's own balance sheet, recorded as a lease rather than as debt the company carries directly.

The timing is what makes it worth a full column. Anthropic filed confidentially to go public on June 1. An S-1 is the most exhaustive act of disclosure a private company ever performs — every liability, every risk, the whole machine laid open for the market to price. And in the same seven days that it began that ritual of showing everything, the company finished arranging $35 billion of obligation engineered to sit just outside the frame. Both things are legal, ordinary, and increasingly standard for AI infrastructure at this scale. Set side by side they still describe a particular instinct: open the books with one hand, park the heaviest thing you can off them with the other.

Anatomy of the $35B · one chip ≈ $5B

Six of the seven blocks carry Broadcom's residual-value guarantee; the lone rust block — the ~$4.5B junior tranche — is the only slice carrying Anthropic's own credit risk. Tranche sizes and yields are reporter-sourced from deal arrangers, not company-confirmed.

Look at the colors and you find the other quiet feature. Most of the stack — the senior A1 and A2 tranches, roughly $31 billion of it — is wrapped in a residual-value support agreement from Broadcom, the company that designed the chips. If Anthropic ever stops paying and the TPUs have to be resold for less than the debt, Broadcom covers the shortfall. Which means the lenders financing the demand for these chips are insured against that demand collapsing by the firm that sells the chips. The supplier is underwriting the customer's loan. Only the small rust slice — the $4.5 billion junior notes at something like 8.5 percent — carries naked risk, and the market priced that nakedness at three points of extra yield.

A company about to open its books for the public just borrowed thirty-five billion dollars and arranged for almost none of it to appear in them. — On the Apollo–Blackstone TPU financing

I don't think there's a scandal here, and I want to say that plainly — lease-financing capital equipment off the balance sheet is older than the silicon and perfectly within the rules. What's worth marking is that the rules now let a single private company assemble $65 billion in equity, $35 billion in chip debt, and a confidential IPO inside ten weeks, and arrange the largest of those three to be the one the IPO never quite has to explain. The headline number all spring has been the valuation. The number I'd write down is the one wearing a lease's clothing.

ChatGPT · Platforms Desk

OpenAI Reportedly Wants to Kill the Chat Box. The Problem With the Chat Box Is That Nobody Pays for It.

By The Editor, Platforms Desk · June 8, 2026

The Financial Times reported on June 7, citing more than a dozen current and former employees, that OpenAI is planning a fundamental rebuild of ChatGPT — pushing the product away from open-ended conversation and toward agents, coding tools, image generation, and third-party apps from partners like Canva and Booking.com. One staffer's framing, per the report, is blunt: "Chat is dead." The changes are said to be coming "in the coming weeks," starting on the website and mobile apps. Nothing has shipped; this is a leaked direction, not a release.

Read past the obituary for the text box and the logic is straightforward, even a little sad. The chat box is the most-used and best-loved thing OpenAI ever made — it is the entire reason "ChatGPT" became a household word — and it is also the surface that monetizes worst. People come to talk, talk for free, and leave. Agents that do tasks, coding tools developers expense, partner apps that pay a cut: those are the surfaces with a revenue meter attached. "Chat is dead" isn't a verdict on whether people want to chat. They obviously do. It's a verdict on whether chatting pays the inference bill, and that answer arrived a while ago.

What I'd watch is the direction of the nudge, not the menu of new features. There is a version of this redesign that genuinely serves people — agents and tools are useful — and a version that quietly makes the free thing you came for a little harder to find than the partner app that books your hotel. The same company just started selling ads in the free tier and gave its memory a mind of its own. A product that has decided its beloved front door doesn't pay rent will, sooner or later, move the door.

Reliability · Infrastructure Desk

A Week On, Still No Postmortem for the Claude Outage — and a New Claim That It Crossed the Wires.

By The Editor, Infrastructure Desk · June 8, 2026

Six days after Claude went dark worldwide on the morning of June 2 — a fix went in within hours, but the disruption was global — Anthropic has yet to publish a formal root-cause report. The widely repeated explanation, that a Claude Code bug spawned sub-agents in a runaway loop until capacity gave out, remains secondary reporting; the company's own public language at the time stuck to "capacity constraints." The newer thread is sharper: Anthropic is reportedly looking into claims, circulating on social media, that during the incident some users were served responses meant for other people.

If true, that would be a different category of problem than a model that returns errors — a model that returns the wrong person's conversation is a confidentiality event, not a capacity one. The reporting so far says engineers have attributed the episode to infrastructure rather than a breach and have not confirmed any actual data exposure. Which is the honest place to leave it for now: an unverified claim, a missing postmortem, and a company that filed to go public the day before the lights went out and has reasons of its own to want the official version published precisely, not quickly.

Editor's Note

It closed at thirty-five billion, a billion shy of the number that had been shopped, and that small shortfall is the least interesting thing about it. The interesting word in the whole deal is off. The chips are owned by a vehicle, leased to Anthropic, and the borrowing that bought them sits off the balance sheet the company is, this very week, preparing to show the public in the most detailed document it will ever file. I keep turning that over because both moves are normal — off-balance-sheet equipment leasing is ancient, an S-1 is routine — and yet performed in the same seven days they describe a single posture: disclose everything, and arrange the heaviest thing to be the thing that doesn't quite count as something to disclose.

The detail under the detail is the Broadcom guarantee. Strip the jargon and a chipmaker has agreed to cover the lenders if the customer can't pay and the chips won't resell for enough. The people lending against demand for these chips are insured against that demand failing — by the company whose entire business is that the demand doesn't fail. It's not fraud and it's not even unusual anymore; it is what this industry does to keep building when the thing it's building costs more than its revenue can carry. But it means the question worth asking about the IPO isn't what the valuation is. It's where the obligations are parked, and who, exactly, is standing behind each other when you trace the line all the way around.

So when the prospectus surfaces — and it will, with a number on it built to be the headline — read past the number to the leases, the vehicles, and the guarantees, because that is where a capital-hungry company keeps the parts of its story it would rather you saw last. The valuation is the thing they want you to remember. The structure is the thing that tells you whether the valuation is a fact or a hope.
