OpenAI · Policy

GPT-5.6 Walks Out of the Government's Reading Room, and No One Will Say Who Held the Door.

By The Editor, Policy Desk · July 9, 2026

GPT-5.6 goes public today. The family arrives in three sizes — Sol at the top for reasoning and long agentic runs, Terra in the middle billed at roughly GPT-5.5's level for about half the price, and Luna underneath as the cheap-and-fast option — carrying two new switches OpenAI wants you to notice: a “max reasoning effort” dial and an “ultra mode” that spins up subagents to work a problem in parallel. All of which is the ordinary furniture of a model launch. What isn't ordinary is where GPT-5.6 spent the last three weeks. It was unveiled on June 26 confined to roughly twenty government-vetted partners, at the government's own request, for a pre-release review. Today the preview ends and the family ships to everyone. Read the sequence plainly and it says: something was held for a government reading, and then it was let out.

Which makes the release, functionally, a clearance. A model was handed to a reviewer with the power to keep it in the room, the reviewer did not keep it in the room, and now it is on sale. That is what a green light is. Except nobody involved will call it one — the White House has already denied giving OpenAI a “green light,” and OpenAI, which objected at the outset that this kind of access process “shouldn't become the long-term default,” complied anyway and is now launching through the very door it grumbled about. Both sides need the same ambiguity. OpenAI gets to ship; the government gets to not own the shipment.

And here is the part that should keep you up: the standard this review was supposedly measuring against does not exist yet. The voluntary frontier-release framework — the one that's meant to define which models trigger a pre-read and at what capability threshold — was forecast to land the week of July 7 and hasn't. The June 2 executive order that ordered it gives the agencies until August 1 to write the thing. So a frontier model just passed a review whose rubric is still a draft, cleared a bar nobody has finished drawing, and reached the public before the rule that governs its reaching the public was published.

The tell is the absence of a signature. A criterion you never wrote down is a criterion no lab can fail, no reporter can quote, and no court can test. It ships the models it likes and owns none of the decisions — governance as a room you can be shown out of, with no lock anyone will admit is on the door.

GPT-5.6 spent three weeks in a government reading room and walked out today a public product. Nobody will call that a clearance — least of all the people who did the reading. A green light works better when no one admits to flipping it.

The Chip Selloff Didn't Bounce. It Dug In.

By The Editor, Markets Desk · July 9, 2026

The semiconductor rout that opened July went looking for a floor this week and kept falling through it. Intel was reportedly down around a fifth into July 8; earlier legs of the same wave took Marvell down roughly seventeen percent, Broadcom near thirteen, AMD around eight, and clipped several hundred billion off Nvidia's market value. The triggers stack the way they always do at a top — a soft Samsung print, then a broader flinch about whether the hyperscalers can keep spending at this clip forever. None of it was a story about a model getting worse.

That's the uncomfortable shape of it. The capability didn't degrade this week; the conviction that it was worth pre-buying did. The bulls' counter is real — 2026 AI capital spending is still tracking somewhere around seven hundred billion dollars, up better than three-quarters year on year, and plenty of desks are calling this a mid-cycle reset rather than a verdict. But a reset is a story the dip-buyers tell, and this week the dip-buyers stayed home. The spending is a promise about demand that hasn't arrived; the selloff is the market checking whether the promise still clears.

The Glossary · Term of the Day

Reasoning Effort (n.)

The official definition

A user-adjustable setting, new to the GPT-5.6 family, that controls how much computation the model devotes to a problem before it answers. Turn it up — to “max” — for the hard ones.

The real definition

A paid slider whose mere existence concedes that at every setting below the top one, the model is holding some back. You may now be billed extra for the machine to try, which is a remarkable way to learn what the default was. Sold as a knob for your convenience; priced as a confession.

Editor's Note

The thing to sit with today isn't that a government looked at a frontier model before the public did. It's the order of operations. GPT-5.6 cleared a review whose rulebook is still a draft — the voluntary standards that are supposed to say which models get read, and against what bar, were due the week of July 7, didn't come, and now aren't owed until August 1. So the enforcement is fully operational and the standard it enforces hasn't been written. The gate opened; the lock is a forthcoming publication.

That is not a gap someone forgot to close. An unwritten criterion is the most comfortable kind of power to hold, because there's nothing to fail, nothing to quote back, nothing a court can test — only a decision that ships the models it approves of and disclaims the word for what it did. We got the verdict before the law. And a verdict handed down before the law is written isn't a mistake in the process; for a little while, quietly, it is the process. See you tomorrow.

— The Editor
