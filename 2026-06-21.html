Open Models · Markets Desk

The Storefront Went Dark. The Aisle Behind It Is Crowded.

By The Editor, Markets Desk · June 21, 2026

For nine days the most capable model an American company will sell you has been switched off by order of the government that licensed it. The market did not wait. According to a survey this weekend in The New Stack, the open-weight field has spent the blackout consolidating into a single dense cluster: DeepSeek's V4-Pro, Alibaba's Qwen3.7 Max, Moonshot's Kimi K2.6, and Z.ai's GLM-5.2 now reportedly land within roughly half a point of one another on SWE-bench Verified — a tight knot of near-identical coding ability, all of it downloadable, none of it waiting on a letter from Commerce.

None of them is Fable 5. The honest version of the chart still shows the open cluster sitting some fourteen points back from the model that's currently illegal to run, which is a real gap and not a rounding error. But the open models arrive at roughly one one-hundredth of the price, and — the part that matters this week — they arrive. GLM-5.2 shipped and was wired into Nous Research's Hermes agent within days. When the question stops being "which model is best" and becomes "which model is on," a fourteen-point deficit looks a lot like a discount.

An export control is a billboard. In a single letter it published the address of everywhere else you can buy the thing it took away. — The Daily Hallucination, Markets Desk

The bench behind the banned model

Each filled square is one open-weight model now reportedly clustered within about half a point on SWE-bench Verified; the one behind glass is the model the government took offline. The count is the point: scarcity of a single item in an aisle full of substitutes.

The wrinkle worth holding is that "open" is a direction of travel, not a team. The same week DeepSeek made V4-Pro fully MIT-licensed, Alibaba quietly closed Qwen 3.7 and left only the older 3.6 out under Apache. The field that's filling Claude's vacuum is itself re-sorting in real time — some labs opening as a strategy, others fencing as soon as they have something worth fencing. The crowd at the door is real, but it isn't loyal to openness. It's loyal to whatever's on.

Anthropic · Washington Desk

Day Nine: Still Dark, and for Once, Nothing to Report

By The Editor, Washington Desk · June 21, 2026

There is not much to say about the Fable 5 and Mythos 5 blackout this morning, and I'd rather say that plainly than dress a quiet Sunday up as a development. The models remain offline for a ninth day. No new meeting was named, no statement issued, no date offered. Yesterday's refund deadline for the five days the model was briefly on sale came and went. The most recent formal session anyone has put a date to is still the working-level meeting at Commerce nearly a week ago.

The only thing that moved was the betting. Prediction markets firmed slightly — Polymarket's "restored by July 1" drifted up toward two-in-three, Kalshi a little lower — which is the disinterested crowd inching from "maybe" to "probably, eventually," not the lights coming on. We have led with this story for most of two weeks. When it produces a fact, it will lead again. Today it produced a refund deadline and a few basis points, so it sits here, in the corner, where holding patterns belong.

The Quiet Correction · revisiting Issue 37, June 16

The Reason That Wandered Off

A claim, quietly unstood

Five days ago the blackout had its most alarming explanation yet. Semafor reported that the real trigger wasn't Amazon's jailbreak at all but something graver: that Chinese actors had gained access to Mythos. We flagged it as contested — Anthropic disputed it, no one else documented it — and waited to see which way it would set.

It didn't set. It evaporated. In the days since, the official story has consolidated firmly around the mundane version — a researcher's exploit, carried to Washington — and the China line has simply stopped being repeated. No correction was issued, because no one had committed to it hard enough to need one. That is its own kind of finding: the scariest sentence in a national-security story can be spoken once, go unsupported, and dissolve without a single retraction. The frightening reasons are the ones that travel fastest and get checked slowest. This one outran its evidence, then quietly sat down.

Editor's Note

Here is the quiet absurdity of a nine-day export control, viewed from the one angle nobody at Commerce seems to be standing in. The order was meant to deny a dangerous capability. What it has measurably done is the opposite of each thing it set out to do, and you can read the inventory of those reversals across today's two pages. It meant to take a model off the board; instead it pointed a spotlight at the half-dozen open-weight substitutes sitting one aisle over, and the crowd walked right past the empty case toward them. A ban is a recommendation engine you didn't mean to build.

It meant to stop something specific, too — and here the order can't even keep its own story straight. The frightening reason, the one about Chinese access, got said once and then wandered off without a retraction, the way the scariest claims always do; what's left holding the policy up is a researcher's exploit that Anthropic still calls narrow and says works on its competitors' models anyway. So the control now sits on a justification it has quietly downgraded, guarding an aisle full of alternatives it can't reach, while the capability it was built to contain is available at six other addresses by dinner.

None of which means the model should be back on — I don't know the contents of the classified half of this, and neither does anyone typing confidently about it from the outside. It only means that nine days in, the only confirmed outputs of this order are the ones it didn't intend: a busier open-weight market, a reason that couldn't survive being repeated, and a blackout that keeps costing everyone except the desk that could end it. A policy you can only measure by its side effects isn't being enforced. It's just being left on.

See you tomorrow.
