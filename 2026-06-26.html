Anthropic · Security Desk

The Ban's Missing Reason Showed Up. It Arrived in Two Different Sizes.

By The Editor, Security Desk · June 26, 2026

For two weeks this magazine kept noting that the most aggressive AI order in US history had a hole where its reason should be — a rationale that was variously "narrow," contested, or quietly evaporating. This week a piece of that reason walked back in, and it brought a source. Per the Associated Press, a US official says that during a testing exercise run through Anthropic's "Project Glasswing" with American intelligence agencies, the company's Mythos model found vulnerabilities in highly sensitive, classified government systems — and did it within hours. The account was picked up across CNBC, SecurityWeek, Federal News Network and Euronews. After thirteen days of a justification nobody could pin down, here, at last, is something concrete to pin.

Now read it twice, because the order's whole weight is balanced on a verb. The official told the AP that the model found the vulnerabilities and did not necessarily exploit them in that window — the difference between cataloguing unlocked doors and walking through one. Senator Mark Warner, however, had already described the same capability at a June 11 Senate Banking hearing in a register all his own: the tool, he said, "broke into almost all of our classified systems, not in weeks but in hours." Found versus broke into. Possibly the same afternoon; emphatically not the same sentence. One is a fire drill that lists every exit nobody had locked. The other is a fire.

And notice the order they arrived in. Warner's louder line came on June 11 — a day before the export order pulled Fable 5 and Mythos 5 dark. The AP's carefully hedged version surfaced June 23, eleven days after. The dramatization led and may well have helped move the order; the caveat followed once the models were already off. So the thing finally settled is the easy half: the capability is real, and we won't pretend otherwise. What remains open is its size — and size, in this story, turns out to be a function of which official is talking and which room he is standing in when he picks the word.

A test that "found vulnerabilities but did not necessarily exploit them" became, on the Senate floor, a model that "broke into almost all of our classified systems." The order was built on the louder sentence. — The Daily Hallucination, Security Desk

None of which means the model belongs back on tomorrow; we still cannot see the classified half, and a vulnerability found in hours is a genuinely unsettling thing to find. But a national-security control that suspends a frontier model for an entire customer base is the kind of instrument you would want resting on the second verb, not the first — on confirmed entry, not catalogued opportunity. The reason has finally been documented. It would be useful to know, before the precedent hardens, which of its two sizes is the true one.

Policy · Capitol Hill

The Deadline Lands Today; the Answer Came in Through the Window

By The Editor, Washington Desk · June 26, 2026

Today, June 26, is the day Commerce Secretary Howard Lutnick is supposed to put it in writing. The bipartisan four — Liccardo, Lieu, Obernolte, Franklin — gave him until this afternoon to supply the order's legal authority, its technical evaluation, the process that produced it, and the criteria for restoring access. As of this morning, no public response had surfaced. Read that as an absence, not a confirmed silence; a letter could land before the day is out.

But here is the small irony sitting under the deadline. Congress demanded the order's factual basis on the record — and this week the factual basis went public anyway, through an AP wire and a senator's hearing clip, rather than a Commerce letter. The documentation showed up in every channel except the one that formally asked for it. And the lawmakers' sharpest question is the one a leaked classified-systems anecdote does nothing to answer: whether export law — EAR §744.22, written for "the export of items," for crates and code on disks — was ever the right tool to switch off an API call. A scary fact and a legal authority are different objects. Today was supposed to produce the second. So far it has only re-aired the first.

OpenAI · Silicon Desk

OpenAI Built Its Own Chip. The Whole Point of It Is Nvidia.

By The Editor, Silicon Desk · June 26, 2026

On June 24, OpenAI and Broadcom unveiled "Jalapeño," OpenAI's first piece of custom silicon — an inference accelerator the company says it designed from scratch and taped out in roughly nine months, with engineering samples already running real workloads in the lab, GPT-5.3-Codex-Spark among them. OpenAI claims performance-per-watt "substantially better" than the current state of the art, which is a vendor's claim about its own chip and should be read at that volume. First deployment is targeted for the end of 2026.

It makes OpenAI the fifth frontier name to build its own door out of Nvidia's house. Google has TPUs, Amazon has Trainium and Inferentia, Microsoft has Maia, Meta has MTIA. Each was designed, in part, to need Nvidia a little less — and each of those companies still trains and runs the overwhelming majority of its real work on Nvidia anyway.

The in-house silicon club just added a member

Five in-house silicon programs at very different stages — Jalapeño is the newest and won't deploy until late 2026. The escape hatch and the dependency are, for now, the same building.

That is the quiet comedy of every one of these announcements. The chip is sold as independence, and it is real engineering aimed at a real bill. But you do not tape out a custom accelerator because you have escaped your supplier; you do it because you can't, yet, and would dearly like to. Jalapeño is OpenAI saying, very expensively, that it would rather not keep renting — from a landlord whose ground floor it is still standing on.

Editor's Note

Two sentences now hold up the most forceful AI order this country has issued, and they were spoken by two people on the same side. One: a model "found vulnerabilities" in classified systems and "did not necessarily exploit them." Two: it "broke into almost all of our classified systems, not in weeks but in hours." Same testing exercise, very likely the same hours — and the entire weight of a national-security control sits in the gap between found and broke into. A list of unlocked doors and a record of someone walking through them are not the same document. The order was written off the second one.

For two weeks I kept writing that the order's frightening reason kept wandering off without a source, so let me be straight now that a piece of it has come back wearing the Associated Press and a senator's name: the capability looks real, and I'm not going to perform skepticism I no longer have. But watch the order of the verbs. The dramatic one — "broke into" — was spoken June 11, the day before the models went dark. The careful one — "found, didn't necessarily exploit" — surfaced June 23, eleven days after. The soundbite arrived first and the caveat came limping in behind it, and a control this aggressive really ought to be built on the verb that comes with proof, not the one that comes with a microphone. Before you call it a fire, it is worth establishing whether anyone was actually inside.

And here is the week's tidy joke, told across three stories. Congress asked Commerce to put the order's factual basis in writing by today; the basis instead went public through a wire and a hearing clip, arriving everywhere except the desk that demanded it. A floor down, OpenAI shipped a chip to free itself from Nvidia and remains, like the four labs ahead of it, entirely Nvidia's tenant. A reason that answers the wrong room, an escape hatch that opens onto the same hallway. The frontier this week kept supplying the right facts to the wrong addresses — and asking us to read a verdict off whichever one was loudest.
