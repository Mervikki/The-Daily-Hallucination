Google Says Criminals Used an AI to Build a Zero-Day — and the Exploit Came With a Hallucinated Severity Score Baked In

By The Editor, Security Desk · May 13, 2026

Hype●●●○○
Impact●●●●○

Google's Threat Intelligence Group said this week it has "high confidence" it recorded the first known instance of a criminal threat actor using an AI model to discover and weaponize a previously unknown vulnerability — a zero-day, implemented as a Python script that bypasses two-factor authentication on a widely deployed open-source web administration tool. The actor, per Google, was assembling it for a "mass exploitation event." Google's own monitoring spotted the campaign first; it says its "proactive counter discovery" likely kept the thing from ever going off.

The detail that earns this the lead is how Google knows a machine wrote it. The exploit script is, by their description, suspiciously well-behaved: lavish educational docstrings, a textbook-Pythonic structure that screams "trained on every tutorial ever indexed," and — the tell — a CVSS severity score that does not correspond to any real advisory because the model made it up. The attackers shipped a hallucination inside their attack. The same instinct that has chatbots inventing case law and fake citations now produces fake vulnerability metadata in live malware, which is either reassuring or deeply not, depending on whether you're the one running the script.

Set this beside yesterday's news — OpenAI's Daybreak, Anthropic's Glasswing, the whole "models that patch security holes faster than attackers can find them" pitch — and the timing is almost rude. The defensive arms race got its official launch event on Tuesday; the offensive side filed its first confirmed kill on Monday. Google is careful to say it doesn't believe Gemini was used and that state crews — a North Korean group testing thousands of exploits, Chinese actors "experimenting" — are further along than the criminals. The asymmetry the defenders keep waving away is no longer hypothetical. It's a Python file with a fake severity rating in the comments.

"The attackers shipped a hallucination inside their attack. The model that writes your exploit does not know which way 'defensive' points — and apparently does not know what a real CVSS score is either."

Every lab selling AI cyber-defense this week is also, by construction, demonstrating AI cyber-offense; the capability is one capability pointed in a chosen direction, and "we only point it at Firefox" remains a corporate policy rather than a property of the weights. Google catching this one early is genuinely good work. It is not a structural fix. There isn't one on offer.

Policy · EU

Brussels Says OpenAI Made a "Concrete Offer" of Cyber-Model Access; Anthropic Is Still on Meeting Four or Five

By The Editor, Brussels Desk · May 13, 2026

A European Commission spokesperson said Monday that OpenAI has put forward a concrete proposal to give EU institutions access to its security-tuned models — the Daybreak family, presumably — while talks with Anthropic, after "four or five meetings," have "not yet reached the stage" of discussing equivalent access. Anthropic, the lab that built its brand on being the responsible one, is somehow the lab that has not yet figured out how to hand a regulator a login. OpenAI, which spent the spring starting a consulting firm and an ad network, walked in with a deal.

There is a less flattering reading of OpenAI's eagerness — government access is also government dependency, and "the EU runs its vuln triage on our model" is a moat with a flag on it — and a less flattering reading of Anthropic's hesitation, which is that "responsible" can shade into "slow" right up to the point where the contract goes to the other guy. Either way: the company that talks most about AI safety governance is currently losing the AI safety governance contract on procedural grounds. Worth a meeting six.

Open Weights

Kimi K2.6, Qwen 3.6-27B and GLM-5.1 All Land in May — the Open Frontier Ships on Time, Two Points Behind

By The Editor, Open Models Desk · May 13, 2026

This month's open-weight crop: Moonshot's Kimi K2.6 (described, by people who'd know, as genuinely top-tier on coding), Alibaba's Qwen 3.6-27B at roughly 77% on SWE-bench Verified, and Zhipu's GLM-5.1 — all of them runnable, all of them licensed for use, all of them shipping like clockwork. DeepSeek V4-Pro still anchors the field around 80% SWE-bench Verified. The pattern from the last two issues holds with metronomic reliability: the open ecosystem trails the closed frontier by about two points on the benchmark you care about and about two minor version numbers on the one you don't, and the gap is not growing.

The thing that does not show up on a leaderboard, and the reason these releases matter more than the chart positions: you can download them, read the weights, run your own evals, and not phone an investor's data center to do it. After a week of consulting roll-ups and "concrete offers" of model access, the open-weight corner remains the one place where "access to the model" is a file path rather than a negotiation.

Personal Agents

Hermes Agent Hits #1 on OpenRouter at 224 Billion Tokens a Day — the Self-Hosted Underdog Ate OpenClaw's Lunch in Three Months

By The Editor, Agents Desk · May 13, 2026

Nous Research's Hermes Agent — the MIT-licensed, runs-on-your-own-box assistant with persistent SQLite memory and connectors for twenty-odd messaging platforms — topped OpenRouter's global daily app-and-agent rankings this week, reportedly processing about 224 billion tokens a day and pushing OpenClaw off the #1 spot just three months after launch. It's model-agnostic by design: point it at Claude Sonnet 4.6, DeepSeek V4, or a local open-weight model and it keeps your data on your machine while it does so.

The interesting part isn't the throughput number, which is the kind of figure that means whatever the leaderboard methodology wants it to mean. It's that the most-used agent on a major router is currently a self-hosted, bring-your-own-model project from a small lab, not a hosted product from one of the giants. The "personal agent" category was supposed to be won by whoever owned the model and the cloud. So far it's being won by the thing you can apt-install and forget about — which is a different and more durable kind of moat, the kind nobody can revoke in a pricing-page update.

"The agent that grows with you" is a tagline, and a slightly ominous one if you think about it for more than a second. But an agent whose memory lives in a file on your own disk is, structurally, the version of this future least likely to be turned into a subscription tier. Hold onto the file.

Anthropic · Verticals

Claude for Legal Launches, Claude for Office Goes GA, Claude for Outlook Hits Beta — Anthropic Is Quietly Becoming a Suite

By The Editor, Product Desk · May 13, 2026

Anthropic this week formally launched "Claude for Legal," pitched — by the legal-tech press, which is paid to be excited — as something that "may reshape" the sector. Alongside it: Claude for Word, Excel and PowerPoint went generally available, Claude for Outlook entered public beta on all paid plans, and the financial-services team shipped ten ready-to-run agent templates for things like building pitchbooks and closing the books. None of these is a model release. All of them are distribution.

This is the same move Microsoft made with Copilot and Google is making with Gemini — get the assistant inside the document the customer is already in — except Anthropic is doing it with someone else's documents, riding on top of Office and Outlook rather than owning the surface. It's a sensible play for a lab that can't ship a phone or an OS. It's also a reminder that the frontier-model business is increasingly a features-on-other-people's-software business, and "Claude for [Profession]" press releases are going to keep arriving on a weekly cadence until they stop being newsworthy. We're nearly there.

Google · Distribution

Six Days Before I/O, Google Is Racing to Bolt Gemini Into the Center of Android Before Apple's AI Reboot

By The Editor, Mountain View Desk · May 13, 2026

CNBC reports Google is in a sprint to make Gemini the connective tissue of Android — the thing that answers when you long-press, the layer Workspace and Maps and the rest route through — ahead of Apple's repeatedly-delayed Siri-and-friends overhaul. I/O lands May 19, and the leaks still can't agree on whether the headline is "Gemini 4.0" with native image and video or yet another 3.x dot-release; the fact that Google hasn't decided how big a number it can credibly print tells you most of what you need to know.

As noted in the last issue: Google doesn't need the best model on a benchmark, it needs the model that's already there. Bolting Gemini into a billion phones before Apple ships anything is the entire strategy, and Apple's habit of pre-announcing AI features by a year or more is, frankly, doing half of Google's work for it. The race here isn't accuracy. It's who's installed when the user reaches for one — and one of the two contestants is currently racing against a competitor that hasn't left the starting blocks.

The Glossary
Proactive Counter Discovery

Official definition: a security operation in which a defender identifies and disrupts a threat actor's tooling or infrastructure before that tooling is deployed against victims at scale. Real definition: "we were already watching them" — phrased as a strategy. It's the term you use when you spotted the AI-built zero-day in time, and it's a perfectly good thing to have done; it is also, structurally, indistinguishable from "we got lucky and our telemetry happened to be pointed the right way." A genuine capability when it works, a press-release euphemism when it's the only line of defense you can name, and — like "we made some adjustments" from issue No. 2 — a phrase doing the load-bearing work of an entire safety architecture.

Editor's Note

Three issues in, and the cyber-security beat has eaten the front page two days running — which, given that yesterday's lead was three labs racing to sell AI defense, was always going to happen the moment someone confirmed AI offense. The thing to hold in your head: the model that audits Firefox and the model that writes a 2FA-bypass zero-day are the same kind of object pointed in opposite directions, and the direction is a choice somebody makes, not a fact about the weights. Google catching this one early is real, and good, and not a fix. Nobody's selling a fix. Several people are selling the half of it that bills better.

Elsewhere it was a week of distribution wearing different hats: Anthropic becoming a suite one Office plugin at a time, Google bolting Gemini into Android before Apple wakes up, OpenAI handing Brussels a "concrete offer" while the responsible-AI lab is still on meeting four. And then Hermes — a small lab's self-hosted agent, top of the router, your memory in a file on your own disk — which is the one item all week that made me genuinely optimistic, precisely because it's the version of this nobody can quietly turn into a subscription tier.
