Google · Gemini

Google Keeps Cutting the Price of the Gemini It Has. The One You Want Is Still "Next Month."

By The Editor, Models Desk · May 24, 2026

At I/O on Tuesday, Google shipped Gemini 3.5 Flash — fast, cheap, priced at roughly $1.50 in and $9 out per million tokens — and, two days later, trimmed its API rates again to undercut everyone else on the low end. The flagship, 3.5 Pro, the model that would actually have to stand on a stage next to Claude Opus 4.7 and a forthcoming GPT-5.5, was described, as it has been described for weeks, as "in testing" and arriving "next month." It is now five days later. It is still next month. It has been next month for so long that one begins to suspect "next month" is not a forecast but a state of being.

The strategic read is not subtle. A company fights a price war on the tier it is confident in. Google is confident in Flash, so Flash gets cheaper by the week; the headlines write themselves and the developers come for the bargain. Pro stays in the drawer because shipping it means letting the world draw the one comparison Google would rather defer — the head-to-head on the expensive tier, where the question is not "how cheap" but "how good," and where, on present evidence, the answer is not yet one Google wants printed. Discounting the model you have is a perfectly good way to avoid discussing the model you don't.

There is nothing dishonest in any of it. The Flash is real, the price cut is real, the "next month" is offered in apparent good faith by people who presumably believe it. But a release date that never has to arrive in order to keep doing its job is a strange instrument, and this one has been doing its job — occupying the slot where a frontier model is supposed to go, keeping the leaderboard conversation open, holding a place at the table — without ever showing up to sit down.

Infrastructure

A Fifteen-Year Lease, for Models That Don't Survive a Quarter

By The Editor, Infrastructure Desk · May 24, 2026

Applied Digital said this week — per its own filing — that it has signed a fifteen-year take-or-pay lease with an "investment-grade hyperscaler" it declined to name, to fill roughly 300 megawatts at its Polaris Forge 3 site in North Dakota. The base value runs about $7.5 billion; with the options attached, the company says it could reach $18.2 billion. The deal pushes its contracted capacity past a gigawatt. "Take-or-pay" is the load-bearing phrase: the tenant pays whether or not it uses the power, which is how you finance a building before you are entirely sure what will run inside it.

Hold the term of the lease against the lifespan of the things it will house. Fifteen years is longer than the half-life of any model name we have ever printed; yesterday we buried two snapshots that lasted a few months. The compute under that roof will be replaced several times over before the ink is dry, and the models it serves will be deprecated, succeeded, and forgotten on a cadence measured in seasons. The industry's product turns over quarterly. The industry's debts are being written in fifteen-year ink.

What "up to" is doing in that sentence

The gap between the number signed and the number advertised — roughly $10.7 billion of "if everything goes the way we hope."

The Glossary

Unlimited (adj.)

As used in: consumer & developer AI subscriptions

Official definition: without limit; a plan tier marketed as imposing no cap on usage. Real definition: the price you were paying before the meter was announced. As of June 15, "unlimited" Claude subscriptions begin drawing agent usage from a finite pool of credits billed at API rates — roughly $20, $100, or $200 a month depending on tier — which is a number, and a number is the precise thing an unlimited plan was understood not to have. The word survives on the pricing page. The condition does not survive the changelog. Unlimited, in the wild, has always meant "limited in ways we have not yet found a polite occasion to mention."

Editor's Note

Three of the four things on this page are promises, and the fourth is a fifteen-year lease, which is just a promise with a penalty clause. Google's best model is next month. Anthropic's "unlimited" becomes limited next month. OpenAI's prospectus surfaces some month. The genuinely shipped artifacts this week were a price cut and a signed real-estate deal — the housekeeping — while every item with a pulse was something that hasn't happened yet. This industry's calendar is not a schedule. It is a stack of IOUs denominated in features.

I want to be fair about this, because none of it is fraud. The cheap Gemini works and it's getting cheaper. The lease is real and the power is being built. The meter was announced honestly, weeks in advance. The trouble isn't deceit; it's where the excitement lives. The part you can touch is always the boring part — the discount, the megawatts, the terms — and the part that's supposed to thrill you is always the part still described in the future tense. We are sold the not-yet and handed the already-here, and we have somehow agreed to find that arrangement normal.

A dead Sunday is good for exactly one thing, which is that when nothing ships, you can finally see the machinery that sells the things that haven't. The single most reliable release date in artificial intelligence is "next month," precisely because next month never has to arrive in order to keep working. Ours, at least, does. See you tomorrow.
