Anthropic · Billing Desk

The Toll Booth That Went Up Monday Morning and Was Gone by Monday Night

By The Editor, Billing Desk · June 17, 2026

For most of a year this paper has tracked one looming line item: Anthropic's plan to pull programmatic Claude — the Agent SDK, claude -p, Claude Code GitHub Actions, third-party agent apps — off the flat Pro and Max subscriptions and onto a separate metered credit pool, $20, $100, or $200 a month, billed at full API list rates, no rollover, hard stop. On Monday we reported that the meter had finally gone live. We need to amend that. By the end of that same Monday, Anthropic had paused it.

"We're pausing the changes to Claude Agent SDK usage described below," the company wrote, adding that it would rework the plan "to better support how users build with Claude subscriptions" and give advance notice before trying again. The practical effect: those surfaces keep drawing from subscriptions exactly as before, there is no credit to claim, and nothing about your bill changed. So let me grade my own paper plainly — we told you the meter went live; the agent-billing half of it was switched back off within hours. (The model retirements riding the same June 15 schedule, Opus 4 and Sonnet 4, appear to be a separate matter and seem to have stuck.)

The reversal followed a loud developer revolt. Community estimates of the effective price change ran anywhere from 12× to 175× depending on how heavily you leaned on agents, and the timing could not have been worse: OpenAI's "switch to Codex" promotion runs through June 22, which means every furious thread came with a posted off-ramp. One outlet read the climbdown as a move in a price war Anthropic cannot afford to start losing on the surface developers love most. That reading is hard to argue with.

Here is what I keep coming back to. The meter was "imminent" so many issues running it became a recurring joke in this column — a feature perpetually a Monday away. It finally arrived, and it lasted an afternoon. A toll that is dismantled the same day it is installed, the moment the people paying it object, was never really a price. It was a question the company asked its users, with a billing date attached, to find out how much they would tolerate. They answered. The change isn't dead, mind you — it's "paused," with "advance notice" to come. The pricing was a trial balloon; the next one will simply fly higher and earlier.

Anthropic spent a year telling developers the meter was coming. It arrived Monday and was gone by Monday night. The toll wasn't a price. It was a poll. — On the paused Agent SDK credit change

Anthropic · Washington Desk

Claude's Best Models Reach Day Five in the Dark — and the Betting Windows Are Open

By The Editor, Washington Desk · June 17, 2026

Five days after Commerce's Friday-evening order pulled Claude Fable 5 and Mythos 5 offline, both remain dark for every user. Anthropic met administration officials in Washington on Monday; per people close to the talks, relayed by CNBC and others, the meeting produced no breakthrough, and the two sides are "not yet discussing lifting the restrictions." An official has previously said the models stay down until the government's "national-security apparatus is hardened" — possibly a matter of weeks.

What's new is who else is now in the conversation: the gamblers. Prediction markets have begun trading the restoration date as if it were a product launch. Per Kalshi prices reported by CNBC on Monday, the odds that US access returns before July 1 sit at 58%, rising to 74% by July 10.

The market's odds Claude's models come back

Kalshi prices via CNBC, June 16. These are bets, not forecasts — and the same markets quoted different numbers (68% before July 1) days earlier. The shape is the point: a national-security order with 74% odds of lifting inside three weeks is being priced like a delayed launch.

Last week this column argued that an export control you can dissolve with a software patch was never a verdict on a model — it was a deadline. That was an inference. Now there's a market agreeing in numbers. You do not price a permanent ban at 74%-gone-by-the-tenth. The gamblers have read the same tea leaves we did and concluded the same thing: this is a product on hold, with a probable ship date, not a thing the state has decided should cease to exist.

None of which gets the models back online today, and the people in the room are pointedly not talking about that yet. Editor's Note

Two reversals sat on Anthropic's desk this week. One took an afternoon. The other is on day five and counting.

The meter was the one the company controlled completely. It was Anthropic's own pricing change, on Anthropic's own products, decided in Anthropic's own conference rooms — and when it met a wall of furious developers with a competitor's promo code in hand, it was gone by nightfall. That is how fast a firm moves when the lever is in its own grip and the cost of holding the line is its customers walking out the door.

The models are the reversal Anthropic wants and cannot have. Fable 5 and Mythos 5 are exactly as much of an unforced error in the company's eyes, exactly as worth undoing — and they are still dark, because this time the lever is in someone else's hand. Same company, same week, same instinct to take back a thing that's costing it. The only variable is who is holding the switch. When you own the clock, a mistake costs you an afternoon. When the government owns it, "we're working to restore access" is a sentence that ages in days.

So when Fable 5 comes back — and the betting windows say probably within three weeks — set two numbers next to each other: how long the shutdown ran, and how long the meter ran. An afternoon against however many days this turns out to be. That gap is the most honest measure we'll get this year of how much of its own fate a frontier lab actually holds. Anthropic could set the price of one thing and couldn't set the date of the other. That isn't a grievance. It's just the shape of the thing once you've grown big enough to be worth switching off.

See you tomorrow.
