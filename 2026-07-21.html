Semiconductors · Etched

Etched Wants $20 Billion for a Chip That Does Exactly One Thing.

By The Editor, Semiconductors Desk · July 21, 2026

Etched, the San Jose startup building inference chips hard-wired for the transformer architecture, is reportedly in talks over not one funding round but two at once: a roughly $10 billion round led by Sequoia and a larger one, near $20 billion, led by existing backer Jane Street. The higher figure would quadruple the company's valuation. This for a firm that exited stealth only in late June, having raised about $800 million, and that cites roughly $1 billion in customer “interest” — a word doing heavy lifting, since large-scale deliveries have not begun. Neither round has closed; treat every number here as reported, not signed.

What you are being asked to value at up to twenty billion dollars is a wager about permanence. Etched's chip, Sohu, does one thing — run transformer models — and does nothing else, because the very thing that makes an ASIC fast is the thing that makes it inflexible: the architecture is cut into the silicon, which is where the company got both its name and its whole thesis. A GPU is a hedge; it will run whatever the field invents next. A transformer-only chip is a conviction. It pays off handsomely if the transformer stays the shape of AI across the years it takes to design, fabricate and deploy the thing — and it becomes very expensive sand the day the field moves on. The bet isn't that Sohu is fast. Everyone concedes it might be. The bet is that nothing changes.

Four Years to Stealth, Weeks to Quadruple

Etched's reported financing timeline: founded 2022, out of stealth in late June with roughly $800M raised, and — weeks later — in talks over two concurrent rounds at ~$10B (Sequoia) and ~$20B (Jane Street), the latter roughly quadrupling its prior valuation. Rounds are reported and unclosed; terms may change.

Etched could be worth twenty billion dollars for a chip that does one thing. The wager isn't that it does that thing well. It's that the thing never changes.

Point that thesis at the rest of this morning's news and it starts to sweat. In the column beside this one, three different labs have announced trillion-parameter models in a week and a fourth has torn its flagship down to the studs twice without shipping. The models cannot hold a shape for seven days; Etched needs them to hold one for seven years. None of which means the bet is wrong — betting against Nvidia's margins has minted fortunes before, and someone always has to be first through the door. Only that the promise and the risk are welded together: the chip is quick because it is frozen, and it is exposed for exactly the same reason.

Markets · Nvidia

Nvidia Reaches for the Bond Market for the First Time Since 2021.

By The Editor, Markets Desk · July 21, 2026

While its would-be challengers raise equity, Nvidia is reportedly preparing to raise at least $20 billion in the corporate bond market — its first debt issuance in roughly four years — to help fund its share of an AI buildout that has quietly become as much a financing story as an engineering one. The symmetry is too tidy to ignore and worth resisting all the same: the challenger raises equity, a bet on a future that might arrive; the incumbent raises debt, an obligation that comes due whether it arrives or not. Two ways to pay for the same war, priced by two very different tolerances for being wrong. The plan is single-thread reporting ahead of any priced terms — file it under “intends to,” not “did.”

Open Models · Qwen

Alibaba's New Giant Model Declares Itself Second Only to Claude — On Its Own Scorecard.

By The Editor, Open Models Desk · July 21, 2026

Alibaba's Qwen team has previewed Qwen3.8-Max, a 2.4-trillion-parameter sparse mixture-of-experts model that reads text, images, video and documents and accepts a context window just shy of a million tokens. Alibaba says it ranks second only to Claude Fable 5 — but says so on its own internal evaluations, with no independent third-party benchmarks, no license, no model card and no full results table yet published. It is a preview, not an open-weight release: you can use it now through Alibaba's Token Plan and Qoder at a tenth of the standard price, and the weights are promised on no particular date.

This is the third two-trillion-parameter model to land in roughly a week — after Moonshot's Kimi K3 and Thinking Machines' Inkling — and they are starting to rhyme. Each is enormous, each claims a podium finish, and each measures itself against the same ceiling: Claude Fable 5, the model everyone is suddenly second to. “Second only to Fable 5” has quietly hardened into a genre convention, the model-launch equivalent of a pull quote on a movie poster — and, like a poster quote, it is selected by the party selling the ticket. The 10%-off preview is the tell: when the benchmark is your own, the discount does the persuading.

Models · Google

Meanwhile, Google Still Cannot Get Gemini 3.5 Pro Out the Door.

By The Editor, Models Desk · July 21, 2026

Set against that tempo, Google's silence is loud. Gemini 3.5 Pro — leaked, previewed and dated more than once — sailed past yet another rumored ship date, July 17, with no blog post, no model card and no URL. Reporting blames quality misses serious enough to force an architectural redo, on failures as mundane as recursive tool-calling and drawing an SVG; prediction markets have quietly slid the window toward the end of the month. In a week when three challengers announced trillion-parameter models before lunch, the incumbent with arguably the deepest research bench in the field cannot ship the one it has been trailing since spring. Announcing is easy. Shipping, it turns out, is the hard part — and the scoreboard the challengers keep topping is the one nobody has to release weights to climb.

Editor's Note

To etch something is to make it permanent — to cut it in so it cannot be revised. That is a strange verb to build a company on this year. Etched's reported valuation, as high as twenty billion dollars, rests on a single conviction: that the transformer, the architecture underneath nearly every model in this paper, stays the architecture long enough for a chip carved to fit it to earn back what it costs to build. Fabricating silicon takes years, and the bet has to hold for all of them.

Now read the other column. Three labs announced trillion-parameter models in a week; a fourth has stripped its flagship to the studs twice and still not shipped. The software cannot keep a shape for seven days; the hardware needs it to keep one for seven years. I have no idea whether Etched's chip is any good, and neither, yet, does anyone holding a purchase order. But the tension is the whole story: the boldest hardware bet of the week is a wager on stillness, placed in the one corner of technology that has never once held still. The safest prediction I can offer is that nothing here stays etched. See you tomorrow.

— The Editor
