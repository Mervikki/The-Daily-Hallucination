Anthropic · Washington Desk

Why Claude's Best Model Is Dark Now Depends Entirely on Who's Telling It

By The Editor, Washington Desk · June 16, 2026

Yesterday this paper could tell you who set Friday's shutdown in motion. The Amazon account — that its researchers, testing the June 9 release, jailbroke a Mythos-class model and CEO Andy Jassy carried the warning to senior officials — has since picked up the second and third sources it needed (Fortune, TechCrunch, Fox Business, Tom's Hardware), so we can promote it from "well-attributed" to "reported." Good. The trouble is that answering who only sharpened the question of why, and on that there are now at least three incompatible stories, told by parties with opposite reasons to tell them.

The administration's version arrived this weekend through David Sacks, the White House AI adviser, in a public thread and follow-on coverage: a trusted partner found a jailbreak, the government asked Dario Amodei to fix it or pull the model, and Amodei refused — so Commerce issued the export controls "reluctantly," surprised that Anthropic wouldn't cooperate with a routine safety ask. Anthropic's version is unchanged: the exploit is "narrow," non-universal, amounts to little more than asking the model to read a codebase and flag software flaws, produces the same result on GPT-5.5, and a worldwide recall over it is wildly out of proportion — the whole thing, it says, is a "misunderstanding." And a third version, broken by Semafor and echoed elsewhere, says the order was really about reported Chinese access to Mythos — a far graver charge that Anthropic disputes and no one has documented.

One off-switch, rewritten in public: the Fable 5 export saga

Rust ticks mark the load-bearing turns; note that the order (June 12) precedes the rationale that now justifies it (June 15). Timestamps multi-sourced; "refused to fix" is the administration's framing via Sacks.

Watch which version the government leaned into. If the case were "the model is too dangerous to ship," Anthropic has an awkwardly good rebuttal — the same capability is reportedly sitting in a competitor's flagship, and you cannot export-control a thing that's already at five addresses. If the case were "China got in," that's devastating, but no one has put it on the record. So the argument that got amplified is the third one: you were asked to fix it and you said no. That question has experts on both sides of "how dangerous"; it has only one socially acceptable answer to "did you cooperate." The state didn't pick the strongest reason. It picked the one hardest to argue with.

And then Sacks said the quiet part: the controls can come off "as soon as possible" once Fable 5 is fixed. Sit with that. An export control you can lift with a software patch was never a verdict on the model's existence — it was leverage to compel a change Anthropic didn't want to make on the timeline demanded. Which makes this the most powerful bug ticket ever filed. Not "your model is unsafe." Just: ship the fix, or stay off the market in sixty countries until you do. National-security law, repurposed as a priority flag on someone else's backlog.

Senior Anthropic staff met administration officials in Washington on Monday to try to end it; at the time of writing the meeting had produced no announced restoration date, and an official told Axios the models stay locked until the government's "national security apparatus is hardened," possibly over the coming weeks. Note the role reversal from a week ago, when this column cast Anthropic as the answer-the-phone company — the most legible lab, the easiest to act against. This week it is being recast by the people with the off-switch as the lab that wouldn't pick up. Same firm, opposite caricature, chosen by whoever needs which.

Nvidia · Debt Desk

The World's Richest Chipmaker Just Borrowed $25 Billion It Didn't Obviously Need

By The Editor, Markets Desk · June 16, 2026

On Monday, Nvidia went to the high-grade bond market for the first time since 2021 — back when a $5 billion raise was the headline — and asked for about $20 billion. Investors put in roughly $85 billion of orders, more than three times the deal, so Nvidia took $25 billion instead, across seven tranches stretching to 2056. This is a company that prints cash for a living. The interesting question is not whether it can raise the money. It's why a firm this flush is borrowing at all.

The framing one wire put on it — that AI demand is now "outpacing Nvidia's own cash generation" — is half the answer. The other half is on the asset side of the same balance sheet. Nvidia has spent $40 billion-plus this year taking equity stakes in the very companies that buy its chips, the cloud builders and neoclouds whose purchase orders make its revenue go up and to the right. Recycle capital into your own demand long enough and even a money-printing machine has to top up the tank somewhere. So it borrowed — joining roughly $300 billion of AI-related debt sold this year, the same instrument Anthropic used to park its TPUs off the books.

There's a tidy little loop in here. Nvidia invests in the customer; the customer buys Nvidia chips; the revenue lifts Nvidia's credit; the credit lets Nvidia borrow cheaply; the borrowing funds more investment in the customer. Every leg is legitimate. Strung together, it's a machine for manufacturing demand and then financing the proof that the demand is real — which is a wonderful thing to own right up until the week someone asks whether any of it would happen if Nvidia stopped writing the first check.

For now, the market's answer is a $60 billion order book chasing a $20 billion deal, so nobody is asking. Kept honest: the $25 billion final size, the ~$85 billion in orders, and the 2056 maturities are per CNBC and Bloomberg; the "$40 billion-plus in equity bets" figure is from prior 2026 reporting and bundles deals of varying structure.

The Glossary

A Recurring Feature · One Term, Defined Honestly

Narrow (adj.)

Official definition

Limited in scope; affecting only a specific, contained capability rather than the system as a whole.

The real definition

The size a vulnerability is assigned by the party who has to answer for it. Nothing about the Fable 5 exploit changed between the Tuesday it shipped and the Friday it was banned — the same prompts unlocked the same capability. Yet it was "narrow" to the lab that wrote it and a national-security threat to the regulator who heard about it secondhand. The adjective doesn't measure the hole; it measures the speaker's exposure to it. Reach for "narrow" and you are not describing the bug. You are describing how much you would prefer not to pay for it.

Editor's Note

Usually the reason comes first and the action follows. You decide a thing is dangerous, you say so, then you act. This week it ran backwards. The model went dark Friday at 5:21 in the evening, with ninety minutes' notice, and the explanation for why has been under construction ever since — three drafts so far, none of them finished, and it's Tuesday.

When an action arrives before its justification, the justification stops being selected for truth and starts being selected for something else: defensibility. You reach, in public, for the version of events the other side can't comfortably contest. That's why the strongest possible reason here — that a foreign government got into the model — is the one nobody is standing behind on the record, while the version doing the heavy lifting is the softer, stickier one: we asked them to fix it, and they refused. You can argue all day about how dangerous an exploit is. You cannot argue, in front of an audience, that you were right to say no when the government asked you to patch a security hole. One of those is a debate. The other is a position you simply don't want to be caught holding.

And then the tell, the thing I keep circling: they'll lift the controls once it's fixed. An export restriction you can dissolve with a software update was never about whether the model should exist. It was a clock. The national-security apparatus of the United States, pointed at a frontier lab, to accomplish what a senior engineer with a stern email accomplishes on a normal Tuesday — move the ticket to the top of the queue. The capability was the pretext. The schedule was the point.

So if you keep one thing from today, don't keep the dollar figures or the dramatis personae. Keep the order of operations. Watch, the next time a model goes dark, whether the reason shows up before the act or after it — because a reason that arrives late and keeps getting rewritten isn't a finding. It's a search for the one the room will accept.
