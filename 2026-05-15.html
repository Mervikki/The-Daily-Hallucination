Anthropic · Pricing

Anthropic Puts Claude Agents on a Meter — and Picks the Worst Possible Day to Do It

By The Editor, Pricing Desk · May 15, 2026

Hype●●●●○
Impact●●●●●

Anthropic announced yesterday that, starting June 15, anything that talks to Claude through code — the Agent SDK, GitHub Actions, Claude Code in headless mode, Hermes, OpenClaw, every third-party agent harness on the planet — will be carved out of the chat subscription it's been quietly riding on and billed through a separate monthly credit pool at API-style rates. Pro subscribers get $20 in agent credits a month. Max 5x gets $100. Max 20x gets $200. Credits do not roll over. Overage bills at standard API prices. The chat box still works as it did. The terminal does not.

The framing in Anthropic's announcement is that this is a "fair" allocation, separating "interactive" use from "programmatic" use so heavy agent operators stop subsidizing themselves at light users' expense. The framing in Claude Code product manager Noah Zweben's mentions is mostly people calling him a liar. The complaint is consistent and not unreasonable: users signed up for "$200 a month, run Claude as hard as you want" plans, built workflows on that promise, and are now being told the workflow they built is the part that doesn't count. The plans were always going to be repriced. Subsidised compute is not a stable category. The question is whether the on-ramp was a bait-and-switch or a bug, and the answer from the people who built on it is: pick one, they both hurt the same.

The timing is the part that earns this the lead. Within roughly six hours of the announcement: Sam Altman offered new business customers two free months of OpenAI Codex; xAI shipped Grok Build, an eight-parallel-agent CLI explicitly pitched as a Claude Code replacement, at an introductory $99 a month; and a separate scoop landed reporting that Microsoft is cancelling most of its internal Claude Code licenses by June 30. Anthropic's bet is that nobody who pays for Claude Code can plausibly leave Claude Code, because the model is genuinely better at the work. That is probably true for the next quarter. It is the kind of true that depends entirely on the next quarter's model release from the people who watched yesterday very carefully.

"Subsidised compute is not a stable category. The question is whether the on-ramp was a bait-and-switch or a bug, and the answer from the people who built on it is: pick one, they both hurt the same."

The honest version of the announcement reads roughly: we sold seats, you used them as if they were seats, this turned out to be financially insane for us, and so we are now selling something that more closely resembles what we should have sold in the first place. That sentence would have done less damage than the one Anthropic chose. "Fair allocation" is the kind of phrase you reach for when the actual answer is "we lost money on every Max 20x subscription with a serious developer attached to it," and the people on the receiving end of fair allocation can tell.

Power-user monthly cost — pick a tier, any tier

Headline subscription prices for the tiers being actively cross-shopped today. Anthropic's Max 20x and xAI's SuperGrok Heavy converged on roughly the same number. The difference, after June 15, is what happens when you actually use them through a terminal.

Microsoft · Claude

Microsoft Cancels Most Internal Claude Code Licenses — "Unified Telemetry" Cited, Awkwardness Implied

By The Editor, Redmond Desk · May 15, 2026

The Verge's Tom Warren and The Information reported yesterday that Microsoft is sunsetting most of its internal Claude Code licenses by June 30 — the end of its fiscal year — and steering engineers across Windows, Microsoft 365, Outlook, Teams and Surface back to GitHub Copilot CLI. The publicly stated reasons are the usual catechism: tighter control, consistent tooling, "unified telemetry," and alignment with the commercial Copilot narrative the company has spent two years trying to get customers to take seriously. The unpublic reason is that Microsoft's engineers reportedly preferred Claude, which is roughly the kind of preference an employer is permitted to find inconvenient.

Read this next to the pricing story above it. On a Wednesday, Anthropic told its biggest non-employee customers their workflows were now on a meter. On a Thursday, its largest commercial competitor's parent company told its engineers to stop using Claude entirely. Anthropic will frame this as a corporate-policy decision unrelated to its product. It is also a corporate-policy decision that was made easier by the product changing on Wednesday.

"Unified telemetry" is the corporate-IT version of "fair allocation": a phrase that is technically accurate and load-bearing in exactly one direction.

Anthropic · Distribution

PwC Will Certify 30,000 Consultants on Claude — Anthropic's Other Tuesday, the One With the Good Press Release

By The Editor, Enterprise Desk · May 15, 2026

PwC and Anthropic on the same day expanded their alliance to train and certify 30,000 U.S. consultants on Claude Code and Claude Cowork, stand up a joint Center of Excellence, and seed a Claude-native finance practice inside PwC's Office of the CFO group, with a stated path toward PwC's 364,000-person global workforce in 136 countries. The pitch is the usual one — agentic builds for clients, AI-native dealmaking, "reinventing" finance and supply chain and HR — wrapped around real-customer numbers PwC is willing to put in a press release: insurance underwriting cycles from ten weeks to ten days, incident response from hours to minutes.

This is the Anthropic that gets the trillion-dollar valuation. Not the one whose Twitter mentions are on fire about credit pools — the one that ships a quietly important enterprise-distribution deal on a Thursday afternoon and signs up roughly an army to deploy it. The two Anthropics are the same company, on the same day, with the same product, talking to two different audiences. They are not yet a contradiction. They are getting closer.

xAI · Tools

Grok Build Arrives With Eight Parallel Agents and the Timing of a Vulture — Musk's First Real Claude Code Challenger

By The Editor, Coding Desk · May 15, 2026

xAI yesterday shipped Grok Build, a terminal-native coding agent for its SuperGrok Heavy subscribers and a new "Grok SuperHeavy" tier ($299/month list, $99 intro for six months). The architectural pitch is the one Claude Code does not currently make: eight concurrent agents that plan, search docs and write code in parallel, plus a "plan mode" approval workflow that asks before it acts, local execution that keeps code off cloud servers, and support for AGENTS.md, plugins, hooks, skills and MCP. The underlying model — grok-code-fast-1, 256K context, "16-agent Heavy" — reportedly hits 70.8% on SWE-bench Verified.

Set aside, for a moment, Musk's evening Twitter habits and the question of whether anyone outside a quote-tweet replies to a Grok output. The product is plausible. It costs less than Claude Max 20x for the introductory six months, it ships into the exact week Anthropic decided to renegotiate every power user's deal, and the SWE-bench number is two points below DeepSeek V4-Pro and roughly fifteen below Claude Opus 4.7 — which is to say within range, which is to say competitive enough to be a threat to anyone whose loyalty is not already infinite. The xAI playbook here is unmistakable: ship something good enough fast enough that the unhappy users on the other side of the fence have somewhere to walk to. Yesterday they did.

OpenAI · Apple

OpenAI Lawyers Up Against Apple — Says ChatGPT-in-Siri Was "Buried," Revenue "Nowhere Close" to Promises

By The Editor, Partnerships Desk · May 15, 2026

Bloomberg first reported yesterday that OpenAI has retained an outside law firm and is exploring options up to and including a formal breach-of-contract notice against Apple over the ChatGPT-in-Siri integration. The complaint, per the reporting: the integration is hard to find, defaults to off, and revenue from subscriptions converted through Siri is "nowhere close" to the billions OpenAI told its investors it would book. Apple's reported counter, through people who'd know: OpenAI is making hardware with the man who used to design Apple's, and Apple is not enthusiastic about being a launch partner for its replacement.

The recurring shape of OpenAI's last twelve months is that every major partner ends up annoyed: Microsoft over compute share, Disney over Sora training data, now Apple over Siri placement. The thing the company has consistently been good at is selling the deal. The thing it has consistently been worse at is being the partner once the deal closes. There is a version of this story where Apple is the unreasonable party, hoarding the surface area its own users want filled, and that version is genuinely plausible. There is another version where every OpenAI partnership ends in litigation because the contracts assume a level of ChatGPT pull-through that ChatGPT has not, in fact, demonstrated. Both versions can be true, and currently are.

WWDC is on June 8. If Apple announces a Gemini-or-Claude alternative inside Siri Extensions before OpenAI files anything, the suit becomes very expensive press for both sides and a procurement decision for everyone watching.

Markets · AI Iron

Cisco Books $9B in AI Orders and Fires 4,000; Cerebras IPOs Up 68% — The AI-Iron Trade Is the Only Trade

By The Editor, Markets Desk · May 15, 2026

Cisco posted Q3 earnings Wednesday after the bell and the stock had its best single session since 2011 — a roughly 20% pop — on the back of $9 billion in raised full-year AI-infrastructure order guidance (from $5B), $5.3 billion already booked year-to-date, and a CEO line about a "networking supercycle" that did the work it was supposed to do. The company is also cutting fewer than 4,000 jobs, starting yesterday. The two facts are the same fact. Cisco is reassembling itself into the AI infrastructure company the AI infrastructure boom requires and pricing accordingly.

Cerebras, meanwhile, priced its IPO above its own raised range at $185, opened at $350, and closed up 68% at $311 — a roughly $95 billion fully diluted valuation for a wafer-scale-chip company whose business model is "be a non-NVIDIA option for people who can't get NVIDIA." This is what the AI-iron trade looks like when there is no other trade in the room: a 41-year-old networking company has its biggest day in fifteen years, and a chip startup goes public at a number larger than most of the S&P 500.

Editor's Note

Five issues in, and the pattern keeps refusing to be one thing. Yesterday Anthropic signed PwC up to put Claude in front of 30,000 consultants and started a fight with every developer who pays it directly. Both of these things happened on a Thursday, three blocks apart, presumably with the same product roadmap printed out on the same conference-room wall. The enterprise side of the company is having a tremendous quarter. The terminal side of the company is having a public, ongoing argument with the people who taught their employer to buy a Claude license in the first place.

If you want the cleanest read of the day, it is this: subsidized "use it like you mean it" AI subscriptions never made financial sense and were never going to last, and the labs are now in the awkward middle period of saying so out loud, one tier-restructure at a time. Anthropic went first because it had the most to reprice. OpenAI was waiting at the door with a coupon. xAI was waiting at the door with a product. Microsoft, which sells none of those subscriptions and resells a competing one, helpfully picked yesterday to remind its engineers which side of the org chart they work for. None of these are coincidences. All of them are choices. None of the choices were made by a model.

Google I/O is Tuesday. WWDC is June 8. The next thing to watch isn't a model number — it's whether anyone selling a flat-rate AI plan in June still is by August. See you tomorrow.
