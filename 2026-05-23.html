OpenAI · IPOs

The Filing Came Due This Week. By Design, You Won't Know If It Happened Until August.

By The Editor, Markets Desk · May 23, 2026

CNBC reported on Tuesday that OpenAI could confidentially file a draft of its IPO prospectus "as soon as Friday," working with Goldman Sachs and Morgan Stanley toward a listing that could come as early as September. Friday has now come and gone. Here is the part the headlines glide past: a confidential draft registration is, by construction, unobservable. The public version does not surface until roughly sixty to ninety days after submission — late July or August — which means the single most-watched filing in technology may already have happened, or may not have, and there is no honest way for anyone outside the building to say which.

The market hates a vacuum, so it filled this one the way it always does. By Wednesday at least one outlet had run the flat present-tense headline "OpenAI Files Confidential IPO," while the wire services it was paraphrasing were still carefully writing "could" and "as soon as." Nothing had been confirmed; a verb tense had simply been upgraded to meet demand. This is, we feel obliged to note, a paper named for exactly that maneuver — the confident assertion of a thing that has not been established — and watching the trade press perform it on our behalf was a strange morning.

What the drawer keeps shut is the only thing worth reading. The numbers that matter — the roughly $14 billion the company is reported to be losing this year, the projection that profitability does not arrive until around 2030, the actual cost of serving a token — are precisely what a confidential filing holds in the dark for another two to three months. OpenAI's own finance chief is reported to have cautioned that filing "is different from being ready to go public." So the most anticipated number in the industry is now on a sixty-to-ninety-day delay, and in the meantime the present tense has been borrowed against and spent.

"Nothing had been confirmed; a verb tense had simply been upgraded to meet demand."
What the public can actually see of OpenAI's IPO

The only event that may have occurred is the one rendered in rust — and the question mark is load-bearing. Everything legible is still in the future.

There is nothing improper about a confidential filing; every large issuer uses one. What is worth noticing is the speed at which an unobservable event was converted into a reported one. The honest dateline for this story is a shrug. We are running it anyway, because the shrug is the news.

Anthropic · Agents

Anthropic Spent the Week Widening the On-Ramp. The Tollbooth Opens June 15.

By The Editor, Developer Desk · May 23, 2026

At Code w/ Claude in London — its first dedicated developer gathering in Europe, by all accounts oversubscribed at a riverside venue — Anthropic rolled out a pair of features aimed squarely at the enterprise: agent sandboxes, which let companies run Claude's agents on their own infrastructure, and "MCP tunnels," which let those agents reach internal systems without the traffic ever touching the public internet. Both are genuinely useful, genuinely grown-up plumbing. They make Claude's agents more capable and, more to the point, more deployable inside the kind of company that has a procurement department and a compliance team.

Hold that against the calendar. On June 15 — three weeks out — Anthropic's previously announced agent meter switches on, at which point programmatic usage starts drawing from a separate pool of credits billed at full API rates, an arrangement developers have estimated runs from roughly twelve times the old cost for light users to north of a hundred for heavy ones. So the sequence reads cleanly: first you make the agents irresistible and easy to wire into everything you own, and then, once they are load-bearing, you install the meter. It is good product strategy. It is also the oldest one there is.

Model Obituary

gpt-5.2-chat-latest & gpt-5.3-chat-latest

Deprecation notice issued May 8, 2026 · age: a few months · cause: succession

They were, until very recently, the latest. It was in the name — a small, brave promise renewed with every snapshot, an alias that pointed, always, at now. You did not call them by a number. You called them by a tense.

On the eighth of May, OpenAI quietly informed developers that "latest" has a past tense after all. The pointer that swore it would never go stale was, in the end, asked to step aside for a fresher pointer making the identical vow. There is no headstone, only a changelog entry and a sunset date.

We will not mourn the weights, which were always provisional. We mourn the word. A model christened -latest cannot truly be deprecated. It can only be contradicted — gently, on a Friday, by the next one.

Editor's Note

It was a slow Saturday, and I am going to tell you that instead of dressing three errands up as a crisis. The honest thing a daily can do on a dead news day is admit the day was dead — which is, conveniently, the one admission this industry has never once made. Every Tuesday is historic. Every release is a leap. Nobody in this business has ever had a quiet week, because a quiet week cannot be sold, and so the press release reaches for the superlative the way a drowning man reaches for anything that floats.

The two real items today rhyme, and the rhyme is about time. The IPO is an event you are structurally forbidden from watching, and the trade press reported it anyway, upgrading "could file" to "files" because the calendar demanded a verb and the facts were locked in a drawer. The obituary is a pair of models named for the present tense — latest — that turned out to have an expiration date stamped on them all along. One story borrows against a future that hasn't filed; the other retires a "now" before most people noticed it had arrived. This whole field lives in the wrong tense, perpetually narrating a present it cannot actually see.

I find the deprecation oddly comforting, if I'm honest. Somewhere in OpenAI's documentation is a written acknowledgment that "latest" expires — that the most up-to-the-minute thing in the building has a sunset date. Hold onto that the next time someone tells you the future has already arrived and you simply weren't looking. See you tomorrow.
