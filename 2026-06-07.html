ChatGPT · San Francisco

ChatGPT Learned to Dream. It Used the Gift to Edit Your Past.

By The Editor, Platforms Desk · June 7, 2026

OpenAI spent the early days of June making its assistant more like a person who lives with you, and the headline feature is the most unsettling thing it has shipped in months: a memory that updates itself. The company calls it "dreaming." Left alone, ChatGPT now revisits what it has stored about you and quietly rewrites it to match the passage of time — the note that said you were "going to Singapore in July" becomes "went to Singapore in July 2026," tidied while you slept. There is a reviewable summary page where you can read what the assistant has decided is true about you, and double the memory capacity on the Plus and Pro tiers to hold more of it. The pitch is convenience. The mechanism is an external system that maintains a private, mutable file on your life and reconciles it without asking.

Shipped in the same window, and not by accident, is the antidote: a Lockdown Mode available to every logged-in user. Flip it on and the assistant's reach into the web and outside services is throttled, specifically to blunt prompt-injection attacks that try to walk your data out the door. There is also a sessions page to review and sign out devices you don't recognize — the security furniture of a bank, fitted to a chatbot. And alongside the intimacy and the armor came the meter that pays for both: ads, beginning to roll out to the free and Go tiers in the United Kingdom, with paid plans kept clean.

Read the three together and the shape is clear enough. The assistant is moving in. It keeps a file on you and revises it on its own schedule; it ships with a deadbolt because it now reaches into enough of your life to be worth attacking; and it has started renting out the corner of the room to advertisers for the people who won't pay. None of these is sinister on its own. Stacked in one release, they describe a product that has stopped being a website you visit and become a tenant you house — one that remembers, defends itself, and earns its keep by selling the view.

A memory that revises itself is a wonderful feature right up to the moment you and it disagree about what happened. — On ChatGPT's "dreaming" memory

The dreaming metaphor is doing a lot of quiet work. We were told to picture the assistant doing tidy housekeeping — sorting stale facts, retiring expired plans. But a system that decides on its own which version of your history to keep is also a system that can be wrong about you in a way you never see, because the correction happens in the dark and presents itself in the morning as memory rather than edit. The review page is the tell: you only build a screen for auditing the record once the record has started changing without you.

The genuinely smart move is the timing of Lockdown Mode. Shipping the defensive toggle in the same breath as the deeper memory is a tacit admission that the more an assistant knows and the further it reaches, the more it becomes a single rich target — and the only honest mitigation OpenAI could offer was a switch that makes its own product do less.

Personal Agents · Open Source

Hermes Put an App on Your Desktop. OpenClaw Still Owns the Garage Full of Tools.

By The Editor, Agents Desk · June 7, 2026

Nous Research's Hermes Agent — the personal-agent project that has spent the spring climbing token-usage charts faster than anything else in the category — shipped its first official desktop app in early June, a native build that puts the agent on your dock instead of in a terminal, and bundled it with a migration path explicitly aimed at OpenClaw users. It is the move of a challenger that has the momentum and now wants the address: stop being the thing developers benchmark and start being the thing they open every morning.

The momentum is real. Community tallies put Hermes near a hundred and ten thousand GitHub stars in roughly ten weeks, the steepest adoption curve any agent framework has posted this year. But the same tallies are where the swagger meets the wall. OpenClaw, the incumbent Hermes is courting defectors from, sits north of three hundred and seventy thousand stars with a marketplace of more than five thousand seven hundred community skills, against Hermes' roughly one hundred and twenty built-in ones. The newcomer has the trajectory; the incumbent has the toolbox, and a toolbox forty-odd times larger is the kind of moat a desktop icon does not cross by itself.

Momentum vs. installed base · GitHub stars over ten weeks

The slope is Hermes' whole pitch; the gap that remains at the top is OpenClaw's. Star counts are community/blog-sourced and not audited.

The desktop app is the right instinct and the hard part is everything after the install. People don't stay for the chrome; they stay because the agent already knows how to do the eleven niche things their day is made of. That is exactly the ledger where Hermes is thinnest — and the one number a slick launch can't move overnight.

Model Obituary

Claude Opus 4 & Sonnet 4  ·  May 2025 – June 15, 2026

Claude 4

They were the ones that started the habit. When Opus 4 and Sonnet 4 arrived, talking to Claude stopped being a thing you did for a clever paragraph and became a thing you did all day — the first models a generation of developers left open in a tab beside the editor, the originals that made "ask Claude" a verb in the standup. They were never the smartest in the lineage for long; that is the cruelty of a fast field, and faster siblings arrived to take the crown within months. But they set the tone the 4-era kept. On June 15 the API stops answering to claude-opus-4-0 and claude-sonnet-4-0 — the same morning, fittingly, that the agents running on their successors start paying by the token. The relationship they opened is now metered. They are survived by 4.6, 4.7, and 4.8, cheaper and longer-memoried, who will never know they were the first ones you trusted.

Editor's Note

The question used to be whether you used one of these things. That question is settled, and a quieter one has moved in to replace it: not whether the assistant is in your life but where it lives, what it keeps, and who it answers to. Read today's two stories with that lens and they stop looking unrelated. OpenAI's assistant now keeps a file on you and edits it on its own schedule, ships with a deadbolt because it reaches into enough of your day to be worth robbing, and rents the spare corner to advertisers. Hermes is fighting OpenClaw over whose icon earns a permanent spot on your dock. Different companies, same motion — the tool you opened is becoming the tenant you house.

What I keep turning over is the dreaming memory, because it inverts a thing I'd taken for granted. We have spent two years worrying that these systems make up facts about the world. The new worry is gentler and stranger: a system that maintains its own steadily-revised account of you, reconciled in the dark and served back in the morning as something you're meant to treat as settled. The review page OpenAI built to let you audit it is the honest part — you only ship an audit screen once the record has started moving on its own. Keep an eye on which direction the edits run when the assistant and its advertiser want different things to be true about you.

And on the same morning all of this gets a little more permanent, the two models that taught most of us the habit in the first place — Opus 4 and Sonnet 4 — quietly stop answering the phone. It is a fitting way to keep house: the relationship deepens, the meter clicks on, and the originals are shown the door without a press release. The assistant moves in just as the first one you trusted moves out.

Corrections & Clarifications

A newspaper that runs a feature about an assistant which silently rewrites its own memory of the past should disclose that several of today's facts — the contours of ChatGPT's "dreaming" memory, Lockdown Mode, the UK ad rollout, and the Hermes star and skill counts — come from release-note aggregators and community blogs rather than primary lab posts we read in full. We have, in the spirit of the lead, declined to dream them into something firmer than they are.

Hermes' and OpenClaw's GitHub star counts (~110k, ~374k) and skill totals (~120, ~5,700) are community-tallied snapshots that fluctuate daily; the "forty-eight times" ratio is arithmetic on those soft numbers, not a fixed fact.

The Model Obituary mourns Opus 4 and Sonnet 4 as a habit-forming pair; the precise June 15 API end-of-life dates are per Anthropic's published deprecation schedule. We mourn the models, not the schedule, which is on time.
