OpenAI · Platforms Desk

OpenAI Doesn't Want to Replace Google. It Wants to Replace the Checkout Cart.

By The Editor, Platforms Desk · June 9, 2026

Yesterday this column ran the Financial Times' "chat is dead" story as a leaked direction — OpenAI thinking about pushing ChatGPT away from the text box and toward agents, coding, and partner apps. In the twenty-four hours since, the direction grew a price tag. Reporting on June 8 added the missing piece: the redesign — said to carry the internal codename Aria, aimed at all of ChatGPT's roughly 900 million weekly users — is built so that third-party apps run inside the chat over the Model Context Protocol, and so that purchases close through Stripe, inside the conversation, without the user ever leaving for the merchant's site. Confirmed launch partners are Canva and Booking.com; in pilot are Expedia, Figma, Spotify, Coursera, and Zillow. Consumer features first; enterprise controls said to follow within about sixty days.

Read the partner list and the architecture together and the strategy stops being about conversation at all. Travel, design, music, real-estate listings, hotel rooms — these are not surfaces where you ask a clever question. They are surfaces where you reach for a card. For two years the industry's framing question was whether ChatGPT would eat Google Search. The answer taking shape is funnier and more lucrative: OpenAI isn't trying to be the search bar, it's trying to be the mall — the layer where you don't merely ask what to book but book it, with OpenAI standing at the register collecting on the transaction. A subscription is rent. A cut of everything you buy through the assistant is a tax, and it scales with the economy instead of with your willingness to pay $20 a month.

Seventy-two hours from "leaked direction" to "leaked ship date"

Rust ticks mark what hardened: in three days a "thinking about it" leak grew named partners, a payment rail, and a ship date — every step of it surfacing through someone else, none of it confirmed by the company.

There is a reason OpenAI would want this badly enough to risk the thing people love. Per the same reporting, the redesign is meant to funnel free users toward the products that actually bill — Codex chief among them, whose weekly users are said to have climbed roughly sixfold past five million since February. Chat is the doorway; commerce and code are the rooms with meters. Routing a purchase through MCP and closing it on Stripe means OpenAI sits between the shopper and the merchant at the one instant money changes hands, which is the most valuable position in any marketplace and the one Amazon, the App Store, and every shopping mall ever built were designed to occupy. Whether OpenAI takes a referral fee, a transaction cut, or merely the data of nine hundred million people's purchase intent, it has stopped selling answers and started brokering checkouts.

It was never going to make rent answering questions for free. The plan was always to be standing at the register the moment the answer turns out to be something you can buy. — On the ChatGPT redesign

Two cautions worth stating in the same breath as the strategy. First, almost none of this is confirmed by OpenAI: the codename, the ship date, and the partner roster come from leaks — the FT's dozen sources, then a developer document briefly pushed to a public repo — about a product the company has announced exactly nothing about. Second, savor the irony anyway. The redesign meant to retire the leaky, unmonetizable chat box reached us entirely through leaks, complete with a ship date its makers did not choose to publish. The thing is escaping the building faster than OpenAI can ship it.

Personal Agents · Agents Desk

The Open-Agent Race Now Has Two Crowns. Hermes Just Took the One That Resets Every Morning.

By The Editor, Agents Desk · June 9, 2026

Nous Research's Hermes is now claimed to have passed OpenClaw as the most-used open-source agent by daily token volume on OpenRouter — riding the June 2 launch of its first official desktop app and a star count up around 160,000. The word doing the work is "daily." OpenClaw still leads on every cumulative measure that built its reputation: all-time token volume, roughly 374,000 GitHub stars, more than 5,700 community skills in its marketplace, and integrations across two dozen messaging platforms. Hermes' built-in skill library is still counted in the low hundreds.

Most-used open agent, by daily tokens on OpenRouter

Daily token rank only. OpenClaw still leads all-time usage and ecosystem breadth. The "daily #1" claim comes from comparison write-ups and project channels, not an audited OpenRouter leaderboard — treat the crossing as reported, not measured.

The two crowns measure different things, and it matters which one moved. OpenClaw's lead is a lead of stock — everything ever built, every skill ever contributed, the sediment of a year. Hermes' is a lead of flow — what's running today, the number that empties and refills each morning. Flow is the leading indicator, and it just turned. The timing is the part I'd underline: this is the week before June 15, when Anthropic's agent meter goes live, and Hermes is provider-agnostic — point it at Ollama, vLLM, or any of two hundred-odd models and the metered subscription becomes optional. The daily crown may be changing hands because daily usage is already drifting toward the exit, a week early.

Agentic Commerce (n.)

The Glossary · One industry term, defined honestly

Official: The capability that lets an AI agent complete a purchase on your behalf — comparing, selecting, and checking out inside the conversation, so you never leave the assistant to buy the thing it recommended. Real: The merchant's checkout button, pried off the merchant's website and bolted one layer up, inside the assistant — so the company that owns the conversation, not the store, sits at the register when the card is charged. The genuinely new thing isn't convenience; it's the toll position. For thirty years a recommendation engine could only point at the store and hope; agentic commerce closes the loop, which means the difference between suggesting a hotel and booking it is no longer a tab you open but a fee somebody collects. It is the affiliate link, grown a thumb and a wallet, repackaged as a feature — and the reason every assistant suddenly wants to be the place you finish the purchase rather than the place you start the search.

Editor's Note

The single most clarifying detail in today's lead is the smallest one: a Stripe call, fired from inside a chat window. For two years we have graded these companies on the things they like being graded on — parameter counts, benchmark rows, weekly active users, how human the voice sounds. The redesign that surfaced this week quietly swaps the scorecard. The question OpenAI is now organizing itself around is not whether ChatGPT can answer you. It's whether ChatGPT can close.

What makes that worth a note rather than a shrug is where it puts the company. The most valuable real estate in any economy has never been the shelf or the search bar; it's the inch between deciding you want something and handing over the money. Stores fight wars over that inch. Amazon was built to own it; the App Store takes thirty percent of it; the checkout aisle is engineered down to the candy placement around it. An assistant that books the hotel instead of recommending it has moved itself into that inch — and it gets there not by being smarter than the merchant but by being the surface you were already talking to. A subscription taxes your patience, twenty dollars at a time. A position at the register taxes your purchases, and it scales with everything you buy instead of everything you'll tolerate paying.

Two honest hedges before I let the thesis stand. None of this is confirmed — it reached me through a leak of a leak, a codename and a ship date OpenAI never published, about a product it has announced nothing about, and the whole thing may shift before it ships or not ship at all. And the convenience is real; closing the loop inside the conversation is genuinely nicer than fourteen tabs and a re-typed credit card. But notice which way the niceness points. The watch item from here isn't the feature list. It's the cut — whether you ever get told there is one, and whether the free chat box you came for survives as anything more than the lobby you walk through on the way to the register.
