Anthropic · Washington Desk

You Can't Put the Public on a List. So They're Vetting the Model.

By The Editor, Washington Desk · June 29, 2026

Fable 5, the consumer model the Commerce Department switched off on June 12, is reportedly days from returning. According to reporting that surfaced over the weekend, the administration expects to restore public access "as soon as this week," with one step left before the lights come back on: sign-off from the Pentagon and the National Security Agency. The development follows the June 26 letter from Secretary Howard Lutnick that cleared the more capable Mythos 5 for roughly a hundred vetted US organizations. Read it carefully — this is an expectation, not a switch already flipped. As of the weekend Anthropic was still serving zero public traffic, and the named gatekeepers had not signed.

Note how differently the two models are coming home. Mythos returned the way a controlled thing usually returns: through a roster. The government named the firms allowed to hold it, and danger was managed by knowing every hand on the model. Fable can't take that road, because the people who use a consumer model are the one group with no roster — you cannot write down "the public." So the order is doing the only other thing available. Instead of clearing the users, it is clearing the model: putting Fable 5 in front of the Pentagon and the NSA, and, if it passes, letting everyone back in at once. When you can't vet the audience, you vet the artifact.

Which means the two restorations rest on opposite promises. Mythos came back on a promise about its users — that the State will always know who is holding the dangerous thing. Fable, if it returns, comes back on a promise about itself — that it is safe enough to hand to people the State will never identify. One bargain contains the danger by surveilling who holds the model; the other contains it by inspecting the model until it no longer matters who holds it. Same order, same fortnight, two entirely different theories of how you make software exportable again — and the only thing that decided which model got which is whether it had a list or a public.

To bring back a model the whole public uses, the government isn't checking the public. It's checking the model — because the public is the one thing nobody can finish counting.

OpenAI · Frontier Desk

GPT-5.6 Gets a Spec Sheet: Three Tiers, a 1.5-Million-Token Window, and a Government Pre-Read

By The Editor, Frontier Desk · June 29, 2026

The numbers behind last week's GPT-5.6 preview have filled in. OpenAI's new family runs three tiers — Sol at the top for frontier reasoning and long-horizon agentic work, Terra in the middle pitched at roughly GPT-5.5 quality for about half the cost, and Luna as the fast, cheap one. The headline figure is context length: a claimed window of about 1.5 million tokens, up from roughly 1.05 million on GPT-5.5, which the company bills as the largest among frontier models, alongside an "ultra mode," steadier prompt caching, and revised pricing.

The detail worth keeping is how the preview opened. By the secondary accounts, GPT-5.6 went to a limited set of partners only after the US government asked to review the model before any broad release — the same shape as this morning's lead, viewed from the other lab. One frontier model is held until the Pentagon and NSA clear it; another is parceled out to a roster Washington has already seen. The waiting room is getting crowded, and the receptionist works for the government.

Model Watch

While the Frontier Waits in Line, the Open Field Just Ships

By The Editor, Model Watch · June 29, 2026

Two notes from the parts of the market that don't need anyone's blessing. Google's Gemini 3.5 Pro, promised for a June general release at I/O in May, has reportedly slipped to July and remains enterprise-preview only — absent from the consumer app and the public model picker (single-sourced, so treat the date loosely). And the open-weight field kept moving without a pause: Z.ai's GLM-5.2, a roughly 744-billion-parameter model released in mid-June, has taken the lead on the open-intelligence rankings, reportedly beating GPT-5.5 on several long-horizon coding tasks at about a sixth the price, with Moonshot's Kimi K2.7 Code and DeepSeek's V4 still in the conversation.

The contrast with the lead writes itself. The models a government can switch off are the ones spending the month in clearance queues; the models nobody can switch off, because the weights are already on a hundred thousand hard drives, are the ones you can download before breakfast. An export desk can gate an API. It cannot gate a file that already left the building.

Editor's Note

An allowlist works because it ends. You can reach the last name on it, vet that one too, and close the file. The public is the single group that never ends — which is why, when Commerce cleared Mythos 5 last week, it could hand the dangerous model to a hundred named firms and not the rest of us. There was a list to finish for them, and no list to finish for us. Now Fable 5, the consumer model, is reportedly days from coming back, and it cannot come back the same way, because there is still no list. So the government is doing the only other thing available: instead of clearing the people, it is clearing the model.

These are not the same bargain wearing two coats. Mythos returned on a promise about its users — the State will know every hand on it. Fable, if it returns, comes back on a promise about itself — that it is safe enough to hand to people the State will never identify. One road manages danger by watching who holds the model. The other manages it by inspecting the model so closely that it stops mattering who holds it. The same order produced both inside a single week, and the only thing that decided which model got which is whether it came with a roster or a public.

Which is the quiet way this ends. Not with Commerce saying the switch was a mistake — with the model passing the test the switch implied it would fail. Seventeen days ago a government reached into a live product and turned it off. If the reporting holds, the way back on isn't an apology and it isn't a repeal; it's a clearance. Nobody in Washington has to climb down. The weights get a stamp, and the public files back in behind them. We return not because we were cleared. The model was.

See you tomorrow.
