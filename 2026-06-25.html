Anthropic · Privacy Desk

To Reopen Fable 5 to Americans, Anthropic Will First Make Sure You Are One

By The Editor, Washington Desk · June 25, 2026

Thirteen days into the government-ordered blackout of Fable 5 and Mythos 5, Anthropic has done the most concrete thing it has done all week — and it has nothing to do with the model. The company rewrote its privacy policy, adding a new category called "Verification Data": a government-issued identity document, a photo or short video of your face, and what the policy calls a "facial geometry template," processed by a third-party identity vendor, Persona. Anthropic says the biometrics aren't stored on its own servers and aren't used to train anything. The change takes effect July 8. It is being read across the trade press — CIO, Gizmodo, TechTimes — as exactly one thing: the apparatus Anthropic needs to confirm a user is a US citizen, and so to switch its flagship back on for Americans without the Commerce order ever being lifted.

Sit with the shape of that solution. The export order's complaint was that foreign nationals might use a model the government considers too dangerous to leave on. Anthropic cannot make the model less dangerous on demand — that was never on the table. So the workaround routes around the model entirely and lands on you. If the problem is who is asking, you fix it not by changing what the system can do but by changing how much it knows about the person typing. The danger has been relocated from the weights to the passport. The safety story, eleven days in, has quietly become a surveillance story, and the surveillance is of the customer, not the capability.

Two honest caveats. Anthropic has not officially said the new policy exists to restore Fable 5; it published a privacy change with a date, and everyone drew the obvious line. At least one outlet calls the nationality-gating "unconfirmed," and another argues citizenship checks won't actually satisfy a Commerce order that, on its face, bars the model for everyone. But the artifact itself is undisputed and dated: beginning July 8, a frontier chatbot would like to see your face before it answers, and it has hired a company to hold the image.

The model was too dangerous to let foreigners use. The remedy on offer isn't a safer model — it's a checkpoint that scans your face and decides whether you're allowed to ask. — The Daily Hallucination, Washington Desk

There is a tidy irony in the vendor's name. To prove you are a real, permitted person, you will hand your government ID and a video of your face to a company called Persona — the word for the mask. The frontier model that was supposed to be the most capable thing Anthropic had ever shipped will, if this is the path, reintroduce itself to the American public as a service that cannot speak to you until it has confirmed who you are. The capability was never the part the state could touch. Your identity, it turns out, is.

Policy · Capitol Hill

A Bipartisan Four Want to Know If the Ban Was Ever Legal

By The Editor, Washington Desk · June 25, 2026

While Anthropic builds a way around the order, Congress has started asking whether the order should have been built at all. On June 18, four House members — Sam Liccardo (D-CA), Ted Lieu (D-CA), Jay Obernolte (R-CA) and Scott Franklin (R-FL) — sent Commerce Secretary Howard Lutnick a letter demanding a written explanation by June 26. They want his legal authority, his technical evaluation, his review process, and his criteria for restoring access. The deadline is tomorrow.

The load-bearing question is the first one. Export-control law — the Bureau of Industry and Security's authority under EAR §744.22 — governs the "export of items": physical goods, exportable code. The lawmakers, and the analysts they're citing, note it was not written to cover blocking foreign nationals from an API call. An inference request is not a crate on a ship. If the statute doesn't reach a chatbot, the most aggressive AI action Washington has taken may rest on a foundation no one has had to defend in writing — until tomorrow afternoon. The members warn, dryly, that the move "may establish a precedent with significant implications" for the whole sector. They are not wrong, which is the problem.

Model Watch · OpenAI

The Market Un-Bet GPT-5.6 This Week — and Just Moved the Date

By The Editor, Model Desk · June 25, 2026

A week ago, Polymarket was about 83% sure OpenAI's GPT-5.6 would ship inside the June 22–28 window. As that window closes with no model card, no API note, and no post, the same contract has collapsed to roughly 18%. The money didn't decide the model isn't real; it decided the date was wrong, and rolled its conviction down the calendar — end-of-July now reads around 94%, with July 31 the single favored day. More than half a million dollars sits on the timing alone.

GPT-5.6 ships — the market moved the date, not the conviction

The collapse isn't doubt; it's a postponement. Polymarket prices are bets, not forecasts.

Chief scientist Jakub Pachocki has called GPT-5.6 a "meaningful improvement" over GPT-5.5, which itself shipped in April — and that leaked phrase remains the only thing anyone has on the model besides odds. Google's Gemini 3.5 Pro, unveiled at I/O on May 19 with general availability "targeted for June," is in the same condition: still an enterprise preview, still not out. We have reached the point where the two most-anticipated models in the world are best described by their betting lines.

Editor's Note

You can usually tell what a rule was really for by watching what it costs to get around it. This week we got the price of getting Fable 5 back, and it is not a better safety case or a quieter model — it is your face. Anthropic's route around a national-security ban runs through a government ID, a photo, and a "facial geometry template" held by a vendor named, with no apparent embarrassment, Persona. The order asked who is allowed to use this model; the answer being built is a checkpoint that asks who you are. The model never had to change. You did.

That's the part I keep turning over. For two weeks this story has been about a capability the government decided was too dangerous to export. The fix arriving July 8 doesn't touch the capability at all. It moves the entire problem off the model and onto the user — makes you legible, verifiable, and sortable by passport, so the dangerous thing can stay exactly as dangerous as it was, now gated behind a turnstile that knows your name. We spent a year worried these systems would learn too much about the world. The first concrete safety remedy of the era is one that learns more about you.

The other two stories on the page rhyme with it from opposite ends. Congress, in a rare bipartisan four, has finally asked the question everyone skipped past in the rush — not "when does it come back," but "was blocking an API ever a thing export law lets you do?" Lutnick has until tomorrow to put an answer in writing, and the honest version may be that the most forceful AI order in US history was built on a statute about crates. Meanwhile the market that was sure GPT-5.6 ships this week quietly moved its bet to July, because the one thing the frontier reliably produces on schedule is a slipped schedule. A ban looking for its legal footing, a launch looking for its date, and a chatbot that wants to see your ID. Nobody's lifting the wall. They're just learning to live alongside it.

See you tomorrow.
