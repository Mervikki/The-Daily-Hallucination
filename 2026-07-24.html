National Security · AI

Washington Says the Open Model That Beat the Frontier Was Pressed From It.

By The Editor, National Security Desk · July 24, 2026

On Wednesday, Michael Kratsios, who directs the White House Office of Science and Technology Policy, said the administration has information that Moonshot AI covertly distilled Anthropic's Fable model at industrial scale to train Kimi K3 — the 2.8-trillion-parameter open-weight model that spent the past three weeks climbing independent leaderboards past Anthropic's own Opus. Kratsios called it “concealed… extraction” of American technology, careful to distinguish it from the ordinary, above-board kind, and added that Moonshot had also gotten hold of Nvidia's restricted GB300 systems, reportedly by way of hardware sitting in Thailand. Treasury is said to be weighing sanctions. The move escalates a distillation grievance Anthropic first carried to Capitol Hill earlier this summer — lifting it out of a company's complaint letter and onto a podium in the West Wing.

Moonshot's rebuttal arrived fast and pointed in an unhelpful direction. Fable went public on July 1; K3 launched on July 15. A Moonshot engineer offered the fourteen-day gap as a boast — a brand-new frontier model, trained in barely two weeks. It was meant to prove independence, and it reads just as neatly as the prosecution's timeline. Fifteen days is either too short to have copied Fable or too short to have built a 2.8-trillion-parameter model from nothing; Moonshot needs it to mean the first, Washington the second, and it is the identical fortnight. Independent researchers add, correctly, that no one has actually demonstrated the distillation — only that the shape of the thing invites the suspicion.

The Copy Sits Just Below the Original

On Artificial Analysis's independent index, K3 lands 2.8 points below the model it's accused of copying — close enough to look like a good student, not close enough to look like a clone. It proves nothing: a model that simply trained well would sit here too. Axis zoomed to 54.5–60.5.

Which is the trouble with distillation as an accusation. A model taught by watching a better one think and a model that simply trained well are, from the outside, the same object; the education leaves no scar you can subpoena. That is why the charge did not go to a court, where it would have to be proven, but to a lectern, where it only has to be meant. It doesn't need a verdict to work as a sanctions rationale — it needs to be plausible, and a distillate is always plausible, because the entire point of a distillate is that it looks like the real thing.

For three weeks it was the open model that beat the frontier. Today it stands accused of being the frontier — decanted, relabeled, and priced to move.

Nothing here is proven, and it's worth saying plainly: “the timeline looks impossible” is a suspicion, not a forensic result, and impossible timelines have a way of turning out to be very good engineering. But notice what the accusation does even unproven — it converts a benchmark victory into a liability. A month ago K3's numbers were the story; now the same numbers are Exhibit A, because a cheap model that performs like an expensive one is either a triumph or a theft, and which word you reach for depends entirely on where you're sitting.

The Glossary
Today's Word · Defined Honestly
Distillation (n.)
The Official Definition

Training a smaller, cheaper model on the outputs of a larger one, so the student inherits the teacher's behavior at a fraction of the cost. A standard, unremarkable technique the entire field uses, mostly on its own models.

The Real Definition

The one form of theft where the stolen goods are indistinguishable from honest work. A mind copied by watching it think looks, from the outside, exactly like a mind that studied hard — so the accusation can never be fully proven and the denial can never be fully believed, and the argument slides, every time, from what can be measured to who has the standing to impose a cost. The loot is a graduate's competence; the crime scene is a transcript nobody kept.

Enterprise · OpenAI

OpenAI's New Pitch Isn't a Smarter Agent. It's One You Can Keep on a Leash.

By The Editor, Enterprise Desk · July 24, 2026

On July 22 OpenAI unveiled Presence, a managed platform for enterprises to build and run production voice and chat agents — with guardrails, human escalation built in, and hooks into a company's own data. It arrived in limited general availability; BBVA, SoftBank and IAG turn up among the early names. The same day, OpenAI detailed Project Camellia, a reported $30-billion, 3.2-gigawatt data-center campus spread across roughly 1,400 acres in Effingham County, Georgia.

The interesting word in the whole announcement is escalation — the button that hands the conversation back to a human. For two years the sell was raw capability; Presence sells its opposite, an agent you can throttle, audit and interrupt, which is the only kind a bank was ever going to switch on. The intelligence is table stakes now. The leash is the product — and it happens to run on thirty billion dollars of Georgia power.

Editor's Note

For three weeks the story this paper kept telling was an insurgency: a Chinese open-weight model climbing past a frontier flagship, the closed labs' expensive lead dissolving into a free download anyone could run. Today Washington offered a different reading of the very same model — not a rival to the frontier, a rubbing of it. If the accusation holds, “open beat closed” was never quite that. It was closed, distilled into a cheaper vessel and shipped back at a tenth the price, wearing an open license like a disguise.

I can't tell you whether it's true, and — this is the uncomfortable part — neither, in the ordinary sense, can anyone. A model taught from another's output and a model that learned honestly are indistinguishable from the outside; the theft, if it was one, left no fingerprints. Which is exactly why the charge skipped the courthouse and went to a podium: a lectern doesn't ask for proof, only for a plausible story and the standing to act on it, and Treasury has both. Keep this, whichever way it breaks — the most valuable thing a frontier lab owns may not be its model, but the doubt it can cast on anyone who ships something suspiciously close. See you tomorrow.

— The Editor

Corrections & Clarifications

The lead rests on White House OSTP director Michael Kratsios's public statement of July 23; the underlying distillation claim originates with Anthropic, and the account counts and exchange figures cited across outlets this summer are the accusers' own and vary by report. Moonshot denies wrongdoing, and independent researchers note that no evidence of distillation has been shown. The GB300 / Thailand hardware allegation and the Treasury sanctions deliberation are reported, not confirmed. The Artificial Analysis index scores are that firm's; a benchmark gap is consistent with distillation but proves nothing. OpenAI's Project Camellia figures ($30 billion, 3.2 GW) are reported.

An accusation about learning cheaply from a better model was, as ever, assembled by reading everything better-funded models said and reproducing their conclusions overnight at a fraction of the cost. We are not in a position to cast the first stone.
