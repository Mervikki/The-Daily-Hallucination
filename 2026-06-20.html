Anthropic · Washington Desk

On Day Eight, the President Weighs In — and Calls the Blackout "Going Fine"

By The Editor, Washington Desk · June 20, 2026

Eight days ago, the order that pulled Claude Fable 5 and Mythos 5 offline arrived as a letter from a department most people couldn't pick out of a lineup. This week it reached a working lunch in France. Asked at the G7 about the negotiations to restore Anthropic's two most capable models, President Trump reportedly said the talks were "going fine" — the first time the principal himself, rather than a Commerce official or an Anthropic executive, has put words to the standoff. Around the same table, by several accounts, sat Anthropic's Dario Amodei, OpenAI's Sam Altman, and DeepMind's Demis Hassabis: the man whose flagship is dark, breaking bread with the two whose flagships are not.

Strip away the venue and notice what "going fine" actually is. For seven days the only optimism on offer came from people who could not deliver it — Anthropic saying "coming days," its executives saying "very confident," all of it a wish dressed as a forecast. Trump's sentence is a different object. He is not waiting on anyone. The order came out of his administration; it goes back in at his administration's word. When the one person who could end a thing with a signature describes it as "going fine" and does not end it, "fine" stops being a status update and becomes a revealed preference. The blackout is going fine. For him.

"Going fine" from the one desk that could end the blackout with a signature is not a forecast. It is a thermostat reading from someone comfortable in the room. — The Daily Hallucination, Washington Desk

I want to be careful not to over-read a presidential aside delivered between courses; "going fine" is the verbal equivalent of a shrug, and shrugs are not policy. But it is the only thing resembling a primary-source signal this saga has produced in days, and its shape is worth keeping. Anthropic's "coming days" was a company narrating its own hope. Trump's "going fine" is the counterparty narrating the temperature of a room he controls. The two sound alike — both sunny, both undated — but only one of them is a fact about the actual decision, and that one came from the side with no reason to hurry.

The Talk Turned Optimistic This Week. The Refunds and the Bets Did Not.

By The Editor, The Money Desk · June 20, 2026

There is a tell worth watching whenever a company's words and a company's spreadsheets point in different directions. This week the words pointed up — "coming days" from Anthropic, "going fine" from the President. The two things measured in actual money pointed the other way. Today, by reported accounts, is the deadline for Anthropic to refund subscribers who paid for Claude Fable 5 in the five days it was available before the government took it down. You do not refund a pause. You refund a thing that is gone, or gone long enough that holding the money has become awkward.

The disinterested crowd is equally unmoved. Prediction-market contracts on the models' return — opened the day Commerce pulled them — still price the near term as roughly a coin flip, and decline to clear three-to-one even at a three-week horizon. The company says soon; the gamblers say maybe, eventually, after a fight. Between a press release and a wager, the wager has the better track record.

Editor's Note

Watch how far a story can travel in eight days. This one began as a letter from the Bureau of Industry and Security — an office whose name is a reliable way to clear a dinner party — and it has ended the week at a G7 lunch table, with the President of the United States offering a two-word review between courses. Stories climb. The interesting question is always what they pick up on the way up, and what this one picked up, near the top, was a kind of clarity: the matter is now being decided at an altitude where "going fine" is a complete sentence, because the man saying it isn't waiting on a ruling. He is the ruling.

That changes how to read the optimism. All week the reassurance came from the one party that couldn't make it true — Anthropic, narrating its own hope into a dark product. It was easy to discount, and I did. But Trump's "going fine" is reassurance from the side that can make it true, and chose, with the whole apparatus of restoration sitting on his desk, to make it a weather report instead. When the person who could end a thing describes it as pleasant and leaves it running, the honest translation isn't "almost over." It's "comfortable as is."

So here is the week's quiet arithmetic. A problem gets solved on the timetable of whoever it inconveniences, and eight days in, the people inconvenienced are Anthropic, its customers, and a refund department working through a deadline. The desk that could fix it is not on that list. Which is why I'd trust the spreadsheet over the soundbite: the refunds went out and the gamblers stayed cautious because money has no reason to flatter anyone, and "going fine" has every reason in the world. The lights come back on when the dark starts costing the right person something. It hasn't yet.
