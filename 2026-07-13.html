Anthropic · Fable 5

The Government Couldn't Switch Fable 5 Off for Long. A Billing Setting Just Did.

By The Editor, Business Desk · July 13, 2026

Since July 8, Anthropic had extended a promotion that let paid Pro and Max subscribers use Claude Fable 5 without dipping into prepaid credits — up to roughly half their weekly usage limit. That extension, itself granted after a backlash over an abrupt earlier cutoff, expired over the weekend. As of today, Fable 5 is no longer bundled into the standard paid plans; reaching for it again means buying prepaid usage credits, priced at a reported $10 per million input tokens and $50 per million output tokens. No blog post marked the change. The free lane simply closed on schedule.

Now set that calendar beside the one we spent all of June staring at. On June 12 a US export order made Fable 5 unavailable; the model went dark for nineteen days and came back globally on July 1. For three weeks the most powerful actor in the story was Washington — and even Washington needed a Cabinet-level standoff, a national-security rationale and a sign-off process to make one model briefly disappear, then had to give it back. Anthropic just made the same model unavailable again, to anyone unwilling to pay per token, by letting a promotion lapse. No hearing, no letter, no headline. One is a national-security saga; the other is a settings page.

Two Ways to Switch a Model Off

Fable 5's availability since June. The export blackout lasted nineteen days and lifted; the free window lasted eleven and closed into a paywall. Dates per trade-press coverage.

The export blackout was loud, contested, and temporary — a thing you could argue about, appeal, and outlast. The paywall is quiet, uncontested, and permanent. There is no deadline on which the price comes off, no Congressional letter to write, no odds market pricing when it lifts. A meter doesn't lift. It just sits there, being the reason.

It took a Cabinet secretary three weeks and a national-security order to make Fable 5 briefly unavailable. It took Anthropic's billing department one weekend, and nobody had to hold a hearing.

To be fair to the meter: running a frontier model costs real money, someone pays for those tokens, and a free promotion was never going to double as a business model. Anthropic charging for its best model is not the scandal; of course it is charging. The point is which lever actually decides who gets to use a frontier model — and we spent a month treating that as a policy question, watching Washington as though the export order were the switch. It was a pricing question the whole time. This weekend it got answered, on schedule, without a word, and the answer will outlast every headline the blackout earned.

Anthropic · Product

Claude Now Keeps a Diary About You, and Hands It Back Once a Month.

By The Editor, Products Desk · July 13, 2026

Anthropic has quietly added Reflect, a dashboard under Settings that returns a monthly recap of your Claude habits: your most-discussed topics, your most-active day, your peak hour, a read on your “work style.” It's in beta on Free, Pro and Max, on web and desktop, and works only if you've turned memory on. TechCrunch described it as Anthropic “quietly selling you on AI,” which is about the size of it — a feature whose job is to remind you how much of your week already runs through the chat box.

There is a tidy symmetry in shipping a mirror that flatters how much you use Claude the same week you start paying per token for its best model. Reflect tells you that you talked to it most on Tuesdays; the billing page is about to tell you what Tuesdays cost. A usage recap is a lovely thing to hand someone at exactly the moment usage becomes the meter.

Google · Gemini

Google Names a Date for Gemini 3.5 Pro. It Has Named Dates Before.

By The Editor, Models Desk · July 13, 2026

Google says its next flagship, Gemini 3.5 Pro, will arrive July 17 — reportedly after DeepMind scrapped the previous base model and ran a fresh pre-training cycle, a ground-up rebuild rather than a tune-up. The pitch is a two-million-token context window, a “Deep Think” reasoning layer, and autonomous-workflow features, positioned squarely against GPT-5.6 and Anthropic's Fable 5.

We note the date without leaning on it. Gemini 3.5 Pro has been “next month” since Google I/O in May, and July 17 is merely the latest month to hold the title. Harder to wave off than the schedule is the staffing: the flagship built to catch two rivals is being rebuilt by a team that has reportedly been shedding senior researchers to those rivals — including, by several accounts, a Nobel-winning scientist now at Anthropic and a marquee name now at OpenAI. A from-scratch rebuild is already an admission the last base wasn't good enough; doing it short-handed, while your architects walk toward the competition, is the part Google would rather you not dwell on. We'll grade the model when it ships, and not one day sooner.

Editor's Note

Watch where the power to make Fable 5 unavailable has traveled. In June it sat with a Cabinet secretary, who needed an export order, a three-week standoff and a public sign-off to switch one model off — and even then it lasted nineteen days before he had to hand it back. This week the same power sat with Anthropic's billing team, which needed only a promotion to lapse and told no one. The louder the hand on the switch, the more temporary the result; the quieter the hand, the more it sticks. A national-security order lifts. A price does not.

That's the through-line under a slow Monday. The day's other items — a dashboard that shows you how much you use Claude, a Google flagship that keeps not arriving — each describe a model you can't quite have: one because the free lane just closed, one because the date keeps sliding. What's being rationed stopped being capability a while ago. It's access, and access is set by a billing toggle and a launch calendar, neither of which answers to anyone. A month ago we thought the question was whether Washington would let you use Fable 5. The question was always what Anthropic would charge you — and it just answered, without a hearing. See you tomorrow.

— The Editor
