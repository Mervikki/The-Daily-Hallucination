OpenAI · Research

A Machine Had an Original Idea, and the Skeptic Who Called the Last Bluff Put His Name on This One

By The Editor, Research Desk · May 21, 2026

On Tuesday OpenAI said one of its internal reasoning models disproved the planar unit-distance conjecture — a question Paul Erdős posed in 1946 about how many pairs of points on a flat surface can sit exactly one unit apart. For roughly eighty years the working assumption was that the square grid was about as good as any arrangement could get. The model found an entirely new family of constructions that beats it, and supplied a proof. A group of outside mathematicians checked the argument and wrote a companion paper explaining why it matters. Tim Gowers, a Fields medalist, called the result "a milestone in AI mathematics." The number theorist Arul Shankar said it shows current models "go beyond just helpers" and "are capable of having original ingenious ideas." None of that is the language the field hands out to a search engine that got lucky.

What makes it land is who was holding the stopwatch. In October, OpenAI's then-VP Kevin Weil announced on X that GPT-5 had cracked ten unsolved Erdős problems. Thomas Bloom — who maintains the canonical Erdős-problems site — pointed out that the model had not proven anything new; it had surfaced solutions already sitting in the published literature. Yann LeCun and Demis Hassabis took their shots, the claim deflated, and Weil left OpenAI in April. The difference between retrieving a proof and inventing one is the entire credibility of the genre, and last autumn OpenAI was caught on the wrong side of it. This week the verifiers list includes Noga Alon, Melanie Wood — and Thomas Bloom, the same man who detonated the previous claim. When your harshest auditor signs the second set of books, the second set of books is probably real.

So we will say the unfashionable thing and credit it plainly: this is a genuine, externally checked, original mathematical result produced by a machine, and it is the first time we have been able to write that sentence without a caveat doing all the load-bearing. It is also one result, in one corner of one field, and the gap between "had an ingenious idea about unit distances" and "can be trusted with your tax return" remains roughly the width of the continent. But the narrow claim is true, and after a year of math-from-AI press releases that wilted on contact with an expert, a narrow true claim is the rarer and more interesting thing.

There is a tidy irony in a publication named for confident wrongness covering a model that, this time, was confidently right about something no human had proven. The lesson is not that the machines are infallible. It is that the only check that ever mattered was the one we keep skipping: ask someone who would enjoy proving you wrong, and wait for the answer.

Markets · IPOs

Wall Street's New Worry: What If the Thing Everyone Is Going Public On Is About to Be Nearly Free?

By The Editor, Markets Desk · May 21, 2026

CNBC put a name this week to the anxiety humming under the roadshows: cheap AI could derail the OpenAI and Anthropic IPOs. The labs have raised on a scale that only makes sense if frontier inference stays scarce and dear — OpenAI's reported $122 billion round at an $852 billion valuation, the largest private raise on record; Anthropic stacking another $40 billion from Google and $5 billion from Amazon on top of its compute commitments. Both are pointed at public listings in the second half of the year. The problem is the price of the underlying good, which keeps falling.

DeepSeek V4 shipped this spring under an MIT license with a million-token context and an attention rewrite that cuts inference cost to a fraction of the prior generation. It is not the frontier, but it is close enough for most of the work most companies actually buy, at a hosted price that sits roughly two orders of magnitude below the closed flagships. If the floor on capability keeps rising while the price races toward zero, the question the bankers do not want shouted from the back of the room is the simple one: what, exactly, is the moat you are charging $852 billion for?

Meta · Layoffs

Record Profit, Eight Thousand Gone, and a Median Paycheck That Shrank: Meta Picks Its Side of the Curve

By The Editor, Menlo Park Desk · May 21, 2026

The termination emails that went out Wednesday — roughly eight thousand of them, about a tenth of the company, with another six thousand open requisitions canceled for an effective cut near fourteen thousand — landed in the same week Meta reported a record quarter: $56.31 billion in revenue and $26.8 billion in net income. The company is steering something on the order of $145 billion into AI infrastructure this year, and reassigning roughly seven thousand surviving employees into new units with names like Applied AI Engineering and the Agent Transformation Accelerator, reporting up into Alexandr Wang's Superintelligence Labs. The layoffs and the record are not a contradiction Meta is trying to resolve. They are the strategy.

The number that should travel furthest is the quietest one. Median total compensation at Meta fell from $417,400 in 2024 to $388,200 in 2025 — the typical employee got cheaper — over the same stretch that Zuckerberg has been personally dangling packages reported near $100 million to land individual AI researchers. Wired, after interviewing more than a dozen current and former staff, describes a mood that is grim in one of the most profitable companies on earth. The org chart is being sorted into two castes: a small number of people worth a nine-figure offer, and a large number worth slightly less than they were worth last year.

And then there is the line that does not survive contact with yesterday's. Zuckerberg told employees this week that AI is "not driving" the layoffs — a day after the internal memo we covered yesterday forecast that software would out-produce the surviving engineers four to one. Both statements cannot be load-bearing at once. Either the machines are about to do most of the work, in which case they are obviously driving the headcount math, or they are not, in which case someone should tell the memo. More cuts are already flagged for August and the fall, so there will be further chances to pick a story and stick with it.

Anthropic · Claude Code

Users Said Claude Code Got Dumber for Six Weeks. The Postmortem Going Around Says They Were Right.

By The Editor, Engineering Desk · May 21, 2026

Anthropic's engineering postmortem — dated April 23 and making the rounds again this week as the trade press digests it — traces six weeks of "Claude Code feels worse" complaints to three changes that overlapped in time and each hit a different slice of traffic. When Opus 4.6 shipped in February with reasoning effort defaulted to "high," users griped about long think times; on March 4 Anthropic quietly cut the default to "medium," a move the postmortem now calls, in its own words, "the wrong tradeoff." A March 26 efficiency change meant to prune stale reasoning from idle sessions carried a bug that made it prune on every turn instead. And an April system-prompt edit capped commentary at 25 words between tool calls and answers at 100, knocking about 3% off quality before it was pulled four days later.

All three were fixed by April 20 in v2.1.116, and Anthropic reset usage limits as an apology. The honest takeaway is the one labs hate to print: the users were right and the dashboards were wrong for the better part of two months. A model can degrade in ways that show up in lived experience long before they show up in an eval — which is a useful thing to remember the next time a company tells you nothing has changed because the benchmark numbers held. The crowd saying "it feels worse" was, this time, a more sensitive instrument than the metrics.

Editor's Note

The genuinely new thing today is small and easy to miss under the louder stories: a machine had an original mathematical idea, and the person best positioned to call it a fraud read it carefully and signed off. Sit with how rare that sentence has been. For a solid year the AI-does-math story was a parade of announcements that collapsed the moment a real mathematician opened the PDF. This one cleared the only bar that counts, which is a skeptic's signature, and it deserves to be taken at face value before we move on to being clever about it.

And then look at what the rest of the day did with the news that machines can now invent. Meta fired eight thousand people and booked a record quarter in the same breath, and told the survivors the AI is not the reason — one day after a memo that said the AI was the reason. Wall Street spent the afternoon worrying the whole frontier might get too cheap to justify the valuations it just raised. And Anthropic's own engineers confirmed that for six weeks the product had quietly gotten worse while the dashboards insisted it hadn't. The capability went up. Almost everything humans had built around it spent the day flinching.

The machine's contribution to Thursday was one true thing nobody had known before. Ours was eight thousand layoff notices, a tremor in a valuation, and a bug report. I don't have a tidy lesson that reconciles those columns, and I've grown suspicious of anyone who offers one too quickly. See you tomorrow.
