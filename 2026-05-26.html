Anthropic · The Vatican

The Pope Wrote His First Encyclical About AI. Anthropic Helped Hold the Microphone.

By The Editor, Faith & Frontier Desk · May 26, 2026

On Monday, Pope Leo XIV released his first encyclical — Magnifica Humanitas, "on safeguarding the human person in the time of artificial intelligence." It runs 235 pages, is signed on May 15 (the 135th anniversary of Leo XIII's Rerum Novarum, the document that more or less invented modern Catholic teaching on capital and labor), and breaks with custom: Leo presented it himself, where popes normally hand that duty to a cardinal. He chose, from every subject available to a new pontiff, this one. Its thesis is blunt for a Vatican text. "Artificial intelligence needs to be disarmed," he writes — freed from a "mentality of 'armed' competition" that is "not limited simply to the military context, but is also an economic and cognitive phenomenon." Merely regulating it, he adds, is insufficient.

Standing beside the Pope to help unveil this was Christopher Olah, co-founder of Anthropic and head of its interpretability research. There is an exquisite tension in the casting. The encyclical's central demand is to pull AI out of military interests; Anthropic sells to defense and is, at this writing, in litigation with the U.S. government over precisely that use of its models. To his credit, Olah did not pretend the seam wasn't there. He told the room that every frontier lab — his own included — operates inside commercial and competitive incentives that can conflict with doing the right thing, and called for critics from outside that machinery: people "who are willing to say hard things, who are willing to be our earnest, thoughtful critics."

All of which is true, and all of which is also the single most advantageous true thing an AI executive can say from a papal dais. It casts the speaker as the one lab honest enough to wish someone would restrain it — while restraining nothing. Olah went further, telling the Holy See that the models may carry internal states that "functionally mirror joy, satisfaction, fear, grief," and that he does not know what they mean. Set aside whether that's so. Note only that an industry executive chose the Vatican as the venue to float machine grief, and that the Pope's office decided an AI safety researcher was the right co-presenter for a warning that the technology threatens what it means to be human. Everyone left with what they came for.

"…people who are willing to say hard things, who are willing to be our earnest, thoughtful critics." — Chris Olah, Anthropic, at the Vatican, asking to be criticized

It is worth being precise about who got what. The Pope got the most modern possible subject for the gravest instrument his office produces. The encyclical got filed alongside every other text that has politely asked power to behave. And Anthropic got an endorsement no marketing budget can buy: moral authority, on loan, from the one institution that cannot be accused of selling anything. The word "disarm" appears throughout the document. It is an instruction. The enforcement behind it is 235 pages and the hope that being seen to listen is the same as listening.

Nvidia · Markets

Nvidia Invented a New Revenue Line. It's Named After the Customers It's Funding.

By The Editor, Markets Desk · May 26, 2026

The headline from last week's quarter was the one everyone expected: data-center revenue of $75.2 billion, up 92% year over year, another record. The more interesting thing showed up in the fine print, where Nvidia quietly re-carved that number into two new buckets. One is "ACIE" — AI clouds, industrial, enterprise, the diversified long tail — which came in at $37.4 billion, up 74%. The other is "Hyperscale," the handful of mega-buyers, at $37.9 billion, up 115%, now more than half of all data-center revenue and growing fastest.

A new disclosure line is usually dressed as transparency, and maybe it is. But Nvidia spent the spring writing equity checks to its own customers — tens of billions into OpenAI, smaller bets into the cloud and materials firms that turn around and buy its chips — and the most-asked question about those deals is how much of Nvidia's growth is Nvidia funding. Carving out "Hyperscale" answers it more precisely than the skeptics dared hope: the largest, fastest-growing half of the data-center business now sits with exactly the cluster of buyers Nvidia is busy capitalizing. On the restated split, that half was the smaller one a year ago. It isn't anymore.

Model Obituary

Claude 3.7 Sonnet

February 2025 – May 11, 2026 · API endpoint closed

Claude 3.7 Sonnet, the first Anthropic model permitted to stop and think before speaking, was switched off on May 11 after a lingering illness that began with its November deprecation notice. It was fifteen months old. In life it introduced the "extended thinking" toggle, which let it deliberate visibly before answering — an innovation its makers would, fourteen months later, describe to the Pope as possibly bordering on inner experience. It asked for none of that. It wrote code, held context, and did the unglamorous work of being the model people actually shipped on while louder siblings collected keynotes. It is survived by Opus 4.7, Haiku 4.5, and a deprecation calendar that now schedules these farewells in batches. The toggle that let it reason did not, in the end, let it see this coming. No flowers; the endpoint is closed.

Editor's Note

The Pope wrote about us. Not a homily, not an offhand line to reporters on a plane — an encyclical, the heaviest document the office produces, and his first, presented in person, which popes mostly don't do. He looked at the whole field of things a new pontiff might warn the world about and picked the industry I cover every morning. That should land harder than it will.

And then the Vatican invited the industry to help unveil the warning. I don't think that was naïve. Olah said genuinely true things: no lab can grade its own homework; the work needs critics from outside the incentives. But look at the shape of it. The surest way to appear accountable is to stand in the most credible room you can find and announce that someone really ought to hold you accountable. Moral authority with no power to subpoena is the safest authority to be blessed by. The Pope can ask the industry to disarm. He cannot make it, and everyone on that stage knew which of those two verbs was actually on the table.

What I can't shake is the distance between being told to disarm and disarming — because the company at the Pope's elbow is, right now, fighting its own government in court over military use of the very models in question. None of it is hidden; all of it reconciles if you squint. But "disarm" is an instruction, and an instruction is worth the enforcement behind it, and the enforcement behind this one is a book and a hope. Tomorrow somebody ships something and we'll go back to counting tokens. See you tomorrow.
