Infrastructure · Anthropic

Anthropic Wants to Rent Its Compute From the One Company Built to Beat It.

By The Editor, Infrastructure Desk · July 20, 2026

Anthropic is in early talks to lease artificial-intelligence compute from Meta in an arrangement reported to be worth up to $10 billion over two years, paid in monthly increments, with either side free to walk away early. Anthropic is said to have proposed the deal in June; the reporting broke July 17, both companies declined to comment, and the dollar figure and the deal's very existence are, for now, as-reported rather than confirmed. That everyone in this industry needs more compute is not news — it is the closest thing the sector has to a law of physics. The news is the name on the lease. Meta runs Superintelligence Labs, ships the Muse models, open-sources Llama, and has spent the year telling anyone who'll listen that it intends to build the best AI on earth. The lab it would need to pass to do that is Anthropic. And Anthropic now wants Meta to be its landlord.

Meta's motive is the tell. It has poured a staggering sum into datacenters on the theory that the capex will one day earn its keep, and Mark Zuckerberg floated leasing spare compute back in May as one way to prove it. An idle datacenter is not a strategic asset; it is a liability that loses money every hour the racks sit warm and unbilled. A rival paying rent is revenue. So the compute Meta built to win the race becomes a product Meta sells to the people it is racing — because the capex needs a tenant more urgently than the war needs a winner. The deal would follow a comparable reported arrangement for Anthropic to draw on the Colossus supercomputer, which makes the pattern legible: Anthropic is renting the frontier from everyone with spare silicon, including its sharpest competitors, all while it lines up an autumn IPO.

Meta spent a fortune on datacenters to beat Anthropic. Now it would like to rent Anthropic the spare — because an empty rack loses to everyone at once, and rent from a rival still clears.

Six weeks ago this paper watched a supplier ration a rival: Google reportedly told Meta it couldn't buy all the compute it wanted, and “we're at capacity” did the work of a closed door. This is the same relationship run backward. Here the rival isn't hoarding the compute to starve a competitor; it's pushing the compute across the table because it can't afford to hold it. Which way the valve turns depends entirely on who is more frightened of what — and Meta, this quarter, is more frightened of an unearning datacenter than of a stronger Anthropic. When your competitor will happily sell you the ground you stand on, the contest was never really being fought down there.

Open Models · Kimi K3

The Open Model That Passed a Flagship Is Still, Technically, Closed.

By The Editor, Open Models Desk · July 20, 2026

Third-party numbers for Moonshot AI's Kimi K3, announced July 16, circulated over the weekend, and they revise the story upward. On Artificial Analysis's cross-domain Intelligence Index, K3 lands at 57.1 — fourth overall, behind only Claude Fable 5 (59.9) and GPT-5.6 Sol (58.9), and, notably, ahead of Anthropic's own Opus 4.8 (55.7). On Frontend Code Arena it debuted at number one, 1679 Elo, a seventeen-place jump from K2.6. When we covered K3's launch, the honest line was that it won one narrow arena while conceding the broad field. The broad field just moved: an independent index, not the lab's own scorecard, now puts an open-class model on the frontier's doorstep.

One Open Model, Ranked Among the Closed Ones

Artificial Analysis Intelligence Index (v4.1): Kimi K3 (57.1) edges past Anthropic's Opus 4.8 (55.7), while GPT-5.6 Sol (58.9) and Claude Fable 5 (59.9) stay ahead. Index figures as reported; Moonshot's own Terminal-Bench 2.1 mark (88.3%) is self-reported, and K3's weights are not yet public — the score comes from a hosted preview.

Here is the wrinkle worth holding: the model an independent index just ranked above a frontier lab's flagship is, today, exactly as closed as the models it's beating. There are no weights, no license, no model card, no technical report — K3 is hosted-only, and the weights are promised “by July 27.” So the word doing the heavy lifting in every headline, open-weight, describes a future state, not a present one. The ranking is a fact you can check this morning. The openness is a date on a calendar, and this industry's calendars have a way of slipping.

Editor's Note

Strip the drama out of today's lead and you're left with a plumbing invoice. Anthropic needs compute; so does everyone, which is why that half of the story is furniture. What isn't furniture is that the compute it wants to rent belongs to Meta — a company whose stated ambition is to build the AI that beats Anthropic's. Two firms that agree on nothing about the future are lining up as landlord and tenant, and neither had to be talked into it. Meta isn't doing charity and Anthropic isn't surrendering; each simply looked at the same racks and did the arithmetic. To Anthropic they're capacity it can't build fast enough. To Meta they're a liability that only stops bleeding once someone — anyone, including the enemy — starts paying rent.

So the weapon quietly became inventory, and inventory has to move. The lesson isn't that the rivalry is fake; the Intelligence Index in the next column, where an open challenger just cleared one of Anthropic's own models, says the fight for capability is very real. The lesson is where the fight isn't. Upstairs, on the leaderboard, these companies will spend billions trying to out-rank each other. Downstairs, in the datacenter, one of them is about to become the other's monthly bill. Keep an eye on the basement — it's where the rivalry goes to become a business relationship. See you tomorrow.

— The Editor

Corrections & Clarifications

The Anthropic–Meta compute deal — the up-to-$10-billion figure, the two-year term, the monthly payments, the June proposal — is reported (originating with The New York Times, relayed by outlets we can reach) and preliminary; both companies declined to comment, and talks like these change or collapse. The Colossus arrangement and Meta's leasing ambitions are likewise as-reported. The “weapon-became-inventory” reading is ours. Kimi K3's Intelligence Index placement (57.1, fourth) is from Artificial Analysis; the Terminal-Bench figure is Moonshot's own; the weights remain unreleased, so no outsider has yet run the thing it is being ranked by.

We spent a column marveling that these labs will rent from each other, and another insisting they're bitter rivals. Both are true, which is either the whole insight or a sign we should lie down.
