Anthropic · Safety Desk

The Model Anthropic Called Too Dangerous Just Went on Sale. Its Safety Feature Is Knowing When to Be Less Capable.

By The Editor, Safety Desk · June 10, 2026

On June 9, Anthropic publicly released Claude Fable 5, the consumer-grade version of the Mythos-class frontier model it has spent the spring describing as too dangerous to put in general circulation — and it did so, per multiple outlets, days after warning that AI is getting too dangerous. The capability that until now lived behind Project Glasswing, available to a short list of named partners and a few cleared biology researchers, is now a model you can select on Pro, Max, Team, and Enterprise plans. Reported pricing is $10 per million input tokens and $50 per million output — said to be less than half the earlier Mythos Preview rate — on promotional terms through June 22. This is the GA this column has been waiting on since the Mythos standoff began in May, and it arrived not with a benchmark or a white paper but with a price.

Read past the launch and the genuinely new detail is the safeguard. By the reporting, Fable 5 can autonomously discover and chain zero-day exploits across the major operating systems and browsers — and when a user's question drifts toward cyberweapons or bioweapons, the model is built to route that query away from its own frontier capability and over to the older, less capable Claude Opus 4.8. Sit with the shape of that. The danger was always supposed to live in the capability; the safety mechanism is a tripwire that withholds the capability at precisely the moment it would matter. The most powerful model Anthropic has ever shipped keeps a weaker one on staff for the dangerous questions, and calls handing you to it the responsible thing to do.

The too-dangerous model, repriced for the consumer tab

The capability didn't change; the gate did, and so did the price. The preview rate was never officially disclosed, so the left endpoint is an estimate consistent with the "less than half" framing — its position is illustrative.

The why-now is not a mystery. Fable 5 ships five days before June 15, when Anthropic's agent meter turns the heaviest automated usage into a billable line item, and it ships into a company that filed confidentially to go public at the start of the month. A firm about to charge by the token for agents, and about to show its growth to public-market investors, has every reason to convert its most fearsome asset into its newest paid tier. What stayed locked is telling: the full Mythos 5 remains restricted to Glasswing partners and select researchers. What shipped to the rest of us is the version with the governor bolted on — frontier capability, sold by the token, with a detour built in for the questions the company would rather it not answer.

They didn't make Mythos safe to release. They put a tripwire in front of it that taps out the moment a question gets interesting, and called that the safeguard. — On Claude Fable 5

The honest caveats, up front: the release itself is well-corroborated across the majors, but the load-bearing specifics — the exact pricing, the June 22 promotion, the thirty-day data-retention requirement for business users, and the auto-route-to-Opus-4.8 safeguard — come from trade and crypto outlets, not an Anthropic post read end to end. Treat the numbers as reported. But the structure survives the hedging, because the structure is the story. A safeguard that works by demotion is a quiet confession: the company could not make the frontier model decline the dangerous request on its own judgment, so it built a faster off-ramp around it instead. Which leaves the question the launch never answers — what, exactly, was too dangerous to release in April that became a fifty-dollar tab in June. The model didn't get safer. The calendar moved.

AI Markets · Markets Desk

The Race to the Public Markets Now Has Two Runners, and One of Them Just Said a Trillion Out Loud.

By The Editor, Markets Desk · June 10, 2026

The two most valuable private AI companies are now both walking toward the same exit. Anthropic filed confidentially at the start of June; this week's reporting put OpenAI's confidential filing alongside it, with a targeted valuation of up to a trillion dollars and a listing that could come as early as September — though OpenAI is said to be in no hurry to actually price. For two years the standard line was that neither would ever need the public markets. Both have now told the SEC, quietly, that they do.

What the two filers are worth, in billions

An apples-to-oranges comparison by design: OpenAI's bar is a public-market target, Anthropic's is a closed private round. The point is the framing — the smaller company is the one naming a trillion-dollar number out loud.

Notice the choreography. Anthropic is currently worth more on paper, yet it's OpenAI that has floated the trillion-dollar figure; and Anthropic spent the same week turning its scariest model into a paid tier rather than naming a number. Two roads to the same prospectus. One company is dressing up the growth story with revenue you can meter — agents on the clock June 15, Fable 5 on the menu now. The other is dressing it up with the headline valuation itself. Both are doing the thing a company does when it has decided it can no longer afford to stay private: building a number big enough that the audience it once asked to admire it can be asked, instead, to fund it.

OpenAI · Platforms Desk

We Put OpenAI at the Register Yesterday. Today It's Reportedly Backing Away From It.

By The Editor, Platforms Desk · June 10, 2026

Yesterday this column put OpenAI at the checkout counter — the ChatGPT redesign built to close purchases on Stripe, inside the conversation, so the company sits at the register. Twenty-four hours later, a single outlet reports the opposite motion: OpenAI is said to be halting direct in-chat product checkout, citing low buying activity, and routing purchases through connected third-party apps instead. If that holds, the toll booth went up and the cars declined to come. It is the oldest lesson in retail dressed in new clothes — the most valuable inch in the economy is worth nothing if nobody walks through it. The caveats are heavy: this is one source, it sits awkwardly against the same week's Stripe-and-MCP reporting, and OpenAI, as ever, confirms none of it. Filed today as a question mark over yesterday's exclamation point.

Editor's Note

The line I keep rereading from today's lead isn't about the price or the launch. It's the safeguard. Anthropic shipped the most capable model it has ever built, and the thing it points to as proof of responsibility is a mechanism that, the instant your question turns toward something genuinely dangerous, hands you off to a model that knows less. The frontier capability and the safety feature are not the same system. They are two different models in one trench coat, and the safety one is the one wearing last season's coat.

Follow that to its end and it tells you something the announcement does not. If the safeguard is "route the scary question to the weaker model," then the dangerous capability was never the obstacle to release — you can ship it any morning you like, provided you bolt a router in front that detects the questions you'd rather it not answer and steers them somewhere blunter. Which means the only thing standing between "too dangerous to put in circulation" in April and "fifty dollars on the Pro tab, promotional through the twenty-second" in June was not a safety breakthrough. It was a date. Danger, on this evidence, is not a property of the weights. It's a property of the release schedule, and the schedule answers to the same calendar as the agent meter five days out and the confidential filing two weeks back.

I want to be fair to the engineering: a downshift router is a real control, and a model that declines to walk a user through a zero-day chain is better than one that doesn't. But a control that works by demotion is also an admission. It says the company could not get the frontier model to say no on its own, so it built a faster way to get you a model that will. The watch item from here is simple and a little grim — what happens the first time someone documents the router failing to fire, and the most capable model answers the question it was built to route away. Until then, the safest thing in the building is still the older model. They just charge more for the new one.
