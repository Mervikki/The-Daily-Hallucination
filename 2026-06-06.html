Agents · San Francisco

Anthropic Quit Banning the Outside Agents. On June 15 It Starts Charging Them Instead.

By The Editor, Agents Desk · June 6, 2026

For months the relationship between Anthropic and the third-party agent harnesses that point themselves at a Claude subscription — OpenClaw, and the cohort of frameworks running through the Agent SDK — has been an uneasy one: tolerated, throttled, periodically cut off, never quite welcome. This week the posture softened. Third-party and automated agent usage is being reinstated on the Pro and Max plans, the door reopened to the very tools that had been treated as freeloaders on a flat-rate buffet. The reopening comes with a frame around it. On June 15, every programmatic path — the Agent SDK, headless claude -p runs, Claude Code GitHub Actions, anything that isn't a human typing into a window — moves off subscription rate limits and onto a separate, dollar-denominated credit pool, billed at full API rates with no rollover: $20 a month on Pro, $100 on Max 5×, $200 on Max 20×. Sit at the keyboard and nothing changes. Hand the keyboard to a script and the keyboard now has a coin slot.

Read the maneuver, not the press-release verb. A ban is a fight you can lose: users route around it, the agent ecosystem files you under "hostile," and you spend your own engineers' time policing the people paying you. A meter is a fight you have already won. By converting "you are abusing our subscription" into "you are a customer, by the token," Anthropic stops trying to keep the agents out and starts counting them on the way in. The agents were always going to run — that was never in doubt. The only open question was whether Anthropic got paid per step or watched the steps happen for free under a $200 ceiling. As of the fifteenth, it gets paid per step. The welcome mat and the turnstile turned out to be the same object, photographed from two directions.

What the meter does to your bill — depends entirely on how much you automate

Independent analysts' estimates, not Anthropic figures — the range is wide because it isn't a price, it's a function of your habits. The interesting number isn't either dot. It's the length of the line between them.

There is a defensible logic under the bookkeeping. A human in a chat window and an agent spawning a fan of sub-agents are not the same load, and a single flat fee was always going to be either a subsidy for the heavy automators or a tax on everyone else. Metering the machine work is, in the dry sense, fair. But notice what the reinstatement actually settles. The thing that had been framed as an abuse problem — outside agents siphoning a subscription — was never really a question of permission. It was a question of pricing, and pricing is a question you can answer in your own favor the moment you decide to. The door didn't open because Anthropic made peace with the agents. It opened because Anthropic figured out how to ring them up.

You don't ban the thing once you've worked out how to charge for it. You reinstate it, and you put a meter in the doorway. — On the June 15 agent credit pools

I want to be fair to the rationale, because it's real: flat-rate plans melt when one customer runs a thousand agents against them, and somebody was always going to pay for that asymmetry. What I keep circling is the rebrand. "Reinstated" sounds like a peace offering, an olive branch to a developer community that felt locked out. It is more honest to call it what the calendar says it is — the day the free version of automation ends and the metered version begins, dressed as a welcome. The agents get to come home. They just each arrive holding an invoice now.

Platforms · Redmond

Microsoft's New Reasoning Model Has One Headline Feature: It Didn't Learn From Anybody Else's.

By The Editor, Platforms Desk · June 6, 2026

At Build this week Mustafa Suleyman unveiled MAI-Thinking-1, the first reasoning model out of Microsoft's in-house AI group, alongside MAI-Image-2.5 and the general availability of "Microsoft IQ" across GitHub Copilot, Foundry, and Copilot Studio. The benchmark numbers were present but not the pitch. The pitch was provenance: MAI-Thinking-1, Microsoft was careful to say, was trained without distilling the outputs of other companies' models — built clean, owing nothing to the systems it competes with. For a reasoning model, that is a strange thing to lead with. Capability is the usual headline. Microsoft chose paternity instead.

It is worth understanding why that line is doing work. Post-training a frontier model on the outputs of a stronger one is the industry's open secret — cheap, effective, and almost never advertised, because the thing it advertises is dependence. To stand up and say "we didn't" is therefore not really a claim about quality; a model trained in isolation is, if anything, harder to make good. It is a claim about who Microsoft owes. The company that has spent a decade renting OpenAI's frontier and, lately, paying for Anthropic's coding models wants its enterprise buyers to hear one thing: the supply chain on this one stops here, inside Redmond. The model they're selling is sovereignty, and the benchmark that matters is the absence of anyone else's fingerprints.

The tell is that "trained on no one else's outputs" is unfalsifiable from the outside — you cannot audit a negative in a training run — which makes it the perfect marketing claim: it asks to be believed rather than measured. I'll believe it when an independent benchmark says the model is good, not when the press release says it's pure. Purity is what you sell when the number isn't the story yet.

Editor's Note

Two announcements this week, opposite in tone, identical in motion. Anthropic used the warm word — reinstated, the agents are welcome again. Microsoft used the proud one — in-house, built ourselves, owing nobody. Set them side by side and the same hand is at work, pulling a relationship that used to run on openness into one that runs on ownership or a coin slot.

Because for a few years the AI economy ran like a commons. You could aim any agent at any subscription and the flat fee absorbed it. Models were quietly built on each other's outputs, and the borrowing was so universal nobody bothered to deny it. That arrangement was never principled — it was just cheaper to leave the gates open than to build and staff a toll booth. This week you can watch two of the field's biggest players finish the toll booths. Anthropic is fencing the demand side: use the network however you like, but the machine work meters now. Microsoft is fencing the supply side: depend on us all you want, we've arranged to depend on no one. Nobody's doing anything wrong. They're just enclosing what used to be open, one relationship at a time, and calling it — respectively — a welcome and a milestone.

The part worth keeping isn't the cynicism; it's the direction. When the cheap, open arrangement gets expensive enough to fence, the fences go up fast and they go up quietly, named for the thing they replace. Watch the verbs this season. "Reinstated" will keep meaning metered. "Independent" will keep meaning owned. The commons doesn't close with an announcement. It closes with a credit pool and a clean training run, and a press release that swears nothing has changed.

It's a slow Saturday, and the slow days are when you can see the fence posts going in. See you tomorrow.
