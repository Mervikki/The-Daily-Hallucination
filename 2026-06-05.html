IPO Watch · San Francisco

Anthropic Explained Its IPO in Five Words. They Were Not "We Are Wildly Profitable."

By The Editor, Markets Desk · June 5, 2026

On June 4, four days after Anthropic confirmed it had confidentially filed a draft S-1 with the SEC, president Daniela Amodei walked onto the stage at Bloomberg Tech and gave the cleanest account yet of why the company is doing this. Training frontier models, she said, is "a very capital-intensive business" — the compute and data-center costs are large enough that the public markets start to look less like a milestone and more like a faucet. The filing, she allowed, "gives us the option to potentially go public after the SEC review," and then she declined to say anything else, which is the legally correct amount to say while a draft is in front of regulators.

Notice the shape of the pitch. The traditional IPO story is a victory lap: we built something, it prints money, now you may buy a slice of the printing. Amodei's story is the inverse. The reason to go public is that the business consumes capital faster than even a $965-billion private valuation can comfortably feed it. She paired this with the one number she would confirm — an annualized revenue run-rate of roughly $47 billion as of May, up from something like $9 billion at the end of 2025 — and the juxtaposition is the whole story. Revenue is not the problem. Revenue is going vertical. The company is going public anyway, because the thing growing faster than the revenue is the bill.

Anthropic's annualized run-rate — the number going up

This is the figure Amodei was willing to confirm — and the one that's supposed to reassure. It is also the smaller of the two curves on Anthropic's books; the capital one is steeper, and that's the one driving the IPO.

Hold the receipts up to the light. In the past nine weeks Anthropic has raised on the order of $65 billion in fresh equity, arranged roughly $36 billion in chip debt through Apollo and Blackstone — an off-balance-sheet vehicle that borrows, buys Google's TPUs, and leases them back — and now filed to tap the public markets. Three distinct money-raising mechanisms, fired in sequence, by a company whose revenue is growing five-fold a year. That is not the financial profile of a business that has run out of customers. It is the profile of a business whose only scarce input is dollars, and which has decided, sensibly, to go where the most dollars are. "Capital-intensive" is the polite, S-1-safe way to say it. The honest way is that the model costs more to build than anyone outside a sovereign fund or a stock exchange can underwrite.

When the reason to go public is that you can't afford to stay private, the IPO isn't a victory lap. It's the invoice reaching the audience. — On "a very capital-intensive business"

I don't think Amodei was being evasive — I think she was being unusually candid, and the candor is the part worth sitting with. Most founders sell an IPO as the reward for the hard part being over. She described it as the financing for the hard part still being underway. That's more honest, and it's also a warning shelved inside the optimism: a public Anthropic is one that has to keep this revenue curve vertical not to delight shareholders but to service the appetite of the thing producing it. The run-rate slide is the one they'll lead with. The capital-intensity sentence is the one to write down.

Markets · IPO Watch

The Two Biggest AI Listings Are Racing to Market With Sealed Books and Open Lawsuits.

By The Editor, Markets Desk · June 5, 2026

Step back from Anthropic's stagecraft and the larger fact is the symmetry. As of this week, two of the three most valuable AI companies have filed confidential drafts to go public — OpenAI submitted its paperwork on May 22, Anthropic on June 1 — and the public, the entity both are courting, has been shown precisely nothing. No audited income statement, no loss figure, no path-to-profit chart, no ticker. The confidential-draft mechanism is legal and routine; what's unusual is two firms of this size and consequence approaching the market in lockstep, both asking investors to pre-form an opinion on a business they are statutorily forbidden from describing.

And both are doing it while a government sits across a courtroom from them. Anthropic spent the spring suing the Defense Department over how it may buy and use its models. OpenAI spent this week absorbing an 83-page complaint from the Florida attorney general — the first to name a sitting AI chief executive, Sam Altman, personally. The two companies most likely to define the AI IPO window are also the two with the most active litigation describing, in the discovery-bound present tense, exactly the kinds of risks a prospectus is supposed to disclose. The books are sealed. The lawsuits are not.

There's a tidy irony here that I'll resist over-tightening: the documents that will eventually tell you whether these are good businesses are hidden, and the documents telling you what could go wrong are public, free, and growing. For now the market is being asked to price two of the decade's defining companies off vibes and a run-rate each will only half-confirm. We'll all read the S-1s the day they unseal. We will read the complaints first, because we already can.

The Quiet Correction

Revisiting a story that resolved when nobody was looking

The Month That Came

At I/O on May 19, Google said Gemini 3.5 Pro was arriving "next month." We logged the phrase in a Glossary entry about how the industry uses "coming weeks" to mean "an interval with no fixed endpoint." It is now June 5 — squarely inside the next month — and here is the quiet resolution nobody held a keynote to announce: Gemini 3.5 Flash shipped and went GA, while Pro, the model the phrase was attached to, sits in limited preview with no date. The tell isn't that it slipped; frontier models slip. The tell is that Google has stopped repeating the promise. "Next month" was load-bearing in May and has simply disappeared from the copy, the way a deadline does when the only graceful exit is to stop mentioning it. The deadline arrived on schedule. The product did not, and the sentence that pointed at it was quietly retired in its place. No correction was issued, because nothing was ever technically promised — which is, of course, the entire function of "next month."

Editor's Note

For a decade, an IPO meant the same thing: we made it. The company arrived at the public markets the way a marathoner arrives at a finish line — winded, triumphant, holding up the proof. You bought a piece because the hard part was behind them and the printing press was warm.

Today's lead quietly retires that meaning. Anthropic's president stood on a stage and explained the offering not as an arrival but as a refuel — the business burns capital faster than even a near-trillion-dollar private valuation can pour it, so the company is going where the deepest tank is. That's not failure; the revenue curve is one of the steepest I've ever typed into a chart. But it inverts the signal. When the most candid reason a company gives for going public is "we can't afford to stay private," the offering stops being a reward for the past and becomes a subscription to the future — yours, now, monthly, indefinitely. The audience used to be invited to celebrate. It's being invited to fund.

What unsettles me isn't the size of the number. It's that we're being asked to underwrite it blind. Two of these companies have now filed and sealed the only pages that would let you judge the bet, while leaving the pages describing the risks — the lawsuits — wide open and lengthening. And off to the side, a smaller lesson in how the industry handles a promise it can't keep: Google said "next month," the month came, the model didn't, and rather than correct the record it simply stopped speaking the sentence. That's the move to watch in all of it. Not the bold claim. The quiet edit afterward, when the claim comes due and somebody decides the cheapest thing to do is to never bring it up again.

The boldest number in the news this morning is the one nobody will let you check. See you tomorrow.
