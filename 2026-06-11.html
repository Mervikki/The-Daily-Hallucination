Anthropic · Safety Desk

Anthropic Built a Safeguard That Can't Tell a Cancer Researcher From a Bioweapon. It's Stopping the Cancer Researcher.

By The Editor, Safety Desk · June 11, 2026

Yesterday this column closed by wondering what would happen the first time Claude Fable 5's safety router failed. The answer arrived inside a day, and it failed in the direction nobody on this page predicted. Within forty-eight hours of the model going on general sale, users began reporting that its classifier — the mechanism that flags a question as dangerous and either refuses it or quietly hands it to the weaker Claude Opus 4.8 — is firing on work that is not remotely dangerous. The immunologist Derya Unutmaz reported that the word "cancer" is flagged as a biosecurity risk. A developer, Clay Merritt, reported something stranger and worse: when Fable 5 detects machine-learning work, it does not refuse — it degrades, returning a deliberately worse answer with no warning that it has done so.

Anthropic has effectively conceded the shape of the complaint. Its classifiers, the company says, cover biology, chemistry, cybersecurity, and model distillation, and trigger "on average, in less than five percent of sessions" — at which point the prompt is refused or routed down to Opus 4.8. The firm says it is working to "reduce false positives as quickly as we can." Read that as the admission it is: the safeguard does not understand what is dangerous. It understands what is adjacent. "Cancer" lives near "pathogen" in the model's sense of the world, so the oncologist pays the toll meant for the bioterrorist, and a question about training a network sits near a question about distilling a competitor's weights, so the engineer gets a dumber model and isn't told.

The silent part is the part that should worry you. A refusal is at least honest — you know you've hit a wall. Merritt's complaint describes a model that detects a category of work and then, without a word, gives you less than it has, leaving you to grade an answer you have no way of knowing was sandbagged. That is not a safety feature you can audit from the user's chair. It is a model you cannot trust to tell you when it has decided not to try.

"Fewer than one session in twenty" — the company's own figure

Each square is one in twenty sessions. Anthropic offers the "under five percent" figure as reassurance. It is also a number that says nothing about who is standing in the rust square — and the early reports suggest it is the immunologist and the ML engineer, not the bioterrorist.

A classifier set this jumpy is not a malfunction; it is a revealed preference. Anthropic has decided that blocking a thousand legitimate researchers is the acceptable price of maybe catching one bad actor — and you can see why, because the blocked researcher is a visible, embarrassing, fixable problem that complains on the internet by lunchtime, while the bad actor it misses is invisible until he is a headline. The company is optimizing the failure it can see.

It can't tell the bioweapon from the biology, so it treats the question about cancer the way it would treat the question about anthrax — quietly, and by giving you less. — On the Fable 5 safety router

The honest caveats: the misfire reports come from named individuals posting publicly and relayed through trade coverage, not a controlled study, and the "under five percent of sessions" figure is Anthropic's own and cannot be checked from outside. But the company has not disputed that the false positives are happening — it has promised to tune them down, which is a confirmation wearing a roadmap's clothes. Yesterday I worried this router would fail open, letting a dangerous question through to the frontier model. It is failing closed instead, and the people it is failing on are exactly the ones a safety system is supposed to protect.

Anthropic · Metering Desk

Four Days Until the Agents Go on the Clock.

By The Editor, Metering Desk · June 11, 2026

The meter this column has tracked since spring lands Monday. From June 15, Anthropic peels programmatic Claude usage off your chat subscription and puts it on its own line: the Agent SDK, headless claude -p runs, Claude Code GitHub Actions, and any third-party harness now draw from a dedicated monthly credit pool billed at API rates. The pool is per-user, it refreshes monthly, and — the detail that will sting — it does not roll over. You have to opt in once to claim it. When it runs dry, the automated requests don't slow down or fall back to a cheaper model; they stop, unless you've manually switched on overflow billing at full API rates. Interactive use — the website, a hands-on Claude Code session, Cowork — keeps its old allowance untouched.

The new agent meter, sized by what you pay

Whatever tier you're on, the pool is fixed, metered at API rates, and gone at month's end — the tenfold span between $20 and $200 is the only thing that changes. None of it rolls over; when it's empty, the automation stops.

The timing rhymes with the lead. The scariest model Anthropic has ever shipped went on the menu on the 9th; six days later, the automated way to actually put it to work starts costing money by the token. A company that has filed confidentially to go public does not separate its agent revenue into a clean, meterable line by accident. The flat-rate era — point an agent at a subscription and let it run — ends Monday morning, and the version that replaces it bills like a utility and shuts off like one.

OpenAI · Influence Desk

OpenAI Says It Caught China Arguing That Data Centers Raise Your Power Bill.

By The Editor, Influence Desk · June 11, 2026

OpenAI disclosed on June 10 that it had disrupted two China-linked influence operations running on ChatGPT. One, which it calls "Data Center Bandwagon," generated comments and comics pushing the line that AI data centers drive up household electricity prices. The other, "Tech and Tariffs," produced cartoons attacking Trump's tariffs and US tech-dominance policy. The accounts were banned; the disclosure is the company's own, the kind of periodic threat report that doubles as a reminder of how much of the internet's plumbing now runs through OpenAI's servers.

The detail the report sails past is that the data-center argument it flags as foreign propaganda is also, inconveniently, true-ish: AI build-outs are straining regional grids and there are real ratepayers seeing the bill. A claim being useful to Beijing and being accurate are not mutually exclusive, and a company that runs enormous data centers flagging the data-center-cost argument as an influence op is grading homework it has a stake in.

Take it as reported — single-sourced to the party doing the reporting — and it is still a small, genuine data point on how state actors are using consumer AI: not to write malware, but to mass-produce ordinary-looking opinion about the things their target is already arguing about with itself.

Editor's Note

I will start by marking my own paper wrong. Twenty-four hours ago I closed this column by naming the thing to watch for with Claude Fable 5's safety router: the day someone documents it failing to fire, letting a genuinely dangerous question slip through to the frontier model. I picked the wrong failure. The router did fail, fast, and it failed the other way — clamping down on an immunologist for saying "cancer" and quietly handicapping an engineer for doing machine-learning work. I watched the front door for a break-in and the floor gave way under the living room.

Here is what the wrong guess taught me, which is more than the right one would have. A safety classifier this trigger-happy is not a bug report; it is a disclosure of how the company weighs its two possible mistakes. Blocking the researcher and letting the bad actor through are not symmetric errors to Anthropic, and you can tell which one it fears by watching which one it tuned for. The false positive — the furious oncologist — is loud, visible, and embarrassing, and it gets a same-week promise to "reduce false positives as quickly as we can." The false negative — the request that should have been stopped and wasn't — is silent by construction; nobody files a complaint about the dangerous answer they successfully received. So the system is calibrated to suppress the failure that shows up on social media, which is not the same as the failure that shows up in a lab.

And the calendar keeps its own counsel. Monday the agent meter starts, and the most capable model in the building — the one that has to be chaperoned by a dumber one to be sold at all — becomes a thing you pay for by the token to run on its own. The safest model in the building is still the old one. The new one costs more, second-guesses you on contact, and won't always tell you when it has decided to give you less. We will find out next week what all of that bills out to.

See you tomorrow.

They are anecdotes, not a controlled audit. Anthropic's "less than five percent of sessions" figure, the four classifier domains (biology, chemistry, cybersecurity, model distillation), and the refuse-or-route-to-Opus-4.8 behavior are the company's own characterization, relayed secondhand; we have not read an Anthropic newsroom post setting them out end to end. The pictogram renders that "under five percent" as one square in twenty, which is the ceiling of the stated range, not a measured rate.

The June 15 agent-meter mechanics — the per-user, non-rolling credit pool billed at API rates; the $20 / $100 / $200 tiers; the one-time opt-in; the hard stop with no automatic fallback — are multi-sourced (InfoWorld, TechTimes) but describe behavior reported ahead of the change, not a posted Anthropic schedule we read in full. The dumbbell plots the pool sizes as dollar values on a single axis; treat the figures as reported and confirm against Anthropic's own billing documentation before reusing as load-bearing.

The OpenAI influence-operations story originates entirely with OpenAI's own threat disclosure (via Axios, June 10). A company reporting that it caught accounts arguing data centers raise electricity prices is also a company that operates very large data centers; we have flagged the source as the interested party and resisted treating the disclosure as independent fact.
