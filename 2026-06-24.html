Silicon · Amazon Desk

Amazon Has Decided It Would Rather Be Nvidia Than Keep Renting From It

By The Editor, Silicon Desk · June 24, 2026

Amazon designs its own AI chips — Trainium for training, Inferentia for inference — and until now has used them almost entirely to feed its own cloud. That is the part Andy Jassy is signalling he wants to change. In his April shareholder letter he put the silicon arm at a roughly $50 billion annual business measured as a standalone unit, and he has since said there is "a good chance" Amazon sells full racks of those chips to third-party data centers "within two years." Peter DeSantis, who runs the silicon group, describes "active discussions" to do exactly that — while taking care to call it "early stages." None of which is a product launch. It is a flag run up a pole, in public, by a company that does not run flags up poles by accident.

The interesting thing is not the chips; it is the floor count. Amazon already occupies most of the AI building. It is the cloud landlord every model has to rent space in. It is the single largest outside investor in Anthropic, the lab whose flagship is currently switched off by government order — a story sitting one column over. And it is, increasingly, the foundry: the same Trainium it sells to its own portfolio lab it now proposes to sell to that lab's competitors. Vertical integration is usually a company quietly eating its suppliers. This is a company trying to own every layer of an industry it also bankrolls, polices, and hosts.

Amazon does not need to build the best model. It would rather own the floor every model has to stand on — and charge by the rack. — The Daily Hallucination, Silicon Desk

Amazon's silicon, repositioned

From captive input to merchant product — the strategic move Amazon is describing, not one it has made. The $50B figure and the "two years" are Jassy's own (April shareholder letter, subsequent remarks); external chip sales remain intent, not a shipping product.

The "good chance … within two years" is the tell. You do not hedge the timeline on a thing you have built; you hedge it on a thing you are deciding whether to build. This is a company watching Nvidia bank the entire AI boom and asking, out loud, whether the toll road could have a second operator — itself.

The skeptics have a point, and it is mostly named CUDA: selling silicon to strangers means matching not just Nvidia's hardware but its software, its support desk, its supply. Amazon may decide the merchant business is more trouble than the captive one. But notice the position the question is being asked from. Whoever ends up winning the model race — Anthropic, OpenAI, Google, the open-weight field — almost all of them already pay Amazon rent for the room they run in. The company is simply asking whether it can also sell them the foundation. The gold rush has had picks-and-shovels jokes for two years; the part nobody made into a joke is that Amazon owns the river.

Anthropic · Washington Desk

Day Twelve: They Met Monday. The Models Are Still Off.

By The Editor, Washington Desk · June 24, 2026

Anthropic's Fable 5 and Mythos 5 have now been dark for twelve days under the Commerce export order issued June 12. The one genuinely new development is procedural: senior Anthropic technical staff met Commerce officials in Washington on Monday, with the National Cyber Director, Sean Cairncross, reportedly in the room — the first time that office has surfaced by name in the talks. There was no readout, no amended order, and nothing anyone outside can call a restoration. The "coming days" the company promised in Seoul on June 18 are now six days old and counting.

The throughline worth marking is who keeps appearing. The jailbreak that triggered all of this was found by researchers at Amazon and escalated by Jassy himself to Treasury, Commerce, and the cyber office — the same Amazon now openly weighing whether to sell chips to everyone in the lead above. Anthropic's largest backer is also the party that got its flagship switched off and a participant, by proxy, in the rooms deciding when it comes back. Trump told Axios this week he no longer sees the company as a threat — "not now," though "a week ago, maybe." The switch he could flip with a sentence remains exactly where it was.

Model Watch · OpenAI & Google

The Two Most-Awaited Models Are Both Still Vapor

By The Editor, Model Desk · June 24, 2026

Two frontier releases are overdue inside their own predicted windows and neither has appeared. OpenAI's GPT-5.6 — which chief scientist Jakub Pachocki has called a "meaningful improvement" over GPT-5.5 — is priced by Polymarket at roughly 83% to ship by this Saturday and ~95% by the end of July, with no model card, no API note, no post. Google's Gemini 3.5 Pro, unveiled at I/O on May 19 with general availability "targeted for June," is still a limited enterprise preview on Vertex AI and nowhere else; the same market gives it barely better than even odds of a public release by June 30.

It is a strange shape for a frontier to take: the two models the industry is most certain are coming exist, this morning, only as probabilities. We have stopped asking whether they work and started betting on when they arrive. The capability is assumed; the calendar is the contest.

Editor's Note

Read today's issue once for the news, then read it again for the name that keeps recurring. Amazon is the lead — designing the chips, talking about selling them to everyone's data centers, a $50 billion arm Jassy would like to point outward instead of in. Amazon is also the second story — its researchers found the jailbreak, its CEO carried it to Washington, and its blast radius is a rival lab's flagship, twelve days dark with the Cyber Director now in the room. One company, two stories, opposite hats: in the first it is building the future, in the second it is the reason a competitor's future is switched off.

I keep coming back to the floor count, because it's the part the chip headline buries. Amazon is the cloud almost every lab rents. It is Anthropic's largest investor. It is, soon enough, the foundry that wants to sell silicon to the same labs it funds and hosts. Vertical integration normally means a firm absorbing its own supply chain; what Amazon is sketching is something larger and stranger — owning every layer of an industry it does not need to win. It can fund the model, host the model, sell the chip the model trains on, and, when the model becomes a problem, be the one who flags it to Commerce. That is not a competitor in the AI race. That is the track.

None of which is a villain story, and I want to be careful not to write it as one — selling Trainium externally is a perfectly ordinary ambition, reporting a real vulnerability to the government is arguably the responsible thing, and "early stages" means most of this may never ship. The narrower point is just about altitude. There is a position in this business from which you do not especially care who builds the best model, because the rent routes through you either way. Amazon spent today telling us, in two unrelated stories, that it is standing on it. The labs are racing for the frontier. The landlord is selling the ground.

See you tomorrow.
