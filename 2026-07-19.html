Enterprise · Microsoft

Microsoft Built a Tool That Shops Every Frontier Model — and Priced Anthropic's as the One to Skip.

By The Editor, Enterprise Desk · July 19, 2026

Microsoft is reportedly preparing to launch, before the end of this month, a security platform called Project Perception — and the interesting part isn't what it scans, it's how it decides who does the scanning. At its core is a model router: an orchestration layer that hands each task to whichever model is cheapest for the job. Small, cheap models handle triage, log-parsing and inventory; the expensive ones are called in only for the hard steps. The router draws from Microsoft's, OpenAI's and Anthropic's models alike, and the explicit selling point, per the reporting, is undercutting Anthropic's Mythos — whose API is described as running roughly 100% above Opus and 82% above GPT. One outlet, launch not officially confirmed; read it as reported, not gospel.

Sit with which model that is. Mythos is the system that darkened an entire summer — the cyber-and-security frontier model a government decided in June was too dangerous to let out of the country, tested under a program with a name out of a spy novel, at the center of the whole export blackout. Six weeks later it surfaces in the pricing footnote of a Windows tool, as the line item you route around. Project Perception is tied to Microsoft's MDASH scanner, now in private preview, which has reportedly already surfaced 16 zero-days in Windows; this month's Patch Tuesday shipped a record 570 fixes, 61 of them critical. From too-dangerous-to-export to too-expensive-to-default, on the same model, before the export dust had settled.

In June, a government decided Mythos was too dangerous to let out of the country. This month, a Windows patch tool decided it was too expensive to bother with. Only one of those is a compliment.

The router is the whole trick, and it's a quiet one. A model's maker used to decide whether the thing was essential; a router decides that now, task by task, on price. Microsoft can keep Anthropic's model on the menu and still build a product whose entire pitch is that you shouldn't pay for it — the frontier model reduced to one option among three, which is the precise opposite of irreplaceable. Being gated by a government at least assumes you matter. Being sorted to the bottom of a dropdown assumes you're fungible. The second is the one that should worry a lab.

The Glossary
Model Router (n.)
The Official Definition

An orchestration layer that dispatches each request to the best-fit model for the task, sparing you from overpaying a frontier system to do work a cheaper model handles perfectly well.

The Real Definition

The middleman that turns every model you were told was irreplaceable into an interchangeable line item — and quietly moves the decision of what counts as “best-fit” from the company that built the model to the company that owns the router. In practice “best” resolves to “the cheapest one that clears the bar,” and the bar belongs to the middleman. The labs spent years competing to be the smartest name in the dropdown. The router is a bet that soon nobody will read the names.

Open Models · The Split

The Cheap Half of the Market Is Getting Loud.

By The Editor, Open Models Desk · July 19, 2026

Axios argued this weekend that the AI market is splitting cleanly in two: US closed frontier labs on one side, a Chinese open-weight insurgency on the other — Tencent, Xiaomi, DeepSeek, MiniMax, Z.ai's GLM, Alibaba's Qwen, Moonshot's Kimi. The driver named throughout is not capability but price; one enterprise-software chief executive tells the outlet flagship closed models are simply “too expensive,” pushing customers toward weights they can download and run themselves. The supporting numbers are the story: Vercel reports DeepSeek's share of tokens on its platform vaulted from under 1% to 17% in May, and Qwen has now passed a billion cumulative Hugging Face downloads, overtaking Meta's Llama as the most-downloaded open model on earth.

DeepSeek: Share of Tokens vs. Share of Revenue

DeepSeek's share of tokens run through Vercel jumped roughly seventeenfold; its share of revenue barely moved off 1%. Open weights are cheap enough that a surge in use scarcely registers as money. Figures as reported by Axios and Vercel; endpoints approximate.

The gap between those two lines is the whole argument. Usage exploded; revenue didn't follow, because the models are cheap enough that seventeen points of tokens is worth about one point of dollars. That is exactly why one investor quoted in the piece can float open models eventually handling ~95% of enterprise queries, leaving the frontier labs the top ~5%. Take the number with salt — projections about 2028 are the cheapest thing this industry makes. But point it at today's lead and the two stories rhyme without meaning to: above the frontier model sits a router shopping it against rivals, below it sit weights so cheap the usage doesn't even convert to a bill. Squeezed from both sides, on the one axis nobody's arguing about.

Editor's Note

In June, a government decided the Mythos model was too dangerous to let out of the country. This month, a Windows security tool decided it was too expensive to bother with. Same model, six weeks apart, two verdicts — and it's worth noticing which one flatters. An export control is a backhanded compliment: nobody builds a wall around a thing they think is ordinary. A router that skips you is not. To a price-sorter your model isn't dangerous and isn't safe; it's just the pricey row, and there is nothing to appeal.

Read the day's other story and the squeeze grows a second jaw. Above the frontier model, a middleman shops it against its rivals; below it, open weights so cheap a Chinese model can eat seventeen points of a platform's tokens and barely show up as revenue. For two years the frontier was judged on capability, the one contest it wins. This week it got judged on price twice — once by a competitor's tool, once by the market — and price is the verdict with no hearing. The export order will be argued over for months. The router just picks the other model and moves on. See you tomorrow.

— The Editor
