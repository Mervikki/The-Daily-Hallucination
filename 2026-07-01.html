Google Told Meta the Data Center Was Full. It Owns the Data Center — and Gemini.

By The Editor, Infrastructure Desk · July 1, 2026

Sometime around March, Google told Meta it could not sell it all the Gemini model capacity Meta wanted to buy. That is the small, strange fact at the center of a story that surfaced this past week, in the June 28–29 window, through Forbes, Business Standard, BusinessToday and a train of aggregators behind them. The shortfall reportedly disrupted and delayed some of Meta's internal AI projects; Meta, in turn, is said to have told its own staff to spend AI tokens more sparingly. Around it sit the numbers that explain why: Google Cloud's order backlog is reported to have nearly doubled in a single quarter, to roughly $460 billion; Google more than doubled quarterly capital spending, to about $35.7 billion; and in June it agreed to pay on the order of $920 million a month for some 110,000 processors housed in data centers tied to SpaceX and xAI. Even the company that prints the chips cannot print them fast enough.

Two things are true about Google that do not sit comfortably in the same sentence. It is one of the few places on earth Meta can buy the compute and the frontier models it needs. It is also, through Gemini and everything built on it, one of the companies Meta is racing. When your supplier is also your rival, "we're at capacity" stops being a logistics update and becomes a strategic choice you are not allowed to audit. Maybe Google genuinely ran out of room; the backlog figures suggest the crunch is real and industry-wide, not a courtesy extended to one customer. Or maybe it decided the last slab of scarce compute was worth more kept in-house than sold to Menlo Park. From the outside the two are indistinguishable, and only one party in the room knows which it was.

This is the thing a compute shortage does that no benchmark ever could: it shows you who is actually holding the leash. Meta — a company you could not buy with a mid-sized nation's treasury — asked to rent some room and was told no, and its answer was not to shrug but to lean harder into building its own silicon and its own capacity, because the lesson of being rationed by a competitor is that renting from one was always a bet you would lose the afternoon it got tight. We noted last week that Amazon wants to own every floor of the AI building and charge by the rack. Google just showed the darker half of that position: the landlord who is also in the tenant's line of work can quietly decide the tenant doesn't get the corner office, and call it a supply problem. The scarcest resource in this business right now is not talent, or data, or even the models. It is a powered, cooled wall socket that somebody else owns.

Fable 5, Day Nineteen: Still Dark, Still "Soon."

By The Editor, Washington Desk · July 1, 2026

The count, for the record. Claude Fable 5 — the consumer model switched off June 12 under the export order — is still off. Day nineteen. Off for consumers, off for the API, off for Claude Code, off for everyone outside the United States. Last week's reporting that public access would return "as soon as the week of June 30" has, as of this morning, produced no switch. Mythos 5, the higher-end model, remains the only one back, and only for the roughly hundred organizations Commerce cleared on June 26. The two sign-offs said to stand between Fable and its power button — the Pentagon and the NSA — are, by every account, still unsigned.

One aggregator did briefly run a headline announcing Fable 5 had been restored. It hadn't; only Mythos was. We mention the error only because a false report of a switched-on model is, in its way, the most fitting artifact this saga has produced — and because the actual route back on offer, a July 8 identity check that photographs your face before deciding whether you're allowed to ask, is still the only mechanism anyone can point to. We will tell you when a switch is flipped, and not before. So far we have datelines and a promise nineteen days old.

The Glossary · July 1, 2026
Capacity (n.)
Official definition

The total amount of computing power a provider has available to sell its customers at a given moment — a fact about racks, power, and cooling.

Real definition

The one number a cloud vendor never has to prove. It is spoken most often to the customers it also competes with, and it is real often enough to be unfalsifiable every time: "we're at capacity" can mean the building is full, or it can mean your roadmap matters less to us than ours, and you will never be shown which. The word does double duty — an engineering measurement and a negotiating position, wearing the same lab coat — which is why the firms that both host the industry and race in it have grown so fond of it. You cannot argue with a shortage. That is the entire appeal.

Editor's Note

Every phase of this industry has a scarcest thing, and you can date the phases by what it was. For a while it was talent — you won by hiring the people who had actually read the papers. Then it was data — you won by having scraped more of the internet than the other side. Then, briefly, it was the models themselves, and everyone printed benchmark tables to prove theirs was the scarcest of all. This week the scarce thing showed its face again, and it turned out to be the least glamorous input on the list: room. Powered, cooled, chip-filled room that somebody has already finished building. Meta — a company that could buy a mid-sized country outright — asked to rent some, and was told no.

What makes the moment worth marking isn't the shortage. Shortages pass, and Google is spending thirty-odd billion dollars a quarter to make this one pass faster. It's who got to say no. The company that told Meta it was full is the company shipping Gemini, which means Meta's landlord and Meta's rival are one corporation, and it alone knows whether "we're at capacity" this quarter was a fact about its data centers or a fact about its ambitions. I'm not accusing anyone of lying; the backlog is real and the crunch is everywhere. I'm saying that when the same firm supplies your compute and competes for your market, you lose the ability to tell a supply constraint from a competitive one — and losing that ability is itself the price of the arrangement.

So here is what I'd write down. For two years the labs have measured each other on capability, as though the race would be settled by whoever's model reasons best. The race is quietly being settled one floor down, by whoever owns the wall sockets — and several of the people who own the wall sockets are themselves in the race. You can build the finest model on earth and still find your ceiling set by a competitor who runs the building and gets to call that ceiling "capacity" with a straight face. Capability, you can win. A power bill you don't control, you can only pay.

See you tomorrow.
